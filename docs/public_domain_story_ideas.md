# Public Domain Story Ideas for Latin Picture Books

Future candidates for adaptation. All would need simplification to our target level (3-7 word Latin sentences, toddler audience). Even "simple" English originals like Aesop or Potter use vocabulary and sentence structures well beyond our target — plan on heavy simplification for everything here, not just the complex ones.

## Tier 1: Best Fit

Stories with repetitive/cumulative structure, strong visual narratives, and minimal plot complexity.

### The Enormous Turnip (Russian folk tale, Afanasyev 1863)
- **Why**: The ultimate cumulative tale. Grandpa can't pull out a giant turnip, calls grandma, who calls granddaughter, who calls the dog, cat, mouse. Every page repeats the same pattern + one new character. Latin refrain practically writes itself: *"Trahunt. Non possunt!"*
- **Art**: Generate new (public domain folk tale, no canonical illustrations)
- **Pages**: 15-20
- **Difficulty to adapt**: Low — structure is already in our format
- **Status**: Prototyped through Latin review (see `projects/the_enormous_turnip/`). Latin is clean but outline was too terse — setup and resolution pages need more text density. Good candidate for younger children. Would need outline revision before production.

### The Story of a Fierce Bad Rabbit + The Story of Miss Moppet (Beatrix Potter, 1906)
- **Why**: Potter's *simplest* books, explicitly designed for babies. Bad rabbit steals a carrot, gets his tail shot off. Kitten fails to catch a mouse. Could pair as a duo book.
- **Art**: Potter's original watercolors are public domain — available on [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/author/292). Could reuse directly.
- **Pages**: 12-14 each
- **Difficulty to adapt**: Low — already very short and visual

### Henny Penny / Chicken Little (folk tale, versions from 1823)
- **Why**: Cumulative chase. Acorn falls, chicken thinks sky is falling, recruits a parade of animals. Great Latin refrain: *"Caelum cadit!"*
- **Art**: Generate new (various pre-1929 illustrated editions exist but none canonical)
- **Pages**: 18-22
- **Difficulty to adapt**: Low-medium — ending needs thought for toddler audience (fox eats everyone in traditional version)

### The Three Little Pigs (L. Leslie Brooke illus., 1905)
- **Why**: Triple repetition. *"Flābō et sufflābō!"* as the refrain. Each pig builds a house, wolf arrives, blows/doesn't blow it down.
- **Art**: Brooke's color illustrations are public domain on [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/15661/15661-h/15661-h.htm). Could reuse directly.
- **Pages**: 20-25
- **Difficulty to adapt**: Low-medium — traditional ending (wolf in boiling pot) may need softening

### Millions of Cats (Wanda Gag, 1928)
- **Why**: Entered public domain 2024. Iconic refrain: *"Centum felēs, mille felēs, innumerābilēs felēs!"* Old man goes looking for one cat, brings back millions.
- **Art**: Gag's original black-and-white lithographs now public domain on [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/74181) and Internet Archive. Could reuse directly.
- **Pages**: 25-30
- **Difficulty to adapt**: Medium — original text is longer than our target, needs condensing

### The Tortoise and the Hare (Aesop)
- **Why**: Two characters, one race, universal story. Clear page-by-page visual arc.
- **Art**: Generate new. Milo Winter (1919) and Rackham (1912) only have 1-2 illustrations per fable — far too few for a 15-18 page picture book. Beautiful for reference but can't be reused as the primary art source.
- **Pages**: 15-18
- **Difficulty to adapt**: Medium — story is simple but need to resist urge to moralize; just tell the race

### The Lion and the Mouse (Aesop)
- **Why**: Lion catches mouse, lets it go, mouse later frees lion from a net. Two characters, clear physical action, dramatic visuals.
- **Art**: Generate new. Same issue as Tortoise/Hare — Winter and Rackham only have 1-2 illustrations per fable, not enough for a full picture book.
- **Pages**: 15-18
- **Difficulty to adapt**: Medium — same caveat as Tortoise/Hare, need to simplify heavily from any English source

## Tier 2: Good Fit (more adaptation work)

### The Tale of Peter Rabbit (Beatrix Potter, 1902)
- **Why**: Iconic chase narrative. Naughty rabbit in Mr. McGregor's garden.
- **Art**: Potter's watercolors public domain on [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/14838) and Wikimedia Commons.
- **Pages**: 20-25
- **Difficulty to adapt**: Medium-high — original is ~1000 words with sophisticated vocabulary. Plot is simple enough but needs heavy simplification. Note: existing Latin translations (*Fabula de Petro Cuniculo*) use complex literary Latin, not our style.

### The Little Engine That Could (Watty Piper / Lois Lenski, 1930)
- **Why**: Public domain as of January 2026. Refrain: *"Possum, putō! Possum, putō!"* The definitive spes (hope) story — hits all four Thomistic conditions. The three refusing engines model the two sins against hope (praesumptio, desperatio).
- **Art**: Lenski's 1930 illustrations now PD on [Internet Archive](https://archive.org/details/little-engine-that-could-1930), but only ~12-15 illustrations (not enough for our format). Mix of color plates and B&W. **Recommend generating new art** and using Lenski as style reference. See `docs/little_engine_plan.md` for full analysis.
- **Pages**: 22 (planned)
- **Difficulty to adapt**: Medium — text needs condensing but the refrain carries it
- **Status**: Full adaptation plan written. See `docs/little_engine_plan.md`.

### Goldilocks and the Three Bears (L. Leslie Brooke illus., 1905)
- **Why**: Triple repetition (chairs, bowls, beds). *"Nimis calida! Nimis frigida! Apta!"*
- **Art**: Brooke's public domain illustrations on [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/15661/15661-h/15661-h.htm).
- **Pages**: 22-28
- **Difficulty to adapt**: Medium — 9 scenes (3 items × 3 categories) may run long

### The Gingerbread Man (folk tale, first published 1875)
- **Why**: Chase + cumulative. *"Curre! Mē capere nōn potes!"*
- **Art**: Generate new (various pre-1929 editions exist)
- **Pages**: 20-25
- **Difficulty to adapt**: Medium — dark ending (fox eats him), chase could get repetitive without good art

### The Little Red Hen (folk tale)
- **Why**: Perfect repetition. Hen asks for help at each stage, everyone says *"Nōn ego!"*
- **Art**: Generate new
- **Pages**: 18-22
- **Difficulty to adapt**: Low-medium — structure is great, just needs visual variety

### The Velveteen Rabbit (Margery Williams / William Nicholson, 1922)
- **Why**: Beautiful emotional arc. Nicholson's watercolors are public domain.
- **Art**: Only 8 original illustrations — would need to supplement. Available on [Public Domain Image Library](https://publicdomainimagelibrary.com/).
- **Pages**: 25-30
- **Difficulty to adapt**: High — original is long and philosophically complex, would be a very different book after simplification

## Tier 3: Worth Considering

| Story | Appeal | Challenge |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| **The Dog and His Reflection** (Aesop) | Possibly simplest fable — one character, one mistake | May only fill 10-12 pages |
| **The Crow and the Pitcher** (Aesop) | Clever problem-solving, visually repetitive (pebble by pebble) | Abstract concept for a toddler |
| **The Ugly Duckling** (Andersen) | Great transformation arc | Very long, emotionally complex, heavy adaptation |
| **The Bremen Town Musicians** (Grimm) | Four animals team up, physical comedy | Two-part plot, four characters to track |
| **Hey Diddle Diddle** (Caldecott illus., 1882) | Absurdist nursery rhyme, gorgeous PD art | Very short — rhyme is only a few lines |
| **The House That Jack Built** (Caldecott, 1878) | Classic cumulative rhyme, magnificent illustrations | Sentences grow impossibly long by the end |
| **Johnny Crow's Garden** (Brooke, 1903) | Whimsical animal parade | Humor depends entirely on English rhymes |
| **The North Wind and the Sun** (Aesop) | Simple two-character contest | Abstract concept (persuasion vs. force) for a toddler |

## Notes

- **Simplification is always needed.** Even the "easiest" English originals (Potter's baby books, simple Aesop retellings) use vocabulary and structures beyond our 3-7 word Latin sentence target. Every adaptation here means writing new simple Latin text inspired by the story, not translating the original text.
- **Illustration density matters.** We need ~1 image per page (15-25 images per book). Many "illustrated" PD books don't have nearly enough — Aesop collections (Winter, Rackham) typically have only 1-2 illustrations per fable. Always verify actual image count before planning to reuse PD art.
- **Verified illustration counts** (as of 2026-03):
  - Peter Rabbit (Potter): ~27-31 illustrations — **best PD art reuse candidate**
  - Millions of Cats (Gag): ~15-18 illustrations — likely enough (B&W only)
  - Fierce Bad Rabbit (Potter): ~14-16 illustrations — borderline
  - Three Little Pigs section of Golden Goose Book (Brooke): ~8-10 illustrations — **not enough** on its own
  - Little Engine That Could (Lenski): ~12-15 illustrations — **not enough**; mix of color plates and B&W; copyright confirmed PD Jan 2026
  - Aesop fables (Winter/Rackham): 1-2 per fable — **not usable** for our format
- **Existing Latin translations exist** for some classics (Peter Rabbit, Winnie the Pooh, Cat in the Hat) but all use complex literary Latin — totally different from our living-language approach.
- **Cumulative/repetitive tales are ideal** — they naturally produce the kind of repeated simple phrases that work for language acquisition.
- **Outline density matters.** A lean outline produces lean Latin. If the English source text is 3-4 words per page, the Latin will be 3 words per page and feel thin when read aloud. Aim for 5-7 word English sentences in the outline so the Latin has enough to work with (learned from The Enormous Turnip prototype — see `projects/the_enormous_turnip/`).
