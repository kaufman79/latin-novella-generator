# The Theological Virtues: A Deep Dive for Story Creation

Research notes on fides, spes, and caritas in the classical and Christian virtue tradition, with practical application to Latin children's book storytelling.

Sources: Thomas Aquinas, *Summa Theologiae* IIa-IIae (the "Secunda Secundae"); Josef Pieper, *Faith, Hope, Love* (1986) and *On Hope* (1935); C.S. Lewis, *Mere Christianity* (1952) and *The Four Loves* (1960); Aristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics*; relevant scholastic commentary.

---

## Preliminary: How the Theological Virtues Relate to the Cardinal Virtues

### The Classical Framework

Aristotle's cardinal virtues (prudentia, iustitia, fortitudo, temperantia) are **acquired** virtues: you develop them through practice and habit. A child who repeatedly acts courageously becomes courageous. They are virtues of human effort and are accessible to anyone through natural reason.

The theological virtues (fides, spes, caritas) are, in Aquinas's framework, **infused** virtues: they are gifts of grace, directed toward God as their ultimate object. Their formal theological definitions always involve a supernatural orientation.

### Why This Matters for Stories (and Why It Doesn't Block Us)

Aquinas himself distinguishes between the **infused** theological virtues and their **natural analogues**. The theological virtue of fides is directed toward God as first truth; but there is a natural human capacity for trust, loyalty, and faithfulness that participates in the same structure. Pieper makes this point explicitly: the theological virtues have natural roots that are visible in ordinary human life.

For children's stories, we are working with the **natural analogues** -- the human capacities that the theological virtues perfect. This is not a compromise or a dilution. It is how these virtues actually appear in the world: in a child who trusts her father, in a sailor who hopes through a storm, in a friend who sacrifices for another. The theological tradition holds that grace perfects nature, which means the natural forms of these virtues are real and good in themselves.

C.S. Lewis makes this accessible in *Mere Christianity* III.10: the theological virtues in their "natural" form are already present in everyone who trusts, hopes, or loves. The specifically Christian claim is that these capacities point beyond themselves -- but the capacities are real at every level.

**Bottom line for our project**: You can write a story that deeply models fides, spes, or caritas without any religious content whatsoever. A story about a dog who waits faithfully for his master is a fides story. A story about a lost child who keeps walking toward home is a spes story. A story about a mother bird who shelters her chicks in a storm is a caritas story. These are not "secularized" versions of the virtues -- they are the natural soil in which the virtues grow.

## I. Fides (Faith / Trust / Faithfulness)

### What Aquinas Actually Says

**Summa Theologiae IIa-IIae, qq. 1-16**

Aquinas defines fides formally as *"actus intellectus assentientis veritati divinae ex imperio voluntatis a Deo motae per gratiam"* -- an act of the intellect assenting to divine truth, commanded by the will, moved by grace (IIa-IIae, q. 2, a. 9). This is the strict theological definition.

But Aquinas's analysis is much richer than this formula suggests. He distinguishes three dimensions of fides (q. 2, a. 2):

1. **Credere Deum** -- believing *that* God exists (assent to a proposition)
2. **Credere Deo** -- believing God, i.e., trusting the one who speaks (trust in a person)
3. **Credere in Deum** -- believing *into* God, i.e., moving toward God in love (the dynamic, relational dimension)

The second and third dimensions are the ones that matter for stories. Fides is not primarily about intellectual propositions. It is about **trust in a person** and **commitment toward that person**. Aquinas says that fides without the movement of love (credere in Deum) is *fides informis* -- "unformed faith," incomplete.

### The Related Concepts

**Fiducia (trust/confidence)**: Aquinas treats fiducia as an act of hope strengthened by faith (IIa-IIae, q. 129, a. 6). It is the felt confidence that comes from trusting someone reliable. In ordinary usage, fiducia is the experiential core of fides: the feeling of security when you trust someone who has proven trustworthy.

**Fidelitas (faithfulness/loyalty)**: This is fides from the other direction. Where fides-as-trust is the act of relying on another, fidelitas is the act of *being* reliable for another. Aquinas treats it under iustitia (keeping promises, honoring commitments), but it is inseparable from fides: to trust is to make yourself vulnerable, and to be faithful is to honor that vulnerability. A faithful friend and a trusting friend are two sides of the same relationship.

### What Fides Actually Means (Not the English Shorthand)

Fides is **entrusting yourself to another person based on their character**. It involves:

- **Vulnerability**: You cannot exercise fides without risk. Trusting someone means accepting that they *could* betray you. This is why fides is a virtue -- it requires courage.
- **Knowledge of the other**: Fides is not blind. Aquinas insists that faith has reasons (q. 1, a. 4-5). You trust someone because you have come to know them as trustworthy. Blind credulity is not fides.
- **Commitment over time**: Fides is not a single act but a sustained disposition. It endures through uncertainty and difficulty.
- **Reciprocity**: Fides creates bonds. When you trust someone and they prove faithful, the relationship deepens. Fides builds community.

Josef Pieper (*Faith, Hope, Love*, ch. 2) emphasizes that all human fides is ultimately interpersonal: "I believe you." Not "I believe that X is true" but "I believe *you* -- I entrust myself to you." This is why fides is so close to love: you can only truly trust someone you care about, and you can only truly love someone you trust.

### What Fides Is NOT

- **Not mere intellectual belief.** "I believe the earth is round" is not fides in the virtue sense. Fides is relational, not propositional.
- **Not credulity or gullibility.** Trusting everyone indiscriminately is not fides but foolishness (a defect of prudentia). Fides involves discernment.
- **Not certainty.** Fides exists precisely in the space where you *don't* have proof. If you could see, you wouldn't need to trust. Aquinas: faith is "of things not seen" (q. 1, a. 4).
- **Not the absence of fear.** You can trust someone and still be afraid. Fides is the decision to rely on another *despite* fear.
- **Not passive.** Fides demands action: you act on the basis of your trust. A child who trusts her father enough to jump into his arms is performing an act of fides.

### How Fides Appears in a Children's Story

The natural analogue of fides shows up whenever a character:

- **Trusts another character who has earned that trust.** A small animal follows a guide through a dark forest because the guide has been reliable before.
- **Remains loyal when it would be easier to abandon.** A friend stays when others leave.
- **Acts on trust without proof.** A character takes a path she cannot see the end of, because someone she trusts told her it leads home.
- **Keeps a promise even when it costs something.** This is fidelitas -- the other face of fides.
- **Returns trust for trust.** A character who was helped by a stranger later helps that stranger in return -- not from obligation, but from the bond of trust.

**The emotional through-line of a fides story**: doubt/fear --> decision to trust --> trust tested --> trust vindicated (or: trust broken --> consequences --> rebuilding). The key moment is always the **decision** to trust when you could choose not to.

**Concrete visible actions** (for illustration):
- A character reaching out a hand to another in darkness
- A character following someone into the unknown, looking nervous but going forward
- A character waiting faithfully (a dog at a door, a friend at a meeting place)
- Two characters making or keeping a promise (hands clasped, objects exchanged)
- A character catching another who falls (trust physically enacted)

### Rating Fides 0-5 in a Story

| Rating | Description | Example |
|:------:|-------------|---------|
| **0** | Trust/faithfulness plays no role in the story. | A story about a character solving a puzzle alone. |
| **1** | A minor moment of trust or loyalty, but not structurally important. | A character briefly relies on a friend's advice. |
| **2** | Trust or faithfulness is present and visible but is not the central driver. | A character has a faithful companion, but the main conflict is about something else (like courage). |
| **3** | Trust is a significant theme. A character makes a meaningful choice to trust or to be faithful, and this choice affects the outcome. | A character must decide whether to trust a stranger's directions. The decision matters but is not the story's climax. |
| **4** | The story's central conflict hinges on trust or faithfulness. There is a real moment where trust is tested and the character's choice to trust (or to be faithful) drives the resolution. | A character follows a guide into danger because she trusts him, and that trust is vindicated at the climax. |
| **5** | The entire emotional arc is built around fides. The story would not exist without the theme of trust/faithfulness. Trust is tested, costs something, and is shown to be worthwhile. | A story about two friends separated by circumstances, each remaining faithful to the other, reunited because of that faithfulness. Think: *Charlotte's Web* (Wilbur trusts Charlotte; Charlotte is faithful unto death). |

## II. Spes (Hope)

### What Aquinas Actually Says

**Summa Theologiae IIa-IIae, qq. 17-22**

Aquinas defines spes as a movement of the appetite toward a **future good** that is **difficult** but **possible to attain** (q. 17, a. 1). He borrows this structure from Aristotle's analysis of the irascible appetite in the *De Anima* commentary.

The four conditions of spes (q. 17, a. 1; cf. I-II, q. 40, a. 1):
1. It is directed toward a **good** (not just any future state -- something genuinely worth having)
2. The good is **future** (you don't hope for what you already possess)
3. The good is **arduous/difficult** (you don't hope for what is easy or trivial -- you just expect it)
4. The good is **possible** (you don't hope for the impossible -- that is delusion)

This is the critical insight: **spes is specifically about difficult goods**. It is not optimism. It is not wishing. It is the sustained movement of the will toward something genuinely hard to achieve, paired with the confidence that it *can* be achieved.

### The Relationship Between Spes and Fortitudo

This is one of the most important connections in the virtue framework, and Aquinas makes it explicitly (IIa-IIae, q. 17, a. 1, ad 2; cf. q. 123, a. 1).

**Fortitudo** (courage/endurance) deals with **enduring evils** -- standing firm against threats, bearing pain, persevering through suffering.

**Spes** deals with **pursuing goods** -- maintaining movement toward something valuable despite difficulty and delay.

They are paired because real difficulty always involves both: you must endure hardship (fortitudo) while continuing to pursue the good (spes). A character who endures but has given up on the goal has fortitudo without spes -- this is grim stoic endurance, not hope. A character who desires the goal but collapses at the first difficulty has (immature) spes without fortitudo -- this is wishful thinking.

The relationship: **spes gives fortitudo its direction, and fortitudo gives spes its backbone.** Hope without courage is fantasy. Courage without hope is mere stubbornness.

### Josef Pieper on Hope

Pieper's *On Hope* (1935) is the essential modern treatment. His key insights:

**Hope is a response to the "not yet."** The human condition is fundamentally one of being *on the way* (status viatoris). We are not yet what we will be. Hope is the virtue that keeps us moving on this journey without either despairing ("it's impossible") or presuming ("it's guaranteed").

**The two sins against hope are despair (desperatio) and presumption (praesumptio).** Aquinas treats both (qq. 20-21). Despair says "the good is impossible for me." Presumption says "the good is certain, so I need not strive." Both destroy spes: despair by denying possibility, presumption by denying difficulty.

**Hope is fundamentally about one's own capacity (aided by others and by grace) to reach the good.** This is personal. It is not "I hope the weather improves" but "I trust that I can make it through the storm." The subject of spes is always the hoper's own journey.

Pieper's formulation: hope is **"the confident, patient, 'not-yet' of the human person on the way."**

### What Spes Actually Means (Not the English Shorthand)

Spes is **the confident, active pursuit of a genuine good that is difficult to reach**, sustained through hardship, delay, and uncertainty. It involves:

- **A real goal worth pursuing.** Spes is always directed at something genuinely good. A character hoping to steal something is not exercising spes in the virtue sense.
- **Real difficulty.** The goal must be hard. If it were easy, there would be nothing to hope for.
- **Active pursuit, not passive waiting.** Spes is a movement *toward* the good. The hoping person is doing something, not just sitting and wishing.
- **Confidence in possibility.** Not certainty -- that would be presumption. But genuine belief that the good can be attained.
- **Endurance through the "not yet."** Spes sustains you in the gap between desire and fulfillment.

### What Spes Is NOT

- **Not optimism.** Optimism is a temperamental disposition ("things tend to work out"). Spes is a virtue that requires effort and can be exercised by a natural pessimist. Pieper: optimism "excuses one from the task of hoping."
- **Not wishing.** "I wish I could fly" is not spes. There must be real possibility and real effort.
- **Not denial of difficulty.** "Everything will be fine" is often the opposite of hope -- it is a refusal to face the real difficulty. True spes looks the difficulty in the face and moves forward anyway.
- **Not certainty about outcomes.** Spes exists precisely because the outcome is uncertain. If you knew it would work out, you wouldn't need hope.
- **Not the same as fortitudo.** Fortitudo endures present evil; spes pursues future good. They often appear together but are distinct. A character who grimly endures without any vision of something better is brave but not hopeful.
- **Not a feeling.** Spes can coexist with fear, grief, and exhaustion. It is a *choice* to keep moving toward the good, not a sunny emotional state.

### C.S. Lewis on Hope

Lewis in *Mere Christianity* III.10 ("Hope") makes a crucial point: hope is not escapism. "Hope is one of the theological virtues. This means that a continual looking forward to the eternal world is not (as some modern people think) a form of escapism or wishful thinking, but one of the things a Christian is meant to do."

In the natural register: the person who keeps working toward a goal they believe in, despite setbacks, is not engaged in fantasy. They are exercising a real virtue. Lewis elsewhere (*The Weight of Glory*): "If we find ourselves with a desire that nothing in this world can satisfy, the most probable explanation is that we were made for another world." The natural form of this: every real hope points toward something worth pursuing.

### How Spes Appears in a Children's Story

The natural analogue of spes shows up whenever a character:

- **Keeps going toward a goal when it gets hard.** Not because they're tough (that's fortitudo) but because they still believe in the goal.
- **Refuses to despair when things look impossible.** A character in a dark place who says "there must be a way" and looks for it.
- **Encourages others not to give up.** Hope is communicable: a hoping character strengthens those around them.
- **Chooses effort over resignation.** When it would be easier and perhaps more "realistic" to quit, the character chooses to continue.
- **Discovers that the journey was harder than expected but still worth it.** The good at the end vindicates the hope that sustained the journey.

**The emotional through-line of a spes story**: desire for a good --> difficulty/setback --> temptation to despair --> renewed commitment --> achievement (or: noble failure that still vindicates the hope). The key moment is the **renewal** -- the point where the character could give up and doesn't, not because of stubbornness but because they still see the good worth pursuing.

**Concrete visible actions** (for illustration):
- A character climbing when the path is steep, looking upward
- A character rebuilding something that was destroyed (a nest, a home, a bridge)
- A character lighting a candle or lamp in darkness
- A character planting seeds in winter / barren ground
- A character holding something precious (a letter, a token, a memory of what they're pursuing) while enduring hardship
- A character pointing forward ("this way") when companions are discouraged
- A small green shoot emerging from cracked ground

### Rating Spes 0-5 in a Story

| Rating | Description | Example |
|:------:|-------------|---------|
| **0** | Hope/perseverance toward a future good plays no role. | A story about a character reacting to present events with no forward-looking arc. |
| **1** | A character briefly wants something and gets it without significant difficulty, or perseverance appears in passing. | A character walks to a destination and arrives without real obstacles. |
| **2** | A character faces a setback and overcomes it, but the "hope" element is not emotionally central. | A character loses something, is briefly sad, finds it again. The story is more about the finding than the hoping. |
| **3** | The story has a genuine "dark moment" where the goal seems unreachable, and the character's perseverance matters to the resolution. But hope shares the stage with other themes. | A character on a journey faces a real obstacle, considers giving up, continues, and reaches the goal. But the story is also significantly about courage, friendship, etc. |
| **4** | Hope/perseverance is the central emotional engine. There is a real moment of temptation to despair, and the character's choice to keep hoping drives the climax. The difficulty is genuine, not token. | A character separated from home endures a long, genuinely difficult journey. The story's emotional weight is in the "not yet" -- the scenes where we see the character holding on. |
| **5** | The entire story is a study in hope. The difficulty is sustained and real. Despair is a genuine temptation (not dismissed easily). The character's hope costs them something. The resolution vindicates hope itself as a way of being. | *The Return of the King*: Frodo and Sam in Mordor. Sam's speech about stories worth being in. The good seems genuinely impossible, the characters are exhausted, and the choice to keep going is the moral center of the narrative. In a children's register: a story about a baby sea turtle making the long journey to the ocean, facing gulls and crabs and exhaustion, sustained by something it cannot name but will not abandon. |

## III. Caritas (Love / Charity)

### What Aquinas Actually Says

**Summa Theologiae IIa-IIae, qq. 23-46**

Aquinas's treatise on caritas is the longest single treatise in the Summa -- 24 questions, over 100 articles. This is not an accident. Caritas is, for Aquinas, the "form of all the virtues" (*forma virtutum*) -- the virtue that gives all other virtues their ultimate direction and life (q. 23, a. 8).

His definition: caritas is **amicitia quaedam hominis ad Deum** -- "a kind of friendship between the human person and God" (q. 23, a. 1). He builds this on Aristotle's analysis of friendship (philia) in *Nicomachean Ethics* VIII-IX, which requires:

1. **Mutual benevolence** (willing the good of the other)
2. **Based on communication** (shared life, not just shared interest)
3. **Reciprocal** (both parties participate)

Aquinas extends Aristotle's philia to the divine-human relationship and calls it caritas. But the structure -- mutual good-willing based on shared life -- applies at every level.

### The Key Thomistic Distinction: Amor, Dilectio, Caritas, Amicitia

Aquinas carefully distinguishes related terms (q. 26, a. 3; cf. I-II, q. 26, a. 3):

- **Amor**: The broadest term. Any attraction or affection. Animals have amor. It includes desire (concupiscentia) and delight.
- **Dilectio**: Love that involves choice (from *diligere*, related to *eligere* -- to choose). Dilectio adds the rational will to amor. You can feel amor for chocolate; dilectio is reserved for persons.
- **Amicitia**: Friendship. Mutual dilectio between persons who share a common life. Requires equality and reciprocity. Aristotle's central concept.
- **Caritas**: The highest form. Amicitia directed toward the ultimate good. In the theological register, friendship with God extended to all whom God loves. In the natural register: love that wills the genuine, deep good of the other -- not what pleases them, but what is truly good for them.

The crucial move: **caritas is not a feeling but an act of the will.** "To love is to will the good of the other" (*amare est velle bonum alicui* -- I-II, q. 26, a. 4, drawing on Aristotle, *Rhetoric* II.4). This is Aquinas's most famous formula about love, and it is the key to understanding caritas.

### The Interior Acts of Caritas (qq. 27-33)

Aquinas analyzes what caritas does internally:

- **Dilectio** (q. 27): The core act -- choosing the good of the other.
- **Gaudium** (q. 28): Joy -- delighting in the good of the beloved. You rejoice when the one you love flourishes.
- **Pax** (q. 29): Peace -- the harmony of wills. Where there is true caritas, there is the end of conflict, because both parties will the good.
- **Misericordia** (q. 30): Mercy/compassion -- grieving at the suffering of the other and being moved to help. Aquinas calls misericordia the external effect of caritas that is "greatest among the virtues that relate to neighbor" (q. 30, a. 4). Misericordia is caritas meeting suffering.
- **Beneficentia** (q. 31): Active doing of good for the other. Caritas that has hands and feet.
- **Eleemosyna** (q. 32): Almsgiving/generosity -- the concrete material expression of beneficentia.
- **Correctio fraterna** (q. 33): Fraternal correction -- loving someone enough to tell them the truth even when it's hard. This is not "being nice." It is caring about the other's genuine good even when that means discomfort.

### The Exterior Acts of Caritas: What Caritas Looks Like

This is where Aquinas gets concrete and where stories find their material:

**Misericordia (mercy/compassion)** -- q. 30: Aquinas defines it as "heartfelt sympathy for another's distress, impelling us to help if we can" (*misericordia est tristitia quaedam de alieno malo*). It requires *feeling* the other's pain (not just knowing about it intellectually) and *acting* to relieve it. This is perhaps the most story-ready form of caritas: a character sees another suffering and is moved -- moved all the way to action.

**Benevolentia (goodwill)** -- q. 27: Simply willing the good of the other. This is the base layer. It does not require friendship or intimacy. You can exercise benevolentia toward a stranger. It is the disposition that says "I want things to go well for you" before any action follows.

**Amicitia (friendship)** -- q. 23, a. 1: Caritas in its fullest natural form. Two persons who share life together, will each other's good, and delight in each other's company. Aristotle's three kinds of friendship (utility, pleasure, virtue) are relevant here: caritas corresponds to the "perfect friendship" (*amicitia perfecta*) that is based on the good character of each friend.

### C.S. Lewis on Love

Lewis's *The Four Loves* (1960) provides the most accessible mapping:

- **Storge** (affection): The comfortable, familiar love of family and long association. Closest to natural amor.
- **Philia** (friendship): Mutual delight in a shared interest or vision. Lewis's analysis of friendship beautifully captures amicitia.
- **Eros** (romantic love): Not relevant for children's stories, but Lewis's point about eros is relevant: even eros, at its best, is self-giving, not self-seeking.
- **Agape** (self-giving love): Lewis's equivalent of caritas. The love that gives without calculating return. Lewis's key insight: agape is not a feeling to be waited for but a pattern of action to be practiced. "Do not waste time bothering whether you 'love' your neighbour; act as if you did."

Lewis also warns against "love" that is really possessiveness, control, or need-satisfaction disguised as generosity. This is crucial for stories: caritas gives freedom. It does not cling, control, or demand gratitude.

### What Caritas Actually Means (Not the English Shorthand)

Caritas is **actively willing and doing the genuine good of another person, even at cost to yourself.** It involves:

- **Willing the other's real good** (not just what they want or what makes them happy in the moment). A parent who says "no" to a child's dangerous demand is exercising caritas.
- **Action, not just feeling.** Caritas without deeds is empty. Aquinas insists on beneficentia -- actually doing good.
- **Willingness to sacrifice.** Caritas is measured by what you give up, not by how you feel. "Greater love has no one than this: to lay down his life for his friends" (John 15:13) -- but also: to give up your last piece of bread, to stay awake keeping watch, to do the boring unglamorous work of care.
- **Universality.** Caritas extends to all -- not just friends, not just the lovable. Aquinas: we are to love even enemies (q. 25, a. 8). In the natural register: genuine goodwill toward those who have not earned it.
- **Freedom and respect.** Caritas does not control or possess. It wills the other's *own* good, including their freedom. A caritas that smothers is not caritas.
- **Joy in the other's flourishing.** Gaudium: when someone you love with caritas succeeds, you are glad -- not jealous, not threatened.

### What Caritas Is NOT

- **Not "being nice."** Niceness is agreeableness, conflict-avoidance, people-pleasing. Caritas sometimes requires difficult truths (correctio fraterna), firm boundaries, or tough choices. A character who is "nice" to everyone, including the villain, is not necessarily exercising caritas -- they may be exercising cowardice.
- **Not "charity" in the modern sense (giving money/things).** Eleemosyna (almsgiving) is one expression of caritas, but caritas is far broader. You can exercise caritas with no money at all: by listening, by forgiving, by being present.
- **Not sentimentality.** Caritas is not a warm feeling. It is a choice sustained over time. It can coexist with frustration, exhaustion, and even anger (Aquinas allows for righteous anger within caritas).
- **Not self-erasure.** Aquinas explicitly includes proper self-love within caritas (q. 25, a. 4). You cannot love others well if you destroy yourself. Caritas is self-giving, not self-annihilating.
- **Not transactional.** Caritas does not keep accounts. It is not "I helped you, so you owe me." The return of caritas is joy, not repayment.
- **Not indiscriminate.** Aquinas teaches an "order of charity" (ordo caritatis, q. 26): you owe more to those closer to you (family, friends, community) than to strangers. A character who neglects her own children to help distant strangers is not exercising caritas well.

### How Caritas Appears in a Children's Story

The natural analogue of caritas shows up whenever a character:

- **Sacrifices something for another's genuine good.** A character gives up her last food, her warm spot, her safety -- not dramatically, but because someone else needs it more.
- **Helps without being asked and without expecting return.** Spontaneous, other-directed action.
- **Forgives.** This is misericordia in action. A character who has been wronged chooses to release the wrongdoer rather than pursue revenge.
- **Shows mercy to the weak, the small, the undeserving.** The lion who spares the mouse. The strong character who could dominate but instead protects.
- **Chooses relationship over advantage.** A character who could win by abandoning a companion but stays.
- **Does unglamorous, patient care.** Not the dramatic rescue but the daily feeding, the staying awake through the night, the mending of something broken. This is the most underused form of caritas in stories and one of the most powerful.
- **Delights in another's success without jealousy.** A character who helped another character succeed and is glad, not resentful.

**The emotional through-line of a caritas story**: awareness of another's need --> choice to act (often at cost) --> the cost felt and borne --> the other's good achieved --> joy (gaudium) or peace (pax) as the result. The key moment is the **choice to act at cost** -- the moment where self-interest and other-interest diverge and the character chooses the other.

**Concrete visible actions** (for illustration):
- A character sharing food (breaking bread, dividing an apple)
- A character sheltering another (a wing over a smaller creature, a cloak shared)
- A character carrying another who is too tired or hurt to walk
- A character bandaging, cleaning, or tending a wound
- A character staying awake while another sleeps safely
- A character building or making something for someone else
- A character reaching back to pull another up a slope
- A character releasing something precious (a bird from a cage, a grudge)
- Two characters reconciling after conflict (forehead touch, hands held)

### Rating Caritas 0-5 in a Story

| Rating | Description | Example |
|:------:|-------------|---------|
| **0** | Self-giving love, mercy, or active goodwill plays no role. | A story about a character achieving a personal goal alone. |
| **1** | A small act of kindness appears but is not important to the plot. | A character shares a greeting or a smile. The story is about something else entirely. |
| **2** | An act of generosity or kindness is present and noted but is not the emotional center. | A character helps another character once, and the story moves on to a different conflict. |
| **3** | Love/mercy is a significant theme. A character makes a meaningful sacrifice or act of compassion that affects the outcome, but the story has other equally important themes. | A character rescues another at some risk, but the story is equally about the adventure itself. |
| **4** | The story's central conflict is resolved by an act of self-giving love, mercy, or compassion. The character's choice to love costs them something real. | A character gives up something precious to save another. The emotional weight of the story is in the giving. |
| **5** | The entire story is built around caritas. The love shown is costly, sustained, and transformative -- it changes both the giver and the receiver. Multiple forms of caritas are visible (sacrifice, mercy, patience, joy in the other's good). | *The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe*: Aslan's sacrifice for Edmund. In a children's register: a story about a mother bird who cannot fly south with the flock because her chick's wing is broken, so she stays through winter, finds food, keeps the chick warm, and in spring the chick flies for the first time -- and the mother's joy is the climax, not her suffering. |

## IV. How the Theological Virtues Work Together

### The Unity of the Three

Aquinas teaches that the theological virtues form an ordered whole (IIa-IIae, q. 17, a. 8; q. 23, a. 6-8):

- **Fides** is the foundation: you must know and trust the good before you can hope for it or love it.
- **Spes** builds on fides: trusting that the good is real, you now pursue it with confidence through difficulty.
- **Caritas** is the crown: having trusted and hoped, you now give yourself to the good and to others in love. Caritas is the "form" that completes the other two.

The order: fides --> spes --> caritas. But also: caritas flows back and strengthens fides (you trust more deeply those you love) and spes (you hope more persistently for those you love).

### In Stories: The Three Often Appear Together

A story rated high on caritas will often have significant fides and spes as well, because self-giving love requires trust and hope. But each virtue can appear independently:

- A **fides** story might have little caritas (a story about trust between equals, not sacrifice)
- A **spes** story might have little caritas (a solo journey toward a personal goal)
- A **caritas** story might have fides and spes as supporting elements but center on the act of love itself

### The Natural Analogues in Summary

| Theological Virtue | Natural Analogue | Story Essence |
|---|---|---|
| **Fides** | Trust, faithfulness, loyalty, keeping promises | "I believe in you / I will not abandon you" |
| **Spes** | Perseverance, resilience, not-despairing, confident pursuit of the good | "It is hard, but it is worth it, and I will keep going" |
| **Caritas** | Self-giving love, mercy, compassion, willing the good of the other at cost to self | "Your good matters more to me than my comfort" |

## V. Practical Application: Our Current Coverage Gap

Looking at the existing book collection:

| Book | Fides | Spes | Caritas |
|------|:-----:|:----:|:-------:|
| Augustine Steals the Pears | 2 | 0 | 2 |
| Dada and the Cockroach | 0 | 0 | 2 |
| Locusts and Dragon | 0 | 0 | 1 |
| Lion/Witch/Wardrobe | 4 | 3 | 5 |
| Link and the Stolen Treasure | 0 | 3 | 1 |
| Peter Rabbit | 0 | 1 | 1 |
| The Enormous Turnip | 1 | 2 | 4 |
| **Total** | **7** | **9** | **16** |

**Fides** is the most underrepresented theological virtue, carried almost entirely by the Lion/Witch/Wardrobe adaptation. The collection badly needs a story where trust or faithfulness is the central theme.

**Spes** is also thin, with only Lion/Witch/Wardrobe and Link providing meaningful hope content. A story where perseverance through genuine difficulty -- not just action-adventure "obstacles" -- is the emotional core would fill this gap.

**Caritas** is better covered thanks to Lion/Witch/Wardrobe (5) and Enormous Turnip (4), but the other books barely touch it. More stories with visible acts of mercy, sacrifice, and care would deepen the coverage.

### Story Types That Would Fill the Gaps

**For fides (trust/faithfulness)**:
- A "faithful companion" story: a character who waits, keeps a promise, or remains loyal when tested. Think: Hachiko, Penelope waiting for Odysseus, Sam Gamgee.
- A "learning to trust" story: a character who has been hurt and must decide to trust again. The trust is rewarded.
- A "keeping the promise" story: a character makes a promise and keeps it despite escalating costs.

**For spes (hope/perseverance)**:
- A "long journey home" story: a character far from home, sustained by the vision of return. Obstacles are real and sustained, not quickly dispatched.
- A "rebuilding after loss" story: something is destroyed, and a character patiently rebuilds. The rebuilding IS the story, not a prologue to revenge.
- A "planting and waiting" story: a character plants something (literal or metaphorical) and must care for it through a long season before seeing results. Patience, not action, is the virtue.

**For caritas (love/mercy/compassion)**:
- A "mercy to the undeserving" story: a character shows kindness to one who has wronged them. The Lion and the Mouse is a perfect candidate (already on the priority list).
- A "patient care" story: a character tends to another through illness, weakness, or need. Not a dramatic rescue but daily, unglamorous love.
- A "giving up something precious" story: a character sacrifices something they deeply value for another's genuine good. The sacrifice is visible and costly.
