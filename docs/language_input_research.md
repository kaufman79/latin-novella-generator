# Picture Books as Language Input: Research and Design Principles

Practical research notes on using picture books as comprehensible input for a young child (age 3-4) acquiring a second language at home through parent-child reading. Written for a living-language context, not a classroom.

Sources: Stephen Krashen, *The Input Hypothesis* (1985) and *Principles and Practice in Second Language Acquisition* (1982); Jim Trelease, *The Read-Aloud Handbook* (7th ed., 2013); Patton Tabors, *One Child, Two Languages* (2nd ed., 2008); Annick De Houwer, *Bilingual First Language Acquisition* (2009); Virginia Mueller Gathercole & Enlli Mon Thomas, research on minority language input quantity; Colin Baker, *A Parents' and Teachers' Guide to Bilingualism* (4th ed., 2014); Elena Nicoladis & Stefka Marinova-Todd, research on bilingual vocabulary development; Elley & Mangubhai, "The Impact of Reading on Second Language Learning" (1983); various studies from the *International Journal of Bilingualism* and *Bilingualism: Language and Cognition*.

---

## 1. Picture Books as Comprehensible Input (Krashen's i+1)

### The Core Theory

Krashen's Input Hypothesis holds that language acquisition (as distinct from conscious language *learning*) happens when a person receives input that is slightly beyond their current level of competence. If a child's current level is *i*, the input that drives acquisition is *i+1* -- comprehensible, but containing some new elements that the child has not yet acquired. The crucial point: the input must be *understood*, even if not every word is known.

This is exactly what picture books do. The child does not need to understand every Latin word on the page because the illustration provides the meaning. A child who hears "Avus rapam trahit" while looking at a picture of grandfather pulling a turnip has received comprehensible input -- the meaning is clear even if "trahit" is new. The picture has done the work of making the unknown word comprehensible. The next time the child hears "trahit" in a different context, it is no longer fully unknown; it has a foothold.

### What i+1 Looks Like at This Level

For a child whose Latin comprehension is roughly at a 1-year-old level but growing, the *i* is very basic: familiar nouns (pater, mater, canis, aqua), simple actions (currit, dormit, videt), a handful of adjectives (magnus, parvus), and basic sentence frames (X est Y, X facit Y). The *+1* at any given moment should be one of:

- A new content word in an otherwise familiar sentence ("Pater **blattam** videt" -- new noun, but the sentence frame and the illustration make the meaning clear)
- A familiar word in a new grammatical form (accusative where nominative was known, perfect tense where present was known)
- A new sentence pattern with familiar vocabulary ("**Subitō** blatta currit!" -- new adverb, but the exclamation mark, the illustration, and the parent's dramatic reading make the meaning obvious)

The key constraint: **only one new element per sentence, and the rest must be graspable from context, illustration, or prior knowledge.** Two unknowns in a short sentence can collapse comprehension entirely. Three unknowns and the sentence becomes noise.

### The Affective Filter

Krashen's related concept of the Affective Filter is equally important. Acquisition is blocked when the learner is anxious, bored, or self-conscious. A child being read to by a parent at bedtime has the lowest possible affective filter -- safe, loved, engaged. This is an enormous advantage over classroom settings. The picture book reading context is nearly ideal for language acquisition precisely because the child is not "studying." The child is hearing a story.

## 2. The Role of Illustrations in Bridging Comprehension Gaps

### Illustrations as the Primary Comprehension Mechanism

For a child at this level, the illustrations are not decorative. They are the primary channel through which the child understands the story. The Latin text is the secondary channel. This is the reverse of how an adult reads a picture book -- an adult reads the text and glances at the pictures. For the L2-acquiring child, the pictures carry the story and the text rides along.

This means that **every illustration must precisely and unambiguously depict the action described in the Latin text.** If the text says "Lincus se in dolio celavit" and the illustration shows Link hiding in a barrel, the child acquires the meaning of "celavit" and "dolium" through the visual. If the illustration instead shows a wide shot of the fortress with Link barely visible, the comprehension bridge is broken -- the text and picture are not tightly coupled, and the new vocabulary floats without an anchor.

### What "Tight Coupling" Means in Practice

Research on bilingual children's book reading (Bus, van IJzendoorn & Pellegrini, 1995; Sénéchal, Thomas & Monker, 1995) consistently shows that the comprehension benefit of illustrations is proportional to how directly the picture depicts the text content. Abstract, atmospheric, or merely decorative illustrations provide little comprehension support. Concrete, action-depicting illustrations provide maximum support.

For our books, tight coupling means:

- **The illustration shows the verb.** If the text says "currit," the character is visibly running. If the text says "celavit," the character is visibly hiding. The verb is the hardest part for the child to extract from context; the illustration must make it visible.
- **New nouns are visually prominent.** If the text introduces "blatta" (cockroach), the cockroach should be large, central, and unmistakable in the illustration. Not a small detail in the corner.
- **The emotional state matches.** If the text says "Mater exclamavit," the mother's face shows alarm. Emotional states shown in illustrations help the child map Latin emotion words to their meanings.
- **One main action per page.** If the text describes one action, the illustration shows that one action. Illustrations that depict multiple simultaneous events split the child's attention and weaken the text-image link.

### The Pointing-and-Naming Opportunity

Illustrations also create opportunities for the parent to extend input beyond the text on the page. The parent can point to objects in the illustration and name them in Latin, ask "ubi est blatta?" (where is the cockroach?), or describe what characters are doing. This interactive use of illustrations is well-documented as a driver of vocabulary acquisition (Whitehurst et al., 1988, on dialogic reading; adapted for bilingual contexts by Roberts, 2008). The richer and more detailed the illustration, the more material the parent has for this kind of extension.

## 3. Optimal Text Complexity

### Sentence Length

For a child at roughly 1-year-old Latin comprehension, the target range is:

- **Primary sentences: 2-5 words.** This is the sweet spot. "Nox erat." (2 words). "Pater in lecto dormit." (4 words). "Avus rapam trahit." (3 words). Short enough that the child can process the whole unit, long enough to model real sentence structure.
- **Occasional stretch sentences: 5-8 words.** These can appear when the context is very clear and most words are familiar. "Mater telephonum capit et patrem vocat." (6 words) works because the illustration shows exactly what is happening, and the child probably knows "mater" and "pater."
- **Never more than 8 words** for a child at this level. Beyond that, the sentence exceeds working memory and the child stops processing it as language and hears it as a stream of sound.

### Vocabulary Load

The research on graded readers in L2 acquisition (Nation & Waring, 1997; Hu & Nation, 2000) suggests that for fluent reading comprehension, a reader needs to know 95-98% of the running words. For a child being read to with illustration support, the threshold is lower -- perhaps 70-80% -- because the illustrations fill in the gaps. But below about 60-70% known vocabulary, comprehension collapses even with illustration support.

Practical implication: **in any given book, roughly 70-80% of the content words should be things the child has heard before** (from previous books, from the father's daily speech, or from earlier in the same book through repetition). The remaining 20-30% are the new vocabulary being introduced. For a 15-page book with ~3 content words per page, that is roughly 45 content words, of which 30-35 should be familiar and 10-15 should be new.

This does not mean every book needs to be carefully vocabulary-controlled. It means the *natural* result of writing simple Latin about familiar topics (family, animals, food, home, adventure) will hit roughly the right ratio. If the father is speaking Latin daily, the child's receptive vocabulary is growing constantly, and what counts as "familiar" expands with each book.

### Grammar

Do not shelter grammar. This point is already established in the project's Latin guidelines, and the research supports it. Children acquiring a first language do not receive grammar-graded input -- they hear the full range of tense, case, number, and construction from birth, and they extract the patterns gradually. L2 acquisition in young children works the same way (Paradis, Genesee & Crago, 2011). The child will not acquire the ablative case on day one, but hearing it repeatedly in context ("in lecto," "in crib," "in dolio") builds the implicit pattern recognition that eventually produces acquisition.

The one caveat: **subordinate clauses and complex syntax should be rare.** Not because they are grammatically "too advanced," but because they increase sentence length past the working memory limit. "Avia, quam Avus vocaverat, rapam prehendere conata est" is grammatically fine Latin but is 8+ words with nested clauses -- too long for this level. Break it into two sentences: "Avus Aviam vocavit. Avia rapam prehendit." Same meaning, same grammar, within the processing window.

## 4. Repetition Strategies

### Why Repetition Matters More Than Variety

In L1 acquisition, a child needs to hear a word roughly 10-20 times in meaningful context before it enters productive use (studies vary: Schwartz & Terrell, 1983, suggest fewer exposures for high-imageability words; some researchers suggest more). In L2 acquisition for young children, the number is likely similar or higher, because the L2 gets less total input time than the L1.

This means that **a single occurrence of a new word in a single book is insufficient for acquisition.** The word needs to recur -- within the same book, across books, and in the father's daily speech. Picture books are uniquely suited to this because children *want* to hear the same book read repeatedly. A book that is read 30 times provides 30 exposures to every word in it.

### Within-Book Repetition Strategies

**1. Refrains and repeated phrases.**
The most powerful tool. A phrase that recurs on every page (or every few pages) provides massive exposure in a single reading. "Trahunt et trahunt. NON POSSUNT!" in *The Enormous Turnip* is a perfect example -- it appears on 5 consecutive pages with only the characters changing. By the third occurrence, the child knows "trahunt" and "non possunt" even if they were new at the start.

Design principle: every book should have at least one recurring phrase. It should be short (2-4 words), rhythmic, emotionally charged, and tied to the core action or conflict of the story. The child should be able to anticipate it and eventually "read along."

**2. Cumulative structure.**
Stories where each new page adds one element to a growing list or chain. *The Enormous Turnip* is the classic example: each page adds one character to the pulling chain, with the rest of the text identical. The child hears the same sentence frame 5+ times with only one substitution per iteration. This is the gold standard for within-book vocabulary repetition.

Other cumulative structures: "The House That Jack Built" (each page adds a clause), "Brown Bear, Brown Bear" (same question-answer frame with new animals), "We're Going on a Bear Hunt" (repeated refrain between new obstacles).

**3. Parallel scenes.**
Different characters doing the same action, or the same character doing the same action in different locations. "Pater dormit. Aurelius dormit. Mater lactat." -- three parallel sentences establishing the nighttime scene in *Dada and the Cockroach.* The repeated sentence structure (subject + verb) makes each subsequent sentence easier to process.

**4. Echo phrases.**
A phrase from early in the book that returns at the end with changed meaning or emotional weight. "Nox erat" at the beginning of *Dada and the Cockroach* could return at the end after the crisis resolves. The child recognizes it, and the recognition itself is pleasurable and reinforcing.

### Across-Book Repetition Strategies

**1. Shared vocabulary across the collection.**
The collection should have a core vocabulary that appears in many books: family words (pater, mater), basic verbs (est, currit, videt, dormit, vocat), spatial words (in, super, ad), emotional words (laetus, territus). Each book adds a few new words on top of this shared base. Over time, the core becomes deeply acquired through sheer volume of exposure.

**2. Recurring character names.**
The Toon Link series has a built-in advantage here: Lincus, Tetra, and Moblini recur across multiple books, providing continuity. The child who has heard three Link adventures knows these names cold, which reduces the cognitive load when encountering a new Link book.

**3. Deliberate vocabulary recycling.**
When planning a new book, check which words appeared in recent books and deliberately reuse some of them. If *Dada and the Cockroach* introduced "blatta," "dormit," and "exclamavit," the next book could use "dormit" and "exclamavit" in different contexts. This cross-book repetition is what moves words from fragile recognition to solid acquisition.

## 5. Conversational Expansion During Reading

### The Amplification Effect

The father's talk *around* the book text is at least as important as the text itself for language acquisition. Research on parent-child book reading in bilingual homes (Patterson, 2002; Scheele, Leseman & Mayo, 2010) consistently finds that it is not the book text alone but the parent's interactive talk -- questions, expansions, connections to the child's life -- that drives vocabulary growth.

The book provides a shared referent (the picture, the story) that makes the parent's expansions comprehensible. Without the book, the father's Latin speech throughout the day may sometimes lack a clear referent -- the child hears words but may not always be sure what they refer to. During book reading, the referent is right there on the page, pointed at, visible. This makes every parental utterance during reading a high-quality piece of comprehensible input.

### Types of Expansion

**1. Labeling.** Pointing to objects in the illustration and naming them. "Ecce! Blatta! Blatta magna et foeda." This is the simplest form of expansion and is especially effective for nouns. It works because the referent is unambiguous -- the parent is pointing directly at the thing being named.

**2. Recasting.** Restating the book's text in simpler or different words. The book says "Mater telephonum capit et patrem vocat." The father says "Mater patrem vocat. 'Venī!' inquit." This provides the same meaning in a different form, giving the child two ways into the same content.

**3. Extension.** Adding information beyond the text. "Pater dormit. Pater fessus est. Pater semper in lecto dormit!" This extends the text with new vocabulary while keeping the topic anchored to the visible illustration.

**4. Connection to the child's life.** "Ecce, Aurelius dormit -- sicut TU dormis! Tu in lecto dormis." Connecting book content to the child's own experience is one of the most powerful comprehension aids, because the child already has a mental model of the referenced experience.

**5. Questioning.** "Ubi est blatta? Quis dormit? Quid facit pater?" Questions prompt the child to actively search the illustration and connect Latin words to visual referents. Even if the child responds in English (or gestures, or points), the question itself was comprehensible Latin input, and the child's mental processing of it is acquisition-productive.

### Practical Advice for the Reading Session

- **Read the Latin text as written first**, with expression and appropriate pacing. Let the child hear the "real" text.
- **Then expand.** Point at the picture, label things, ask questions, connect to the child's life. This second pass can be informal, slower, and responsive to the child's interest.
- **Follow the child's attention.** If the child points at the cockroach, talk about the cockroach in Latin, even if you were planning to focus on something else. The child's attention is the guide.
- **Do not translate.** Resist the urge to say the English after the Latin. If the child looks confused, point at the illustration, repeat the Latin more slowly, or rephrase in simpler Latin. The illustration should provide the meaning. Translating trains the child to wait for the English and stop trying to process the Latin.
- **Reread.** The same book, the same night, or the next night, or every night for a month. Rereading is not boring to the child -- it is acquisitionally productive every time. The child notices different things on different readings and acquires new elements each time.

## 6. What Makes a Picture Book Effective for L2 Acquisition (Not Just Entertainment)

### The Overlap and the Distinction

A good children's book is already doing most of what L2 acquisition needs: engaging narrative, clear illustrations, emotional investment, age-appropriate language. But there are specific features that make some books *more* effective as language input than others, and these can be designed for without making the book feel like a textbook.

### Features That Enhance Acquisition

**1. High text-image correspondence.** (Discussed in section 2.) The single most important feature. If a child can follow the story through the pictures alone, the Latin text riding alongside those pictures becomes comprehensible input automatically.

**2. Predictable structure.** Books where the child can anticipate what comes next -- because of a pattern, a refrain, or a cumulative structure -- allow the child to process the language with less cognitive load. When the child knows that the next page will have another character joining the pulling chain, they can devote more attention to the specific Latin words rather than trying to figure out what is happening. Predictability frees cognitive resources for language processing.

**3. Emotional salience.** Words encountered in emotionally charged moments are acquired faster and retained longer (Dewaele, 2004; Pavlenko, 2012, on the emotional resonance of L1 vs. L2). A cockroach running across a pillow, a barrel flying through the air, a tiny mouse saving the day -- these are emotionally salient moments. The Latin words attached to them get an acquisition boost. Design for emotional peaks: surprise, humor, suspense, triumph, relief. The flatter the emotional arc, the less acquisitionally productive the book.

**4. Physical action verbs.** Concrete, physical actions (running, pulling, hiding, jumping, eating, sleeping) are easier to depict in illustrations than mental states, and they are the verbs young children acquire first in their L1. Books heavy in physical action verbs provide more comprehensible input per page than books about feelings or abstract events. This does not mean avoiding all inner states -- "Mater exclamavit!" works perfectly because the illustration shows the exclamation -- but the balance should tilt toward the physical.

**5. Limited characters per page.** When too many characters appear simultaneously, the child cannot easily track who is doing what. Two to three characters per page is ideal. When a larger cast is needed (as in *The Enormous Turnip*), the cumulative structure solves the problem: each page focuses on the newest addition.

**6. Clear cause-and-effect chains.** "Blatta currit. Mater exclamavit. Mater patrem vocat. Pater venit." Each sentence causes the next. The child can follow the logic even if they miss individual words, and the logical chain makes each sentence more predictable (and therefore more comprehensible). Cause-and-effect is the narrative equivalent of scaffolding.

### Features That Do NOT Help Acquisition (or Hurt It)

- **Vocabulary lists, glossaries, or translations printed on the page.** These turn the book into a textbook and signal to the child that this is a "learning" activity. The glossary at the back of the book for the parent's reference is fine; anything that interrupts the reading experience is not.
- **Text that describes what the illustration already shows without adding anything.** "The dog is brown" under a picture of a brown dog provides no new information. The text should add to the picture, not merely label it. "Canis Puellam prehendit" adds the action -- who grabbed whom -- to the visual of the dog and the girl.
- **Clever wordplay, puns, or culturally specific humor** that only works if you already know the language. These are fine for L1 books but provide no acquisition value for L2 readers and may actually confuse.
- **Excessively long text per page.** Even if the Latin is simple, too many sentences on one page overwhelm the child's processing capacity and cause them to stop listening. One to three sentences per page is the range. If the story needs more text, add more pages.

## 7. Balancing New and Known Vocabulary

### The 70-80 / 20-30 Principle

As discussed in section 3, roughly 70-80% of content words in any given book should be previously encountered by the child (through earlier books, the father's daily speech, or repetition within the current book), and 20-30% should be new. This is not a rigid formula -- the illustration support means the child can handle a somewhat higher new-word density than a reader without pictures -- but it is a useful design target.

### Practical Strategies for Managing the Balance

**1. Categorize vocabulary by source.**
When planning a book, consider where the child might already know each word from:
- **Daily life Latin**: Words the father uses every day (pater, mater, veni, da mihi, aqua, edere, dormire, bonus, malus). These are "free" -- they cost no acquisition effort in the book.
- **Previous books**: Words that appeared in earlier books, especially books read frequently. These are mostly acquired but benefit from reinforcement.
- **New in this book**: Words being introduced for the first time. These are the ones that need illustration support and ideally within-book repetition.

**2. Front-load familiar vocabulary.**
The first few pages of a book should use heavily familiar vocabulary to establish the setting and characters. "Nox erat. Pater dormit. Aurelius dormit." -- the child understands everything, is settled into the story, and is ready to encounter something new. The new vocabulary ("blatta!") lands better when it arrives in an already-comprehensible context.

**3. Introduce new words with maximum illustration support.**
When a new word first appears, the illustration should make its meaning unmistakable. The first occurrence of "blatta" is accompanied by a large, central, unmissable cockroach in the illustration. Not a small cockroach in the corner of a busy scene.

**4. Repeat new words within the book.**
A word introduced on page 5 should recur on pages 7, 10, and 14 if possible. Each recurrence can be in a slightly different context, building the child's sense of the word's meaning range. In *The Enormous Turnip*, "trahit/trahunt" appears on pages 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, and 10 -- by the end, it is no longer a new word.

**5. Use word families, not isolated words.**
If a book introduces "trahit" (pulls), it can also include "trahunt" (they pull) naturally. The child begins to notice the pattern without being taught it. Similarly, "vocat" (calls) and "vocavit" (called) -- same root, different form. This is not grammar instruction; it is natural morphological exposure.

### What to Track Across the Collection

It is worth maintaining an informal sense of which vocabulary domains the collection covers:

- **Family/people**: pater, mater, avus, avia, puer, puella, infans, rex, pirata
- **Animals**: canis, feles, mus, blatta, leo, cuniculus
- **Body/physical**: manus, oculi, pedes, caput
- **Actions**: currit, dormit, videt, vocat, trahit, capit, celat, intrat, edit, bibit
- **Descriptors**: magnus, parvus, bonus, malus, laetus, territus, fessus
- **Spatial**: in, super, sub, ad, per, trans
- **Narrative**: subito, tum, ecce, iterum, tandem

Gaps in this coverage suggest topics for future books. A collection with many adventure books but no domestic or nature books will over-represent action verbs and under-represent everyday nouns, for example.

## 8. Emotional Engagement and Narrative in Language Retention

### Why Story Beats Drill

The research on this is unambiguous and converges from multiple fields. Emotional engagement enhances memory encoding (Cahill & McGaugh, 1995; Kensinger, 2009). Narrative structure provides a retrieval framework that isolated words lack (Mandler, 1984; Bruner, 1991). And intrinsic motivation -- the child *wants* to hear the story again -- provides the repeated exposure that acquisition requires (Krashen's "compelling input" refinement, 2011).

A child who asks for *Dada and the Cockroach* at bedtime every night for three weeks is receiving approximately 21 high-quality exposures to every Latin word in the book, in an emotionally engaged state, with low affective filter, and with the parent's interactive expansions layered on top. No flashcard system or vocabulary drill can compete with this. The story is the delivery mechanism, and it is vastly more efficient than any explicit instruction.

### What Makes a Story Emotionally Engaging for a 3-Year-Old

**1. A clear problem.** Something is wrong, something is wanted, something is threatened. The cockroach appears. The treasure is stolen. The turnip will not come out. The problem creates tension, and tension creates engagement.

**2. Escalation.** The problem gets worse before it gets better. More characters fail to pull the turnip. The moblins get closer. The cockroach runs toward the baby. Escalation sustains engagement across pages and provides natural repetition of key vocabulary (each escalation repeats the core problem in a new form).

**3. Physical humor.** A barrel flying through the air. A chain of six characters falling over when the turnip comes out. Exaggerated facial expressions. Physical comedy is universally engaging for young children and is easy to depict in illustrations.

**4. A satisfying resolution.** The turnip comes out. The cockroach is caught. The treasure is recovered. The resolution should feel earned (the character's effort produced the result) and should be emotionally clear in the illustration (triumph, relief, joy).

**5. Characters the child cares about.** Familiarity breeds attachment. Recurring characters (Lincus, the child's own family members as characters) have an emotional advantage over one-off characters. Characters who are small, brave, and slightly in over their heads (Link, the tiny mouse in *The Enormous Turnip*) naturally attract a young child's identification.

### The Emotional Arc as Acquisition Architecture

Here is the deeper point: **the emotional arc of the story IS the acquisition architecture.** The tension of the problem motivates the child to pay attention (attention is the prerequisite for acquisition). The escalation provides natural repetition (each iteration of the problem repeats key vocabulary). The resolution provides a satisfying payoff that makes the child want to hear the story again (repetition is the engine of acquisition).

This means that story quality and acquisition quality are not in tension. They are the same thing. A story that is emotionally flat is also acquisitionally weak, because the child does not pay attention to it and does not request rereading. A story that is emotionally gripping is also acquisitionally strong, because the child is attending to every word and will hear the book dozens of times.

The worst thing you can do is sacrifice story quality for "educational" features. A boring book with perfect vocabulary control is acquisitionally worthless because the child will not sit through it. A gripping book with imperfect vocabulary control is acquisitionally powerful because the child will demand it nightly.

## Summary: Design Checklist for Acquisition-Effective Picture Books

For each new book, check:

- [ ] **Text-image coupling**: Does every illustration precisely depict the action in the Latin text? Could a non-Latin-speaker follow the story through the pictures alone?
- [ ] **Sentence length**: Are most sentences 2-5 words, with none exceeding 8?
- [ ] **Vocabulary balance**: Are roughly 70-80% of content words familiar from daily life, previous books, or earlier in this book?
- [ ] **New vocabulary support**: Is every new word introduced with strong illustration support (visually prominent, central in the picture)?
- [ ] **Within-book repetition**: Is there at least one recurring phrase or refrain? Do new words recur at least 3 times within the book?
- [ ] **Emotional arc**: Is there a clear problem, escalation, and satisfying resolution?
- [ ] **Physical action**: Do most sentences describe visible, physical actions rather than abstract states?
- [ ] **Expansion opportunities**: Do the illustrations contain enough detail for the parent to label objects, ask questions, and extend the text conversationally?
- [ ] **Predictable structure**: Can the child anticipate what comes next (through pattern, refrain, or cumulative structure)?
- [ ] **Rereadability**: Is the story engaging enough that the child will request it repeatedly?

These are not rigid rules. They are tendencies to design toward. A book that hits 8 of 10 is doing well. A book that hits 4 of 10 may still work if the story is compelling enough. **Story quality is never sacrificed for pedagogical optimization** -- the story is the pedagogy.

## Appendix: How This Maps to Existing Books

A quick assessment of how the existing collection performs against these principles:

**Dada and the Cockroach (Fabula de Blatta)** -- Strong. Very short sentences (2-5 words). High text-image coupling. Clear emotional arc (calm -> shock -> crisis -> resolution). Heavy in familiar domestic vocabulary. Good parent-expansion opportunities (family members, nighttime routine). Could benefit from more within-book repetition (no refrain).

**The Enormous Turnip (Rapa Ingens)** -- Excellent on repetition. The cumulative structure provides massive within-book repetition of "trahunt et trahunt / NON POSSUNT." Predictable structure allows the child to anticipate each page. Clear escalation and satisfying resolution. The chain structure naturally limits characters per page to a manageable number while building to a large cast.

**Link and the Stolen Treasure (Lincus et Thesaurus Raptus)** -- Strong emotional engagement through adventure/suspense. Good physical action verbs. Benefits from the child's attachment to Link as a recurring character. Slightly higher vocabulary density than ideal for this level (more new words per page than the domestic stories). The adventure setting means more specialized vocabulary (arx, dolium, thesaurus) that may not recur in daily speech -- offset by the fact that the child will request rereading.

**Lion, Witch, and Wardrobe (Leo, Malefica et Armarium)** -- The most complex Latin in the collection. Sentence length pushes the upper limit. Rich vocabulary but heavy new-word load. Benefits from a universally compelling story. May function better as a "reach" book -- the child gets the gist from illustrations and picks up pieces across many rereadings, rather than comprehending most sentences on first hearing.

This range is healthy. A collection needs both "comfort zone" books (heavily familiar vocabulary, simple structure, easy comprehension) and "stretch" books (more new vocabulary, more complex sentences, requiring more rereading to fully acquire). The child should have both available and will self-select based on mood and current capacity.
