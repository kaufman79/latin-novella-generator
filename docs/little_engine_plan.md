# The Little Engine That Could: Latin Adaptation Plan

Research and planning document for adapting the 1930 Watty Piper / Lois Lenski edition as a Latin picture book.

---

## 1. Research: The 1930 Edition

### Publication History

The story's lineage:
- **1902**: The phrase "I think I can" first appears in print in a Swedish journal.
- **1906**: Charles S. Wing publishes "Story of the Engine that Thought It Could" in the *New-York Tribune* — a sermon illustration with one engine and a steep grade, no toys, no children.
- **1920**: A version titled "The Little Engine That Could" appears in the *My Bookhouse* anthology series, edited by Olive Beaupré Miller.
- **1930**: Arnold Munk, owner of Platt & Munk publishing, publishes the definitive version under the house pseudonym **"Watty Piper"**, with the title page reading "Retold by Watty Piper from *The Pony Engine* by Mabel C. Bragg." Munk hires **Lois Lenski** to illustrate. This is the version everyone knows.

### Copyright Status

**Public domain as of January 1, 2026.** Under US copyright law, works published in 1930 with proper notice entered the public domain 95 years after publication (1930 + 95 = 2025, entering PD on January 1, 2026). This applies to both the text and Lenski's illustrations.

Confirmed by Duke Law's Public Domain Day 2026 list and Internet Archive's public domain celebration, both of which explicitly name this title.

### The 1930 Story Text (Reconstructed from Sources)

The 1930 Piper version structures the story as follows:

**Opening**: "Chug, chug, chug. Puff, puff, puff. Ding-dong, ding-dong." A little train carries wonderful things for children on the other side of the mountain: toy animals, dolls (blue eyes and yellow hair, brown eyes and brown hair), spinning tops, jack-knives, puzzles, picture books, and good food — big round oranges, fat red apples, long yellow bananas, fresh cold milk, and lollipops. The train puffs along happily.

**The Breakdown**: The little red engine stops with a jerk and cannot go another inch. "What were all those good little boys and girls on the other side of the mountain going to do?" The toys and food need help.

**Engine 1 — The Shiny New Engine**: A beautiful passenger engine passes by. The toys plead for help. The Shiny New Engine refuses: "I pull you? I pull the likes of you? Indeed not!" Too grand for toys and food.

**Engine 2 — The Big Strong Engine (Big Black Freight Engine)**: A powerful freight engine comes along. He has just pulled big machines that print books and newspapers for grown-ups. "I am a very important engine indeed." Too important for children's things.

**Engine 3 — The Rusty Old Engine (Kind Old Engine)**: A tired old engine approaches. "I can not. I can not. I can not." Too old and weary — "I must rest my weary wheels."

**Engine 4 — The Little Blue Engine**: Finally, a very little engine comes by — one that is used only for switching trains in the yard and has never been over the mountain. The toys ask for help. She says she is not very big, she has never been over the mountain. But the children need toys and food. "I think I can. I think I can. I think I can."

**The Climb**: She is hooked to the train. Slowly, slowly she begins to pull. Up the mountain — "I think I can — I think I can — I think I can — I think I can — I think I can — I think I can — I think I can — I think I can — I think I can." The refrain stretches across the page, growing more strained as she climbs.

**The Summit and Descent**: She reaches the top. Down the other side: "I thought I could. I thought I could. I thought I could. I thought I could. I thought I could. I thought I could." The children will have their toys and food.

### Illustration Research: Lois Lenski's 1930 Art

**Format**: The 1930 first edition is a large-format book (10.25" x 12.25"), 80 pages (unnumbered), bound in cloth with a picture inlay. Fourteen leaves (i.e., 28 printed page surfaces, though "80 pages" appears in some descriptions — possibly counting flyleaves and endpapers).

**Style**: Lenski's illustrations use flat primary colors with a simplified, slightly folk-art quality. The PRINT Magazine comparison with the later 1954 Hauman edition describes Lenski's work as having "the simplicity of the design, the flat primary colors" and a "nice additional touch of blue." The 1954 edition has "more illustrations — one on every page," implying the 1930 edition does NOT have one illustration per page.

**Color**: Mix of color plates and black-and-white illustrations. Includes a color glossy frontispiece (red train with brown front engine). Interior pages have both color and B&W art.

**Estimated illustration count**: Based on our notes in `public_domain_story_ideas.md` and cross-referencing with the book's physical description: **approximately 12-15 illustrations total**. The PRINT Magazine article explicitly states the 1954 edition has more illustrations (one per page) than the 1930 edition. This means Lenski's originals are NOT dense enough for our format (we need ~20-22 illustrations, one per page).

**Availability**: The full 1930 scan is on Internet Archive at `archive.org/details/little-engine-that-could-1930`, marked Public Domain Mark 1.0. Downloadable as PDF, EPUB, and individual page images (JP2). Free and unrestricted.

### Illustration Decision

**Recommendation: Generate new illustrations.** Reasons:

1. **Insufficient density.** Lenski provides ~12-15 illustrations for a 22-page book that needs ~22 illustrations. We would need to supplement with AI-generated art anyway, and mixed-media books look terrible.
2. **Style mismatch.** Lenski's 1930 art, while beautiful, has a distinctly period aesthetic (flat, simplified, somewhat stiff) that differs from our existing cel-shaded / digital illustration style. Consistency matters.
3. **Resolution concerns.** The Internet Archive scan, while usable, is a scan of a ~95-year-old book. Image quality may not match our output standards.
4. **Opportunity.** Generating allows us to design engines with maximum expressiveness for a toddler audience — big eyes, clear emotions, visual personality that differs between engines. This serves the virtue arc: we need the child to *see* pride in the Shiny Engine, exhaustion in the Old Engine, and hope in the Little Blue Engine.

**The Lenski illustrations remain valuable as style reference** for the art director — they establish the "look" of this story in cultural memory.

## 2. Virtue Analysis: Why This Is the Definitive Spes Story

### The Thomistic Structure of Spes (from `theological_virtues_deep_dive.md`)

Aquinas's four conditions of spes (IIa-IIae, q. 17, a. 1):
1. Directed toward a **good** — delivering toys and food to children
2. The good is **future** — the children are on the other side of the mountain
3. The good is **arduous/difficult** — the mountain is steep, the train is heavy, the engine is small
4. The good is **possible** — the mountain can be crossed; other engines have done it

The Little Blue Engine hits all four conditions perfectly. This is not accidental — the story is structurally a parable of hope.

### The Two Sins Against Hope — Embodied by the Other Engines

Pieper identifies **desperatio** (despair) and **praesumptio** (presumption) as the two sins against hope. The story gives us both:

- **The Shiny New Engine** = **Praesumptio** (a form of it). Not presumption about the task, but the inverse: he is so secure in his own status that the children's need doesn't register. "I pull the likes of you?" He has no need to hope because he has no concern for the good in question. He is *above* it.
- **The Big Strong Engine** = **Praesumptio** (another form). His self-importance makes the children's good invisible. "I am a very important engine indeed." The good that requires hope is beneath his notice.
- **The Rusty Old Engine** = **Desperatio**. "I can not. I can not." He has given up. He may once have been capable, but he sees only impossibility now. This is despair: the denial that the good can be attained.
- **The Little Blue Engine** = **Spes**. She is small. She has never crossed the mountain. The task is genuinely beyond her proven capacity. But she sees the children's need (the good is real), she acknowledges the difficulty (she doesn't pretend it will be easy), and she commits to movement: "I think I can." This is Pieper's "confident, patient, not-yet" — the sustained movement toward a difficult good.

### The Refrain as the Sound of Hope

"I think I can" is the perfect verbal embodiment of spes. It is NOT "I know I can" (that's presumption — denying the difficulty). It is NOT "I can't" (despair). It is the middle ground: *I believe this is possible, even though it is hard, and I will keep moving.* The shift to "I thought I could" on the descent is the vindication of hope — the moment where the "not yet" becomes "now."

### Virtue Ratings

| Virtue | Rating | Justification |
|--------|:------:|---------------|
| **Spes** | **5** | The entire story is built around hope. All four Thomistic conditions are present. The difficulty is sustained (the whole climb). Despair is a genuine alternative (the Rusty Old Engine models it). The resolution vindicates hope as a way of being. |
| **Fortitudo** | **3** | Endurance through the climb is real — the Little Engine strains, struggles, keeps going. But the emotional center is the *hope* (pursuing the future good), not the *endurance* (bearing present difficulty). Fortitudo is the backbone; spes is the heart. |
| **Caritas** | **2** | The Little Engine helps because children need toys and food. This is genuine benevolence — she acts for others' good. But caritas is not tested or costly in a deep way; it's more a motivation than the emotional center. |

| **Fides** | 1 | The toys trust the Little Engine, but trust is not tested or central. |

## 3. Story Outline (22 Pages)

### Latin Refrain

**"Possum, puto! Possum, puto!"** — "I can, I think! I can, I think!"

On the descent: **"Potui! Potui! Putavi! Putavi!"** — "I could! I could! I thought so! I thought so!"

(Note: *puto* in the sense of "I reckon, I think" is natural colloquial Latin — exactly the register we want. The Latin Scribe should verify macrons: *putō*, *potuī*, *putāvī*.)

| **Pupae et Cibus** (Dolls and Food) | Anthropomorphic toys: a rag doll, a teddy bear, a toy clown, a jack-in-the-box. Plus crates of oranges, apples, bananas, bottles of milk. | The cargo — they plead for help |

| **Machina Magna Nigra** (Big Black Engine) | Enormous, powerful black freight engine. Massive wheels, thick smoke, covered in soot. Looks strong but self-important. | Refuses — too important (praesumptio) |
| **Machina Vetusta** (Old Rusty Engine) | Small, rusty, dented engine with drooping features. Faded red paint, cracked smokestack, weary eyes. | Refuses — too tired (desperatio) |
| **Parva Machina Caerulea** (Little Blue Engine) | Very small, bright blue engine with large, earnest eyes. Short, round, with a tiny smokestack. Not impressive to look at, but determined. Female (for Latin grammar — *parva*, *caerulea*). | The hero — chooses hope |

## 4. Story Evaluation: Five Questions

### 1. Would this story be interesting if the child already knew the "lesson"?

**Yes.** The story works as a railroad adventure even without the virtue dimension. Kids love trains. Kids love the suspense of "will she make it?" Kids love the repetitive refrain ("Possum, puto!" is extremely fun to say). The mounting refusals create narrative tension through pattern-and-break. The physical climb is dramatic. The story entertains independently of its moral content.

### 2. Does the character WANT something and face a genuine obstacle?

**Yes.** The Little Blue Engine wants to get the toys and food to the children (the good). The obstacle is genuine and multi-layered: the mountain is steep, the train is heavy, she is small, she has never done this before. The difficulty is not token — four pages are spent on the climb, and page 18 is explicitly the moment where she is exhausted and could stop.

### 3. Is emotion shown, not told?

**Yes.** The Shiny Engine's disdain is shown through her sneering face and dismissive rush. The Old Engine's despair is shown through his sagging frame and creaking retreat. The Little Blue Engine's hope is shown through the physical act of climbing — straining wheels, thinning steam, eyes focused forward. We never say "she was hopeful" — we show her *doing hope*, which is pulling when it's hard. The refrain itself ("Possum, puto!") is the sound of hope as action, not feeling.

### 4. Does the resolution come from the character's own choice?

**Yes.** No one forces the Little Blue Engine. No one even asks her specifically — she could have passed like the others. The decisive moment is page 15: "But the children need their toys. 'I think I can,' she said." This is a free choice, made by a character who honestly acknowledges the difficulty ("I am not big. I have never been over the mountain.") and chooses to try anyway. The resolution flows directly from this choice.

### 5. Is there one clear emotional through-line?

**Yes.** State the arc in one sentence: *A small engine who has never crossed the mountain chooses to try because children need help, and her sustained effort carries her over.* The emotional through-line is the movement from "this seems impossible" to "I will try" to "it is so hard" to "I did it." That is the arc of spes — the confident, patient "not yet" of the person on the way.

## 5. Virtue Rubric Check

From the spes rating table in `theological_virtues_deep_dive.md`:

> **Rating 5**: The entire story is a study in hope. The difficulty is sustained and real. Despair is a genuine temptation (not dismissed easily). The character's hope costs them something. The resolution vindicates hope itself as a way of being.

Checklist:
- [x] **The entire story is a study in hope.** The story exists to show what hope looks like in action. Remove spes and there is no story.
- [x] **The difficulty is sustained and real.** Four pages of climbing (pp. 16-19). The engine is exhausted on p. 18. The mountain is physically imposing throughout.
- [x] **Despair is a genuine temptation.** The Old Engine (p. 11-12) embodies despair explicitly: "I cannot. I cannot." After three refusals, giving up is the reasonable response. Page 18 is the internal temptation to despair.
- [x] **The character's hope costs them something.** Physical exhaustion, strain, the real risk of failure. She has never done this before.
- [x] **The resolution vindicates hope itself as a way of being.** "Potui" — the quiet final word. Not "I was strong" or "I was smart" but "I could." Hope was right.

**Confirmed: Spes 5.**

## 6. Production Notes

### Image Generation Strategy

Generate all illustrations using `gemini-3.1-flash-image-preview`. Reasons:
- Lenski illustrations insufficient in number (~12-15 vs. 22 needed)
- Opportunity for maximum expressiveness on engine faces (critical for virtue arc)
- Consistent style across all pages
- No copyright entanglements with later editions (1954 Hauman illustrations are under separate copyright)

**Style direction**: Bright, warm, cel-shaded children's book illustration. Anthropomorphic trains with expressive faces (think Thomas the Tank Engine meets Miyazaki — round, friendly, but capable of showing real emotion). Primary colors. The mountain should feel genuinely imposing but not scary.

**Key visual challenges**:
- Each engine needs a distinct visual personality that communicates its character at a glance
- The Little Blue Engine must look small and unimpressive but sympathetic — big eyes, round body
- The climb sequence (pp. 16-19) must show progressive difficulty without becoming visually monotonous — vary the angle, the altitude, the background, the engine's expression
- The summit (p. 19) must be a visual payoff — wide sky, golden light, the "opening up" feeling

### Latin Notes for the Scribe

- **Refrain**: "Possum, puto! Possum, puto!" should be verified for macrons. Expected: *Possum, putō! Possum, putō!* (present tense). Past: *Potuī! Potuī! Putāvī! Putāvī!*
- **Engine genders**: Machina is feminine in Latin, which works perfectly — the Little Blue Engine can be *she* naturally.
- **Onomatopoeia**: "Chug, chug, chug" could stay as sound effects or be Latinized. Options for the Scribe to consider.
- **Sentence target**: 3-7 words per sentence in Latin. The English outline runs 5-10 words per page, which should translate well.
- **Repetition**: The refusal pattern ("Please help us!" / excuse / departure) repeats three times. The Latin should use identical phrasing for the plea each time, with only the excuse varying.

### Page Count

22 pages. This is at the top of our usual range but justified by the story structure:
- 4 pages setup (cargo is important — the child needs to see what's at stake)
- 2 pages problem
- 6 pages refusals (three engines x 2 pages each — need both the plea and the refusal)
- 3 pages turning point (the choice must breathe)
- 4 pages climb (the difficulty must be sustained, not rushed)
- 3 pages resolution

Could be trimmed to 20 by condensing each refusal to 1 page, but this would sacrifice the emotional rhythm. The two-page structure (plea, then refusal) gives each encounter its own beat and lets the illustrations show both the toys' hope and the engine's character.

### Estimated Cost

At ~$0.045/image (Gemini, 512px): 22 images x $0.045 = ~$1.00. Negligible.

## 7. Open Questions for User Review

1. **Illustration approach confirmed?** Generate all new (recommended) vs. attempt to use Lenski originals (not recommended). The Internet Archive scan is available if we want to reference Lenski's designs during art direction.

2. **22 pages OK?** Could trim to 20 by compressing refusals. But the three distinct refusals (pride, self-importance, despair) are structurally important for the virtue arc.

3. **Latin refrain**: "Possum, puto!" — does this land right? Alternatives the Scribe could consider:
   - "Possum, credo!" (I can, I believe!) — but *credo* has heavier theological weight
   - "Possum, opinor!" (I can, I reckon!) — more colloquial but less punchy
   - "Possum, puto!" feels best: colloquial, rhythmic, captures the "I think" meaning precisely

4. **Engine names in Latin**: The outline uses descriptive names (Machina Nova Splendida, etc.). The Scribe could simplify if needed. The key is that the child sees visual differences in the illustrations, not that the names are memorable.

5. **Cargo specifics**: The 1930 text lists many items (dolls, bears, oranges, apples, bananas, milk, lollipops). For our simplified version, I've kept "dolls and bears, oranges and apples and bananas, and cold sweet milk." The Scribe may want to simplify further for Latin sentence length.
