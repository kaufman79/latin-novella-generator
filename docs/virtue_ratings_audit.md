## Justifications (Non-Zero Ratings Only)

### Dada and the Cockroach

| Virtue | Rating | Justification |
|--------|:------:|---------------|
| Fortitudo | 1 | Father faces a mild nuisance (cockroach) and deals with it, but there is no real danger, fear, or endurance. |
| Caritas | 1 | Father helps mother when called -- a small act of domestic service, but routine and not costly. |

### Augustine Steals the Pears

| Virtue | Rating | Justification |
|--------|:------:|---------------|
| Prudentia | 1 | Conscience whispers "don't steal" -- a moment of moral perception that is present but ignored and incidental. |
| Iustitia | 3 | Stealing violates the owner's rights; the entire moral arc rests on recognizing that something was unjustly taken, making justice a significant theme. |
| Temperantia | 5 | The whole story is the temperantia arc: disordered desire -> yielding to impulse -> the stolen fruit isn't even good (desire was hollow) -> emptiness -> remorse -> recognition that the appetite itself was wrong. |
| Fides | 1 | The closing prayer is a brief act of turning to God in trust, but it is not structurally central. |
| Caritas | 1 | God's forgiveness at the end is a minor presence of mercy, but Augustine himself does not exercise caritas. |

### Locusts and Dragon

| Virtue | Rating | Justification |
|--------|:------:|---------------|
| Prudentia | 2 | Antonius devises the plan to buzz together after brute force fails -- a real consilium moment that affects the outcome, but not the emotional center. |
| Iustitia | 1 | The gold is shared at the end ("inter se diviserunt"), a minor distributive justice beat. |
| Fortitudo | 3 | The cicadas enter a dark cave, fight a dragon, many die, survivors regroup -- genuine danger and real losses, but the emphasis is on attack (aggredi) not endurance (sustinere), and the solution comes quickly. |
| Spes | 1 | After many cicadas die, the survivors regroup and try again, but the "dark moment" is brief and quickly resolved. |
| Caritas | 1 | The cicadas help the townspeople spontaneously, but the act is adventurous rather than self-sacrificial; the cost is incidental, not chosen. |

### Lion, Witch and Wardrobe

| Virtue | Rating | Justification |
|--------|:------:|---------------|
| Prudentia | 1 | Lucy correctly perceives Narnia is real; Edmund fails to see through the Witch's deception. Present but incidental to the story's structure. |
| Iustitia | 4 | The central crisis is a justice problem: the Witch claims Edmund's life under the Deep Magic, and Aslan acknowledges the claim is legitimate. The resolution (Aslan's sacrifice) satisfies justice while transcending it; Edmund becomes "Edmund the Just." |
| Fortitudo | 3 | Peter fights bravely in battle, Aslan faces death with courage, Lucy and Susan keep vigil -- real acts of courage, but the emotional center is the sacrifice (caritas), not the endurance. |
| Temperantia | 3 | Edmund's yielding to Turkish Delight is a classic temperantia failure -- appetite overrides reason, leading directly to betrayal and the story's central crisis. Significant but not the resolution. |
| Fides | 3 | Lucy trusts Tumnus (and he proves faithful by letting her go); the Beavers trust in Aslan's return; the children must believe in Aslan before meeting him. Trust is a real theme but not the story's hinge point. |
| Spes | 3 | Narnia under "always winter, never Christmas" is a world without hope; Father Christmas's arrival and Aslan's return break through. Hope is atmospheric and significant but not character-driven as a decisive choice. |
| Caritas | 5 | Aslan willingly dies for Edmund -- someone who betrayed him -- at the ultimate cost. This is the paradigmatic act of self-giving love: willing the good of the other at cost to yourself. The story would not exist without this sacrifice. |

### Link and the Stolen Treasure

| Virtue | Rating | Justification |
|--------|:------:|---------------|
| Prudentia | 3 | On the second attempt, Link waits longer, uses barrel disguise, opens the chest quietly -- memoria, circumspectio, cautio in action. He learns from failure and adjusts. Significant but shares the stage with fortitudo. |
| Iustitia | 1 | Retrieving stolen treasure has a justice premise (restoring what was taken), but it is a background premise, not a developed theme. |
| Fortitudo | 5 | The emotional core: Link is afraid, remembers the Moblins, does not want to go back -- and chooses to go back. Fear is explicitly shown (shaking knees, sitting alone, thought-cloud of being grabbed). The bravery is in the second attempt, not the first -- he knows what awaits him. The rubric's exemplar: sustained danger, fear shown, endurance as the core. |
| Fides | 1 | Tetra's quiet encouragement ("You can try again") is a small relational trust moment, but there is no decision-to-trust arc. |
| Spes | 3 | After failure and the "dark moment" (sitting alone, afraid, the fortress far away), Link renews his commitment. The goal is still there; he chooses to pursue it. Genuine spes, but the emotional weight is on the courage, not the hope. |

### The Tale of Peter Rabbit

| Virtue | Rating | Justification |
|--------|:------:|---------------|
| Prudentia | 2 | Peter wisely avoids the white cat (a "moment of prudence at last") and eventually finds the gate, but the overall story is about disobedience, not discernment. |
| Iustitia | 2 | Natural justice structure: disobedience leads to consequences (lost jacket, illness, missed supper); obedience is rewarded (sisters get bread and milk). Fair outcomes but not character-exercised justice. |
| Fortitudo | 2 | Peter faces real danger (chased by McGregor, stuck in net) and struggles to escape, but the "courage" is reactive flight, not chosen endurance for a good cause. |
| Temperantia | 4 | The central conflict: Peter cannot resist the garden, eats too much, gets sick. Everything bad flows from his failure to govern his appetites. Mother's warning was about self-control. The story hinges on temperantia failure and its consequences. |
| Spes | 1 | Peter wants to get home and searches for the gate, but the pursuit is survival-driven, not a sustained hope toward a difficult good. |
| Caritas | 1 | Mother puts Peter to bed with tea despite his disobedience; sparrows encourage him. Minor acts of care, not central. |

### The Tale of Benjamin Bunny

| Virtue | Rating | Justification |
|--------|:------:|---------------|
| Prudentia | 2 | Peter is cautiously prudent in the garden (nervous, hears noise, says "we must go"); Benjamin is recklessly imprudent (eats lettuce, ignores danger). The contrast is visible but not the central theme. |
| Iustitia | 2 | Retrieving Peter's jacket is commutative justice (getting back what belongs to him); Mr. Bouncer's punishment of Benjamin is proportionate correction; Peter gives onions to his mother (gratitudo). Small justice moments that matter. |
| Fortitudo | 2 | Benjamin shows boldness entering the garden; the cat scene involves real fear and endurance (trapped under basket). But the endurance is passive (stuck, not choosing to endure), and Mr. Bouncer rescues them. |
| Temperantia | 1 | Benjamin eating lettuce in a dangerous garden shows mild lack of self-control (wrong place, wrong time to indulge), but it is incidental. |
| Fides | 2 | Benjamin's loyalty to Peter -- showing up when Peter is in trouble, leading the mission to recover his jacket -- is a real form of faithfulness. But there is no moment where trust is tested or chosen against an alternative. |
| Spes | 1 | They want to recover the jacket and do, but the difficulty is not sustained and the resolution comes from outside (Mr. Bouncer). |
| Caritas | 1 | Benjamin helps Peter, and Mr. Bouncer rescues them both -- minor acts of goodwill, not costly or central. |

### The Tale of Tom Kitten

| Virtue | Rating | Justification |
|--------|:------:|---------------|
| Iustitia | 1 | Mother's punishment of the kittens is a small act of correction (vindicatio), and the ducks taking the clothes creates a minor injustice. Incidental. |
| Temperantia | 3 | The kittens cannot keep their clothes clean -- immediate failure of self-control. Mother explicitly commands orderliness; the kittens fail at once. But there is no genuine choice or moral tension -- the kittens are simply undisciplined by nature, and they learn nothing (they play in bed at the end). Comedy of temperantia failure, not moral arc. |

### The Enormous Turnip

| Virtue | Rating | Justification |
|--------|:------:|---------------|
| Iustitia | 2 | Everyone who helped shares in the reward (soup); each contributes what they can. Distributive justice is present in the structure but not explored as a theme. |
| Fortitudo | 3 | Repeated pulling and failing, face-scrunching effort, not giving up -- persistence through physical struggle that visibly matters to the outcome. Brief but real. |
| Fides | 1 | The community encourages the mouse and trusts she can contribute, but there is no decision-to-trust moment against an alternative. |
| Spes | 2 | They face a setback (can't pull the turnip) and keep trying, the mouse doubts herself but tries anyway. The difficulty is real but brief. |
| Caritas | 3 | The community cooperates for a shared good, encourages the smallest member who doubts herself, and the final image (mouse sleeping in grandfather's palm) shows care for the least. Real caritas -- valuing and including the smallest -- but not deeply costly or transformative. |

### Link and the Thief's Lantern

| Virtue | Rating | Justification |
|--------|:------:|---------------|
| Prudentia | 4 | The story's pivot is Link pausing, looking at the Moblin, looking at the lanterns, and seeing reality clearly instead of acting on his preconceptions. Consilium, iudicium, imperium -- he perceives the truth (not a villain, but a scared child), judges correctly, and acts on that judgment by sitting down. The insight drives the resolution. |
| Iustitia | 4 | The story navigates competing claims: the town's lanterns vs. a lonely Moblin's need for light. Link's solution (take half back, leave half) is proportionate distributive justice -- rendering to each what is due. "The thief is gone" is veritas calibrated with mercy. |
| Fortitudo | 1 | Link follows the thief into the dark hillside, which takes some courage, but the expected danger evaporates when he finds a small Moblin. |
| Fides | 2 | Link's choice to sit down is partly an act of trust (trusting the Moblin won't attack); Grunio uncurls and trusts Link won't hit him. Reciprocal, tentative trust that supports but does not drive the arc. |
| Caritas | 4 | Link's final act -- leaving his OWN lantern by the cave -- is a pure act of self-giving love at cost to himself. Choosing to sit down instead of punishing is mercy to the undeserving. The entire second half is about compassion for a frightened, lonely creature. Central, but shares the stage with prudentia and iustitia. |

## Gap Analysis

### Strongest Virtues

**Iustitia (20) and Fortitudo (20)** are tied as the best-covered virtues. Fortitudo is carried heavily by Link and the Stolen Treasure (5) and Locusts and Dragon (3). Iustitia benefits from both Lion/Witch/Wardrobe (4) and Link and the Thief's Lantern (4), but no book is a pure iustitia story -- the coverage is broad rather than deep.

**Caritas (17)** is well-covered, anchored by Lion/Witch/Wardrobe (5) and Link and the Thief's Lantern (4). The collection has a genuine paradigm of self-sacrificial love (Aslan) and a strong mercy story (Link/Grunio).

### Weakest Virtues

**Fides (10)** is the weakest virtue in the collection. No book scores above 3, and only Lion/Witch/Wardrobe reaches that level. The collection has no story where the decision to trust -- entrusting yourself to another when you could choose not to, and having that trust tested -- is the central theme. This is the most critical gap.

**Spes (11)** is the second weakest. Link and the Stolen Treasure (3) and Lion/Witch/Wardrobe (3) provide the best hope content, but neither centers on spes as its primary virtue. The collection badly needs a story where perseverance toward a genuine, difficult good through a sustained "dark moment" is the emotional core -- not just an adventure obstacle but a genuine temptation to despair that is resisted.

**Prudentia (15)** is moderate but unevenly distributed. Link and the Thief's Lantern (4) is the only strong prudentia story. Most books score 0-2. The collection needs more stories where seeing reality clearly and thinking well is the virtue that matters.

### Specific Gaps and Recommendations

1. **Fides (critical gap)**: Need a story centered on a decision to trust -- a "faithful companion" story, a "keeping the promise" story, or a "learning to trust" story. Candidates: Hachiko/faithful dog, Penelope waiting for Odysseus, a Sam Gamgee-type companion story.

2. **Spes (critical gap)**: Need a story centered on sustained pursuit of a difficult good through genuine temptation to despair. Candidates: a "long journey home" story, a "planting and waiting" story, a "rebuilding after loss" story.

3. **Prudentia (moderate gap)**: Need more stories where insight and discernment drive the resolution. Candidates: an "Emperor's New Clothes" story (seeing through deception), a "listen to the wise elder" story.

4. **Temperantia (moderate)**: Well-served by Augustine (5) and Peter Rabbit (4). Could use one more story exploring a different dimension -- perhaps humilitas (under temperantia) or patience/waiting.

5. **Iustitia (moderate)**: Broadly covered but no single story is a pure iustitia story. A "wrongful accusation" or "fair sharing" story would deepen this.
