# The Cardinal Virtues: A Deep Dive for Story Creation

Research notes on prudentia, iustitia, fortitudo, and temperantia in the classical and Christian virtue tradition, with practical application to Latin children's book storytelling.

Sources: Thomas Aquinas, *Summa Theologiae* IIa-IIae (the "Secunda Secundae"), especially qq. 47-170; Josef Pieper, *The Four Cardinal Virtues* (1966); C.S. Lewis, *Mere Christianity* (1952) and *The Screwtape Letters* (1942); Aristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics*, especially Books II-IV and VI; Cicero, *De Inventione* and *De Officiis*; relevant scholastic commentary.

---

## Preliminary: The Cardinal Virtues as a System

### Why "Cardinal"?

The word comes from *cardo*, a hinge. The cardinal virtues are the hinges on which the whole moral life turns. Every other moral virtue is either a form of one of the four or attached to one of them as a "potential part" (a related virtue that shares some but not all of its character). Aquinas's architecture is systematic: all of IIa-IIae, qq. 47-170 is organized under the four cardinals and their associated parts. There is no moral virtue that falls outside this framework.

This is not a medieval invention. Plato identifies the four in the *Republic* (IV, 427e-435a): wisdom (*sophia*), courage (*andreia*), temperance (*sophrosyne*), and justice (*dikaiosyne*). Aristotle analyzes them extensively in the *Nicomachean Ethics*, though he does not formally group them as a tetrad. The Stoics adopted the fourfold scheme. Cicero transmitted it to the Latin tradition (*De Inventione* II.53; *De Officiis* I.5). Ambrose of Milan coined the term *virtutes cardinales*. Augustine adopted the scheme. Aquinas gave it its definitive philosophical elaboration.

### How the Four Relate: Prudentia as Architectonic

The four cardinal virtues are not a list of four equally independent traits. They have an internal order:

**Prudentia** stands apart from the other three. It is the virtue of the *intellect* (specifically the practical intellect), while iustitia, fortitudo, and temperantia are virtues of the *will* and the *appetites*. Prudentia's role is to *see correctly* what the right action is in a given situation. The other three virtues *execute* that right action in their respective domains:

- **Iustitia** executes it in relation to *other people* (giving them their due)
- **Fortitudo** executes it in the face of *danger and difficulty* (not being deterred)
- **Temperantia** executes it in the face of *pleasure and appetite* (not being seduced)

This is why prudentia is called the *auriga virtutum* -- the charioteer of the virtues. A charioteer does not pull the chariot; the horses do. But without the charioteer, the horses run wild. Prudentia does not itself produce just or brave or temperate actions; but without prudentia, the other virtues cannot find their proper object. A person who wants to be just but lacks prudentia will give the wrong things to the wrong people. A person who wants to be brave but lacks prudentia will charge into the wrong battles. Courage in a bad cause is not fortitudo but recklessness; generosity toward the wrong ends is not iustitia but foolishness.

Pieper (*The Four Cardinal Virtues*, "Prudence" ch. 1): "The meaning of the virtue of prudence is this: that the objective recognition of reality should determine our action; that the concrete ethical situation should be apprehended by a knowing attentive to reality." Prudentia is the virtue that sees things as they are, not as we wish them to be or fear them to be, and acts accordingly.

### The Unity of the Virtues

Aquinas holds (following Aristotle, *NE* VI.13, 1144b30-1145a2) that the cardinal virtues are *connected*: you cannot fully possess one without possessing the others. This is the *connexio virtutum* thesis (IIa-IIae, q. 65).

The argument runs like this: prudentia requires right appetite (you cannot see clearly what is just if your desires are disordered -- anger, greed, lust, and fear all distort judgment). So prudentia requires at least some iustitia, fortitudo, and temperantia. Conversely, iustitia, fortitudo, and temperantia all require prudentia to find their proper object. Therefore, a deficiency in any one virtue necessarily limits all the others.

This does not mean you must be perfectly virtuous in all four before you can be virtuous in any. Aquinas distinguishes between *perfect* virtue (which requires the connection) and *imperfect* or *inchoate* virtue (beginnings of virtue, which can exist in relative isolation). A child can have real but imperfect courage without yet having developed full justice or temperance. But the virtues naturally tend toward unity: growing in one creates pressure to grow in the others.

**For stories**: This means the most interesting virtue stories will often involve multiple virtues in tension or cooperation. A character who must be brave (fortitudo) also needs to know *when* to fight and when to flee (prudentia). A character who must be just (iustitia) also needs the courage to do what is right when it is costly (fortitudo) and the self-control not to be swayed by bribes or flattery (temperantia).

### The Cardinal and Theological Virtues Together

The cardinal virtues are *acquired* through practice and habit. They are natural human excellences, accessible to anyone through reason and effort. Aristotle knew nothing of the theological virtues but had a complete account of the cardinals.

The theological virtues (fides, spes, caritas) are *infused* -- in the Thomistic framework, they are gifts of grace directed toward God. But they do not replace the cardinal virtues; they perfect and elevate them. Aquinas: grace does not destroy nature but perfects it (*gratia non tollit naturam sed perficit*). A person with caritas still needs prudentia to know how to love wisely. A person with spes still needs fortitudo to endure the difficulty of hoping.

The relationship: the theological virtues give the cardinal virtues their ultimate direction (toward God); the cardinal virtues give the theological virtues their operational effectiveness (in the concrete details of daily life). Caritas without prudentia is sentimental and potentially harmful. Prudentia without caritas is cold calculation.

For children's stories set in the natural register, the cardinal virtues carry themselves without any need for explicit theological content. They are fully visible in any human life: the wise grandmother, the brave child, the fair leader, the self-controlled athlete. This is their strength as story material.

## I. Prudentia (Prudence / Practical Wisdom)

### What Aquinas Actually Says

**Summa Theologiae IIa-IIae, qq. 47-56**

Aquinas defines prudentia as *recta ratio agibilium* -- "right reason about things to be done" (q. 47, a. 2), drawing on Aristotle's *phronesis* (*NE* VI.5, 1140a25-28). This compact formula is the key to everything.

Break it down:
- **Recta** -- "right" or "correct." Not just any reasoning, but reasoning that gets it right. Prudentia is not cleverness; it is *correct* practical reasoning.
- **Ratio** -- "reason." This is an intellectual virtue, residing in the practical intellect. Prudentia involves thinking, not just feeling or wanting.
- **Agibilium** -- "of things to be done." This is what distinguishes prudentia from theoretical wisdom (*sapientia*) or scientific knowledge (*scientia*). Prudentia is about *action* -- what to do here, now, in this particular situation.

Aquinas's treatise is remarkably detailed. He identifies three acts of prudentia, each building on the last (q. 47, a. 8; developed in qq. 51-53):

1. **Consilium (deliberation)** -- Taking counsel, gathering information, considering the situation. Looking before you leap. "What is going on? What are my options? What are the likely consequences?" This is the input phase. Aquinas associates it with the virtue of *eubulia* (good deliberation) from Aristotle (*NE* VI.9).

2. **Iudicium (judgment)** -- Weighing the options and forming a correct assessment. "Given what I know, what is the right thing to do?" This is not just logical deduction; it requires *synesis* (good judgment in ordinary cases) and *gnome* (good judgment in exceptional cases where the usual rules don't quite apply). The person of prudentia can recognize when the standard response is wrong and the situation calls for something different.

3. **Imperium (command)** -- The decisive act: commanding the will to act on the judgment. This is the principal act of prudentia (q. 47, a. 8). Many people deliberate well and judge correctly but fail to act. Prudentia is not complete until it issues in action. A person who always knows the right thing to do but never does it does not have prudentia.

This three-act structure is essential: **prudentia is not just seeing what is right but actually doing it.** Deliberation and judgment without command is like a general who makes brilliant battle plans but never gives the order to advance.

### The Integral Parts of Prudentia

Aquinas lists eight "integral parts" of prudentia (q. 48) -- components that must all be present for prudentia to function well. These come from Macrobius and Cicero, refined by Aquinas:

1. **Memoria** -- Memory of past experience. You learn prudentia partly from what has happened before. A child who has been burned learns to be careful with fire. Without memoria, you repeat mistakes endlessly.

2. **Intellectus** -- Understanding of present circumstances. Not book-learning but *seeing what is in front of you*. The ability to read a situation accurately. Pieper: "the silent contemplation of reality."

3. **Docilitas** -- Teachableness. Willingness to learn from others, to take advice, to recognize that you don't know everything. Aquinas says this is necessary because no one has enough experience alone to judge every situation (q. 49, a. 3). The prudent person listens.

4. **Solertia** -- Quick-wittedness, the ability to assess a new situation rapidly when there is no time for extended deliberation. Not impulsiveness -- *accurate* rapid assessment.

5. **Ratio** -- The ability to reason from what you know to what you don't. Drawing correct inferences.

6. **Providentia** -- Foresight. The ability to see where present actions lead. This is the component closest to the English word "providence" -- and it is the *principal* integral part, giving prudentia its very name (*prudentia* is a contraction of *providentia*, q. 49, a. 6).

7. **Circumspectio** -- Attention to circumstances. The same action can be right in one situation and wrong in another. The prudent person notices the relevant differences.

8. **Cautio** -- Caution in the sense of being alert to obstacles and dangers that might derail a good plan. Not timidity but awareness that good intentions can go wrong.

### Pieper on Prudentia

Josef Pieper's treatment in *The Four Cardinal Virtues* ("Prudence") is the indispensable modern commentary. His central argument:

**Prudentia is not "being careful."** The English word "prudent" has degenerated into meaning "cautious, risk-averse, timid." This is almost the opposite of what Aquinas means. Aquinas's prudentia is *decisive*. It culminates in *imperium* -- command. The prudent person acts. Pieper: "Prudence is the mold and mother of all virtues, the circumspect and resolute shaping power of our minds which transforms knowledge of reality into realization of the good."

**Prudentia is fundamentally about seeing reality clearly.** The first requirement of prudentia is not cleverness or strategy but *objectivity* -- seeing what is actually there, not what you want to be there or what you're afraid is there. Pieper calls this the *cognoscitive* character of prudentia. Before you can do the right thing, you must see the true thing. Self-deception, wishful thinking, willful ignorance, and ideology are all enemies of prudentia because they distort the perception of reality on which right action depends.

**Prudentia is not mere cleverness (solertia).** Cleverness is the ability to find effective means to any end. A clever thief is not prudent. Prudentia is ordered to the *good* -- it finds effective means to genuinely good ends. Aquinas makes this distinction explicitly (q. 47, a. 13): cleverness (*astutia*) directed to bad ends is not prudentia but a counterfeit.

**The relationship between prudentia and moral goodness is circular but not viciously so.** You need right desire to see clearly (disordered appetites distort judgment), and you need clear sight to desire rightly (you must know what is genuinely good). The circle is entered through practice, habituation, and the guidance of others (docilitas). A child begins with imperfect prudentia, guided by parents and teachers, and gradually develops the capacity for independent moral judgment. This is moral education, and it is exactly what these stories participate in.

### What Prudentia Actually Means (Not the English Shorthand)

Prudentia is **the ability to perceive what the situation really is and to determine the right response to it, followed by the decisive will to act on that determination.** It involves:

- **Seeing reality clearly.** Not projecting your wishes or fears onto the situation. A character with prudentia notices the clue that others miss, reads the stranger's face correctly, understands why the river is rising.
- **Drawing on experience and counsel.** Prudentia is not impulsive. The prudent person remembers what happened before, listens to those who know more, considers the circumstances.
- **Judging correctly.** Not just gathering information but *weighing* it rightly. Knowing which facts matter, which risks are real, which opportunities are genuine.
- **Acting decisively.** Prudentia does not paralyze. It sees, judges, and commands. The prudent person does not endlessly deliberate; at the right moment, they act.
- **Adapting to particulars.** Prudentia is always about *this* situation, *this* person, *this* moment. It cannot be reduced to rules. The prudent person knows when the rule applies and when it doesn't.

### What Prudentia Is NOT

- **Not caution or timidity.** "The prudent course" in modern English usually means the safe, risk-averse option. Aquinas's prudentia might command bold action. A prudent general sometimes orders a daring attack. A prudent person sometimes takes a great risk -- because they have correctly judged that the risk is worth taking.
- **Not cleverness or cunning.** Cleverness finds effective means to any end; prudentia finds the right means to genuinely good ends. Odysseus is clever; whether he is prudent depends on whether his ends are good. A villain can be extremely clever but cannot be prudent.
- **Not mere knowledge or expertise.** A person can have vast knowledge and still lack prudentia. Knowledge is about universals; prudentia is about this particular situation. A doctor who knows all of medicine but cannot diagnose *this* patient lacks practical wisdom.
- **Not following rules.** Rules are generalizations; prudentia deals with particulars. The prudent person often acts according to rules, but *gnome* -- the ability to recognize when the standard rule doesn't apply -- is a genuine part of prudentia. Blind rule-following is not prudentia but rigidity.
- **Not indecisiveness.** "I need more information" is sometimes prudent, but chronic indecisiveness is a *failure* of prudentia. Prudentia culminates in imperium -- command. Analysis paralysis is the opposite of prudentia.
- **Not manipulation.** Using your understanding of people and situations to control or exploit them is not prudentia but *astutia* (craftiness), which Aquinas lists as a vice opposed to prudentia (q. 55, a. 3-5).

### C.S. Lewis on Prudentia

Lewis in *Mere Christianity* III.2: "Prudence means practical common sense, taking the trouble to think out what you are doing and what is likely to come of it." He immediately adds: "Nowadays most people hardly think of Prudence as one of the 'virtues.' In fact, because Christ said we could only get into His world by being like children, many Christians have the idea that, provided you are 'good,' it does not matter being a fool. This is a misunderstanding."

Lewis's point: goodness without intelligence is dangerous. God "wants a child's heart, but a grown-up's head." Christians are not called to be stupid. Prudentia is a virtue, not a worldly compromise. The idea that the virtuous person is a naive innocent is a modern sentimentality that the classical tradition firmly rejects.

In *The Screwtape Letters*, Letter 1: Screwtape advises Wormwood to keep the patient from thinking clearly -- because clear thinking leads to virtue. The enemy of prudentia is not wickedness but *muddledness*: vague feelings substituted for clear thought, slogans substituted for analysis, conformity substituted for judgment. This is directly relevant to story construction: a prudentia story shows a character *thinking clearly* when everyone around them is confused, distracted, or deceived.

### How Prudentia Appears in a Children's Story

The natural analogue of prudentia shows up whenever a character:

- **Stops to think when others rush ahead.** The character who pauses at the fork in the road, looks around, remembers something, and chooses the right path.
- **Notices something others miss.** A clue, a warning sign, a change in weather, a false note in someone's words. The "paying attention" moment.
- **Listens to wise counsel.** A character asks an elder, a guide, a friend -- and acts on the advice. Docilitas in action.
- **Resists a deceptive temptation.** Not through raw willpower (that's temperantia) but through *seeing through* the deception. The character recognizes the trick, the false promise, the hidden danger.
- **Makes a plan and executes it.** Not an elaborate heist-movie plan, but a simple, correct plan: "If we go this way, we can avoid the wolf." Consilium, iudicium, imperium.
- **Changes course when the situation changes.** Rigidly following a plan when conditions have changed is the opposite of prudentia. The character who says "wait -- the bridge is out, we need a new plan" is exercising circumspectio.
- **Learns from a mistake.** Memoria: a character who tried something that failed and does not try the same thing again, but adjusts.

**The emotional through-line of a prudentia story**: confusion/uncertainty --> attention to reality --> insight/understanding --> decisive action --> right outcome. The key moment is the **insight** -- the point where the character sees what is really going on, often after a period of confusion or deception.

**Concrete visible actions** (for illustration):
- A character kneeling to examine tracks, a broken branch, a mark on the ground
- A character holding up a hand ("wait") while others rush forward
- A character looking carefully at a map, a fork in the path, or the sky before a storm
- A character listening intently to an elder or a guide (leaning in, ear turned)
- A character's face showing the "aha" moment -- eyes widening, mouth opening
- A character pointing out something small that others overlooked (a crack, a hidden door, a detail)
- A character choosing between two doors, two paths, two objects -- with deliberation visible in the pose

### Rating Prudentia 0-5 in a Story

| Rating | Description | Example |
|:------:|-------------|---------|
| **0** | Practical wisdom, discernment, or clear-headedness plays no role in the story. | A story driven entirely by emotion, impulse, or luck. |
| **1** | A character makes a sensible choice, but it is incidental to the story's structure. | A character takes an umbrella because it looks like rain. The story is about something else. |
| **2** | A character's judgment or perceptiveness matters to the plot but is not the central driver. | A character notices a danger and avoids it, but the main conflict is about courage or friendship. |
| **3** | Practical wisdom is a significant theme. A character must think through a real problem, and their discernment visibly affects the outcome. But the story also relies on other virtues. | A character must figure out which path is safe, and this judgment is important -- but the climax depends on bravery, not wisdom. Think: a detective story where the detection sets up a courage-driven climax. |
| **4** | The story's central conflict is resolved by an act of practical wisdom. The character sees what others do not, judges correctly under pressure, and acts on that judgment. The insight is the turning point. | A character surrounded by confusion or deception sees through to the truth, and that perception drives the resolution. Think: the child in "The Emperor's New Clothes" -- seeing reality when everyone else is deceived. |
| **5** | The entire story is built around prudentia. The character faces a genuinely complex or ambiguous situation, must gather information, weigh competing considerations, resist false certainties, and act on imperfect knowledge. Every step requires reading the landscape, remembering past experience, listening to conflicting advice from other animals, and making decisions with real consequences. ## II. Iustitia (Justice)

### What Aquinas Actually Says

**Summa Theologiae IIa-IIae, qq. 57-122**

Aquinas's treatise on iustitia is the longest in the entire *Summa Theologiae* -- 66 questions, dwarfing every other virtue. This is because iustitia governs the entire domain of human relations. Every duty, every right, every debt between persons falls under iustitia or one of its parts.

Aquinas adopts Ulpian's classic definition (Digest 1.1.10): *Iustitia est constans et perpetua voluntas ius suum cuique tribuendi* -- "Justice is the constant and perpetual will to render to each one his right" (q. 58, a. 1). Every word matters:

- **Constans et perpetua voluntas** -- "constant and perpetual will." Iustitia is not a single act but a stable disposition. The just person does not render another's due once and then forget; they have a settled character of doing so. This is what makes it a virtue and not a lucky accident.
- **Ius suum cuique** -- "to each one his right." The central concept is *ius* -- right, what is due, what belongs to someone. Iustitia presupposes that people have genuine claims on each other. This is not charity (giving from generosity) but justice (giving what is *owed*).
- **Tribuendi** -- "rendering" or "giving." Iustitia is active: it does something. It is not merely refraining from harm but positively giving what is due.

### Ius: The Concept of Right

The foundation of iustitia is *ius* (q. 57). This is not "law" in the modern positive-law sense but something deeper: the objective rightness of a relationship. When Aquinas says a person has a *ius* to something, he means there is an objective order (natural, not merely conventional) that determines what belongs to whom.

Aquinas distinguishes (q. 57, a. 2-3):
- **Ius naturale** -- Natural right. What is due to persons by their nature. A parent owes care to a child not because of a contract but by the nature of the relationship.
- **Ius positivum** -- Positive (established) right. What is due because of human agreement or law. Property rights, contracts, civil law.

The critical insight: iustitia is **relational**. It always involves *at least two persons* (q. 58, a. 2). You cannot exercise iustitia toward yourself; you exercise it toward *another* (*ad alterum*). This is what distinguishes iustitia from fortitudo and temperantia, which primarily govern the agent's own internal states. Iustitia faces outward.

### The Species of Iustitia

Aquinas (following Aristotle, *NE* V) identifies three species of justice (q. 61):

1. **Iustitia commutativa** (commutative/exchange justice) -- Justice between individuals. Governs exchanges, contracts, promises, and injuries between persons. If I borrow your tools, commutative justice requires me to return them. If I damage your property, commutative justice requires me to compensate you. The standard is *strict equality*: what I give back should equal what I took or received. This is the simplest form and the easiest to illustrate: fairness in one-to-one dealings.

2. **Iustitia distributiva** (distributive justice) -- Justice from the community to its members. How goods, honors, and burdens should be distributed within a group. The standard is *proportional equality*: not that everyone gets the same, but that each gets according to their contribution, need, or merit (the precise criterion is debated, but the principle of *proportionate* rather than *arithmetic* equality is key). A parent distributing chores among children of different ages exercises distributive justice: the older child gets harder tasks, not because the younger child is favored but because the distribution should be proportionate.

3. **Iustitia legalis** (legal/general justice) -- Justice from individuals to the community. What individuals owe to the common good. This is the justice of the citizen who pays taxes, obeys just laws, and contributes to the community's welfare -- not from generosity but from genuine obligation. Aquinas treats this as the most "general" form because the common good encompasses all particular goods.

### The Parts of Iustitia (Associated Virtues)

Aquinas attaches a remarkable number of virtues to iustitia as "potential parts" -- virtues that share the character of iustitia (rendering what is due to another) but in domains where the debt cannot be exactly measured or fully repaid. The most important for stories:

**Pietas** (qq. 101-102) -- Duty to parents and homeland. What you owe to those who gave you life, upbringing, and community. Pietas cannot be fully repaid (you can never fully repay your parents for giving you life), but the debt is real. This is not mere sentiment but an obligation of justice. A child who cares for aging parents exercises pietas. A citizen who serves the common good exercises pietas toward the *patria*.

**Religio** (qq. 81-100) -- Duty to God (in the natural register: duty to the source of one's being). The debt of the creature to the creator -- again, a debt that can never be fully repaid.

**Observantia** (q. 102) -- Respect and honor owed to those in positions of legitimate authority (teachers, elders, leaders). Not servility but recognition of genuine excellence or responsibility.

**Gratitudo** (qq. 106-107) -- Gratitude. What you owe to someone who has done you a favor. The debt of gratitude is real but cannot be exactly measured. Ingratitude is a genuine violation of iustitia.

**Veritas** (qq. 109-110) -- Truthfulness. Aquinas places truthfulness under iustitia because you owe the truth to others. Lying is unjust because it takes from the other person something they have a right to: an accurate representation of reality. This is a powerful insight: honesty is not just a personal virtue but a matter of justice.

**Amicitia / Affabilitas** (q. 114) -- Friendliness. The ordinary pleasantness and courtesy owed in social interaction. You owe other people a certain basic decency in how you treat them -- not from charity but from justice.

**Vindicatio** (q. 108) -- Vindication / right punishment. The just response to wrongdoing. Not revenge (which is disproportionate and self-serving) but proportionate correction aimed at restoring order. This is the justice of the parent who disciplines appropriately: not from anger but from the duty to correct.

### Pieper on Iustitia

Pieper's treatment in *The Four Cardinal Virtues* ("Justice") emphasizes several points:

**Iustitia presupposes the reality of the other person.** The foundation of justice is that other people are *real* -- they have genuine rights, genuine claims, genuine needs that exist independently of my wishes. Injustice begins with treating other persons as instruments, obstacles, or extensions of oneself. Pieper: "Justice is distinguished from the other cardinal virtues by the fact that in it the 'bonum' (good) is objective to a degree not found in the other virtues... The just man is just not because of any subjective state of mind but because he gives to another what belongs to that other."

**Iustitia is the minimum of love.** Justice is not the highest virtue (caritas is), but it is the necessary foundation. You cannot love someone justly if you are treating them unjustly. Caritas without iustitia is sentimental self-congratulation. A person who gives generous gifts to their neighbor but cheats their employees is not exercising caritas; they are violating iustitia. Pieper: "The just man does not 'love' his creditor -- he pays him."

**Iustitia requires knowing what is owed.** This is where iustitia connects back to prudentia. You cannot render what is due if you don't know what is due. Many injustices arise not from malice but from ignorance or self-deception about what we owe. The person who genuinely does not see their own privilege or the burden they impose on others is unjust through blindness, not cruelty.

### What Iustitia Actually Means (Not the English Shorthand)

Iustitia is **the settled disposition to recognize and render to each person what genuinely belongs to them -- their rights, their due, their fair share -- in every relationship and interaction.** It involves:

- **Recognition of the other as real.** The just person treats other people as genuine subjects with their own claims, not as objects to be used. This is the most fundamental act of iustitia.
- **Knowledge of what is owed.** Not just good intentions but accurate understanding of rights, debts, obligations, and fair shares. The just person takes the trouble to find out what is really due.
- **Consistent action.** Iustitia is "constant and perpetual" -- not a mood or an impulse but a reliable character trait. People can count on the just person to be fair.
- **Proportionality.** Iustitia is calibrated to the situation. Equal cases are treated equally; unequal cases are treated proportionally. A one-size-fits-all approach is not just.
- **Both giving and refraining.** Iustitia both gives what is owed (wages to the worker, gratitude to the benefactor, truth to the questioner) and refrains from taking what is not owed (not stealing, not lying, not cheating).

### What Iustitia Is NOT

- **Not mere fairness in the playground sense.** "That's not fair!" often means "I want more." Iustitia is not about equal distribution of treats but about the objective rights of persons. Sometimes iustitia requires unequal distribution (distributive justice gives more to those who need more or have contributed more).
- **Not rule-following.** Laws and rules are instruments of justice but they are not justice itself. An unjust law is still unjust (Aquinas, q. 96, a. 4: *lex iniusta non est lex*). Iustitia sometimes requires breaking or resisting unjust rules.
- **Not punitive harshness.** Modern usage of "justice" often connotes punishment. Aquinas's iustitia is broader: it includes mercy, gratitude, truthfulness, and courtesy. Vindication (just punishment) is only one small part.
- **Not impersonal bureaucracy.** Iustitia is deeply personal. It is a virtue of the *will* -- the just person *wants* to give others their due. It is not a cold procedure but a warm commitment to the rights of others.
- **Not charity.** Iustitia and caritas are distinct. What I owe you in justice is yours *by right*; I am not being generous by giving it. Paying a worker their wages is not charity; it is justice. The confusion of the two leads to a patronizing attitude where the powerful "graciously" give the powerless what was owed to them all along.
- **Not revenge.** The desire to see wrongdoers punished can be a form of iustitia (vindicatio), but only when it is proportionate, aimed at restoring order, and free from personal malice. Revenge is disproportionate, self-serving, and corrosive. In stories, the line between just vindication and revenge is one of the most interesting moral tensions.

### How Iustitia Appears in a Children's Story

The natural analogue of iustitia shows up whenever a character:

- **Shares fairly.** The simplest form: dividing food, taking turns, including others. Not because they feel generous but because it is the right thing to do.
- **Keeps a promise or repays a debt.** Commutative justice at its most visible. A character who borrowed something and returns it. A character who promised to help and shows up.
- **Stands up for someone being treated unfairly.** This is iustitia meeting fortitudo: seeing injustice and having the courage to oppose it. The character who says "that's not right" when a weaker character is bullied.
- **Tells the truth, especially when it is costly.** Veritas under iustitia: a character who could benefit from a lie but chooses to tell the truth because the other person deserves it.
- **Accepts fair consequences.** A character who has done wrong and accepts proportionate punishment without self-pity. This is iustitia toward the community.
- **Shows gratitude.** A character who acknowledges help received, thanks the helper, and looks for ways to reciprocate. Gratitudo as a form of iustitia.
- **Honors parents, elders, or teachers.** Pietas: a character who respects and cares for those to whom they owe a debt of upbringing or guidance.

**The emotional through-line of an iustitia story**: imbalance/wrong --> recognition of what is owed --> decision to act justly (often at cost) --> restoration of right order. The key moment is the **recognition** -- the point where the character sees that something is owed (to another person, by themselves, by the community) and decides to render it.

**Concrete visible actions** (for illustration):
- A character dividing something carefully into equal or proportionate shares (a loaf of bread, a pile of berries)
- A character returning something borrowed to its owner (hands extended, object passed)
- A character stepping between a bully and a victim (arm outstretched, protective stance)
- A character speaking up in a group when the group is doing something wrong (standing, pointing, mouth open)
- A character sitting in judgment -- not angrily but thoughtfully (listening to both sides)
- A character bowing or offering thanks to someone who helped them
- A character accepting a consequence with dignity (head down but not collapsed -- owning the wrong)
- Two characters shaking hands or exchanging something, sealing a fair agreement

| **1** | A minor act of fairness or duty appears but is not structurally important. | A character takes turns with a friend. The story is about an adventure. |

## III. Fortitudo (Fortitude / Courage)

### What Aquinas Actually Says

**Summa Theologiae IIa-IIae, qq. 123-140**

Aquinas defines fortitudo as the virtue that **firms the appetite against the danger of death** and, more broadly, against all *arduous evils* that might cause one to deviate from the good (q. 123, a. 1-2). He follows Aristotle (*NE* III.6-9), who analyzes courage primarily in the context of facing death in battle, then extends it.

The critical and counterintuitive teaching: **the principal act of fortitudo is not attack (aggredi) but endurance (sustinere)** (q. 123, a. 6). This is one of the most important and most overlooked points in the entire virtue tradition.

Aquinas's argument (q. 123, a. 6): sustinere is more difficult than aggredi for three reasons:

1. **Endurance faces a present evil; attack faces a future one.** It is harder to stand firm in the midst of danger than to advance toward a danger you have not yet reached.
2. **Endurance involves protracted time; attack is typically brief.** It is harder to hold your ground over a long period than to make a single brave charge.
3. **The one who endures appears weaker; the one who attacks appears stronger.** Endurance requires bearing apparent inferiority without breaking. The person who attacks has the psychological advantage of initiative; the one who endures has nothing but the will to hold on.

Aquinas: *"principalior actus fortitudinis est sustinere, idest immobiliter sistere in periculis, quam aggredi"* -- "the principal act of fortitude is endurance, that is, to stand immovable in the midst of dangers, rather than to attack them" (q. 123, a. 6).

This reframes everything. Fortitudo is not the berserker's rage or the hero's dramatic charge. It is the mother who endures a long illness while caring for her children. It is the farmer who plants again after the flood. It is the sentry who stands watch through the cold night. The person who holds on when everything in them says "quit."

### The Two Acts of Fortitudo

Aquinas does recognize both acts (q. 123, a. 3-6):

**Sustinere (endurance)** -- The principal act. Standing firm, bearing up under difficulty, not breaking under pressure. The paradigmatic case: a soldier standing his ground when outnumbered. The everyday case: anyone who endures suffering, opposition, tedium, or fear without abandoning the good they are committed to.

**Aggredi (attack/initiative)** -- The secondary act. Advancing against a danger or difficulty. Launching the assault, taking the initiative, confronting the threat. This is what most people think of when they think of "courage" -- but Aquinas insists it is secondary. It matters, but it is not the essence.

Both acts require that the person **acts for a genuine good** (q. 123, a. 1). Fortitudo is not mere fearlessness or recklessness. The brave person is afraid (otherwise there would be nothing to endure or confront) but acts rightly despite the fear. A person who genuinely feels no fear is not courageous but deficient in perception (they don't understand the danger). Aristotle makes this point repeatedly: courage requires *feeling* the danger and acting well *despite* the feeling.

### The Relationship Between Fortitudo and Spes

This connection is treated in the theological virtues document but bears repeating from the fortitudo side:

**Fortitudo deals with enduring present evils; spes deals with pursuing future goods.** They are paired because genuine difficulty always involves both: you must endure hardship (fortitudo) while continuing to pursue the good (spes). Aquinas explicitly links them (q. 123, a. 1, ad 2; cf. q. 128): fortitudo is strengthened by hope, because the person who hopes for the good beyond the difficulty has reason to endure. Fortitudo without spes becomes grim stoicism -- endurance without purpose. Spes without fortitudo becomes daydreaming -- desire without backbone.

For stories: the best fortitudo stories will almost always have a significant spes component. The character endures *because* there is something worth enduring for. If the character endures for no reason, that is stubbornness or despair, not courage.

### The Parts of Fortitudo

Aquinas identifies four principal associated virtues (qq. 128-140):

**Magnanimitas (magnanimity/greatness of soul)** -- qq. 129-133. The disposition to attempt great things worthy of great honor. Not arrogance but a justified confidence in one's ability to do something genuinely excellent. Aristotle's *megalopsychia* (*NE* IV.3) -- the person who knows they are capable of great things and acts accordingly. The opposite vices are *pusillanimitas* (smallness of soul -- underestimating yourself, shrinking from what you could do) and *praesumptio* (presumption -- overestimating yourself). For stories: the character who accepts a difficult mission not from recklessness but from a clear-eyed recognition that they can do it.

**Magnificentia (magnificence)** -- qq. 134-135. The willingness to undertake great expenditures for worthy purposes. Less relevant for children's stories.

**Patientia (patience)** -- qq. 136-137. The ability to endure *sorrows* without being overwhelmed -- specifically the sorrows that come from external evils and losses. Patientia is fortitudo applied to the daily, undramatic sufferings of life. Not a flashy virtue, but Aquinas treats it as essential. The patient person does not collapse under grief, frustration, or chronic difficulty. For stories: the character who keeps going through boring, grinding, unglamorous hardship -- not the dramatic battle but the long march.

**Perseverantia (perseverance)** -- qq. 137-138. The ability to persist in a difficult good *over time*. Where patientia endures suffering, perseverantia endures *duration*. The challenge is not the intensity of the difficulty but its length. The vice opposed to perseverantia is *mollities* (softness) -- giving up not because the difficulty is unbearable but because one is tired of bearing it. For stories: the character who is tempted to quit not because the obstacle is too great but because the road is too long.

### Pieper on Fortitudo

Pieper's treatment (*The Four Cardinal Virtues*, "Fortitude") is characteristically penetrating:

**Fortitudo is ultimately about accepting vulnerability.** The brave person is not the person who has eliminated risk or fear, but the person who accepts that some goods are worth suffering for. Pieper: "Fortitude presupposes vulnerability; without vulnerability there is no possibility of fortitude." A being that cannot be hurt cannot be brave. This is why fortitudo is a virtue and not a power: it is the right response to genuine weakness, not the absence of weakness.

**Fortitudo's deepest expression is not killing but dying.** Pieper: "The essential act of fortitude is not to charge the enemy but to endure and bear." The martyr, not the warrior, is the paradigmatic case. In the natural register: the person who suffers loss, damage, pain, or death for the sake of the good, without abandoning the good, is exercising fortitudo at its deepest.

**Fortitudo is always in service to something else.** No one is brave for the sake of being brave. Fortitudo exists to *protect and serve* the other virtues. You are brave so that you can *be just* when justice is dangerous, *be truthful* when truth is costly, *be merciful* when mercy requires risk. Fortitudo without a good cause is recklessness or aggression. Pieper: "Fortitude without prudence is not fortitude." The brave person must know what they are being brave *for*.

**The line between fortitudo and recklessness is drawn by prudentia.** The reckless person charges into danger without discernment. The brave person has first judged (prudentia) that this danger is worth facing, for this cause, at this time. Fortitudo without prudentia is foolhardiness; with prudentia, it is genuine courage.

### What Fortitudo Actually Means (Not the English Shorthand)

Fortitudo is **the firm disposition to endure dangers, hardships, and sufferings for the sake of a genuine good, without being broken or deflected from that good.** It involves:

- **Feeling the danger.** The courageous person is not fearless but *correctly afraid*. They perceive the real threat and feel the natural human response to it. Denial is not courage.
- **Holding firm despite the feeling.** Not suppressing fear but acting rightly in its presence. The knees shake, but the person stands.
- **Enduring over time.** The most common and most overlooked form: not the single dramatic moment of bravery but the sustained willingness to bear difficulty day after day. Patientia and perseverantia are the everyday faces of fortitudo.
- **Acting for a real good.** Fortitudo is always directed toward something worth suffering for. If the cause is bad, the "courage" is mere recklessness or zealotry.
- **Accepting vulnerability.** Fortitudo does not pretend to be invulnerable. It accepts that you can be hurt, and chooses to act anyway.

### What Fortitudo Is NOT

- **Not fearlessness.** The person who feels no fear is not courageous; they are either foolish (they don't understand the danger) or pathological. Courage requires fear as its material.
- **Not aggression.** The primary act of fortitudo is endurance, not attack. The tough, aggressive, combative personality is not necessarily brave; they may simply be angry. Fortitudo is compatible with gentleness, patience, and quiet demeanor.
- **Not recklessness.** Throwing yourself into danger without discernment is *audacia* (rashness), a vice opposed to fortitudo (q. 127). The reckless person has too little fear; the coward has too much; the brave person has the right amount, governed by prudentia.
- **Not stoic impassibility.** The Stoic ideal of *apatheia* (absence of passion) is not the Christian/Aristotelian ideal. Aquinas and Aristotle both hold that the brave person *feels* fear, grief, and pain. Fortitudo does not eliminate these emotions but governs them.
- **Not mere physical toughness.** Physical endurance is not a moral virtue. A soldier who endures physical hardship for a bad cause does not have fortitudo. Conversely, a physically frail person who endures chronic illness with dignity and patience has genuine fortitudo.
- **Not the willingness to inflict violence.** Fortitudo is about what you are willing to *suffer*, not what you are willing to inflict. A person eager to fight may be exhibiting anger or cruelty, not courage. The truly brave person would rather not fight but will if the good requires it.

### C.S. Lewis on Fortitudo

Lewis in *The Screwtape Letters*, Letter 29: Screwtape discusses courage and notes that the devils' greatest successes come from getting humans either to cowardice (abandoning the good when it becomes dangerous) or to "courage" in a bad cause (fanaticism). True fortitudo, Lewis suggests through Screwtape's frustration, is almost impossible for demons to counterfeit because it requires both *right desire* (prudentia/iustitia determining the cause) and *right endurance* (the willingness to suffer for it). A character can have fake courage (recklessness, anger, fanaticism) but not fake fortitudo, because fortitudo by definition serves a genuinely good end.

Lewis in *Mere Christianity* III.2: "Courage is not simply one of the virtues, but the form of every virtue at the testing point." This is Pieper's point in different language: every virtue requires fortitudo when it becomes difficult to practice. Justice at no cost requires no courage; justice that might get you punished requires fortitudo. Truthfulness that everyone welcomes requires no courage; truthfulness that will anger powerful people requires fortitudo. Fortitudo is thus the "testing virtue" -- the one that reveals whether the other virtues are genuine or merely convenient.

### How Fortitudo Appears in a Children's Story

The natural analogue of fortitudo shows up whenever a character:

- **Stands firm when afraid.** The simplest and most powerful form: the character's knees shake, their hands tremble, but they do not run. They face the dark cave, the angry wolf, the looming storm.
- **Endures hardship without quitting.** The long march, the cold night, the boring wait, the painful recovery. Not dramatic but deeply courageous. Patientia and perseverantia in action.
- **Protects someone weaker.** Stepping between a threat and a vulnerable character. Fortitudo in service of caritas or iustitia.
- **Speaks up when it is dangerous to speak.** The small character who tells the truth to the powerful character. Fortitudo in service of veritas.
- **Accepts suffering rather than doing wrong.** A character who could escape difficulty by cheating, lying, or abandoning a friend, but chooses to bear the difficulty instead.
- **Gets back up after being knocked down.** Failure followed by renewed effort. The character falls, is hurt, rests, and tries again.

**The emotional through-line of a fortitudo story**: threat/danger --> fear (genuinely felt) --> decision to stand firm or advance --> endurance through difficulty --> resolution (victory, or noble failure that preserves what matters). The key moment is the **decision** -- the point where the character feels the full weight of the danger and chooses not to flee.

**Concrete visible actions** (for illustration):
- A small character standing before something much larger (a mouse before a cat, a child before a storm, a small boat in large waves)
- A character visibly trembling but not retreating (shaking hands, wide eyes, feet planted)
- A character shielding another with their body (arms spread, body between the threat and the protected)
- A character walking forward into darkness, wind, rain, or danger (leaning into it, face set)
- A character sitting vigil through a long night (eyes heavy, still awake, a small lamp burning)
- A character picking themselves up after falling (on one knee, pushing up, looking forward)
- A character gripping something tightly -- a weapon, a staff, a friend's hand -- holding on
- A character's face in close-up showing fear but also determination (eyes narrowed, jaw set, but tears visible)

## IV. Temperantia (Temperance / Self-Governance)

### What Aquinas Actually Says

**Summa Theologiae IIa-IIae, qq. 141-170**

Aquinas defines temperantia as the virtue that **moderates the pleasures of touch** -- specifically the pleasures of food, drink, and sexuality (q. 141, a. 4). He follows Aristotle (*NE* III.10-12), who notes that temperance concerns the most basic animal pleasures, the ones most likely to dominate reason because they are the most intense and the most universally felt.

But the treatise extends far beyond food, drink, and sex. Aquinas attaches to temperantia a wide array of virtues that share its essential character: the **inner ordering and governance of the self**, the ability to not be ruled by one's own appetites, impulses, or passions. The full scope of temperantia covers:

- **Abstinentia and sobrietā** (qq. 146-150) -- moderation in food and drink
- **Castitas** (qq. 151-154) -- moderation in sexual desire
- **Clementia and mansuetudo** (qq. 157-159) -- mildness, restraint of anger
- **Humilitas** (qq. 161-163) -- moderation of the desire for one's own excellence
- **Studiositas** (q. 166) vs. **curiositas** (q. 167) -- ordered vs. disordered desire for knowledge
- **Modestia** (qq. 160, 168-169) -- moderation in outward behavior, dress, play
- **Eutrapelia** (q. 168) -- moderation in play and amusement

### Pieper on Temperantia: The Crucial Reframing

Josef Pieper's treatment in *The Four Cardinal Virtues* ("Temperance") provides the essential corrective to the puritanical misunderstanding of this virtue:

**Temperantia is not about denying pleasure. It is about preserving the self.**

Pieper's central argument: the word *temperantia* comes from *temperare* -- to temper, to mix correctly, to bring into proper proportion. A tempered blade is not a dulled blade; it is a blade brought to its proper hardness. A tempered mixture is not a diluted mixture; it is a mixture in the right proportion. Temperantia is not the suppression of desire but the *right ordering* of desire.

Pieper: "Temperance is the inner order of man... The temperate man is one who disposes himself rightly. Intemperance is a destruction of this inner order."

The key insight: **intemperance does not destroy the object of desire; it destroys the self.** The glutton does not harm the food; he harms himself. The drunkard does not damage the wine; he damages his own capacity for clear thought and right action. The person obsessed with social media does not harm the internet; they fragment their own attention and erode their own agency. Temperantia protects the integrity of the person.

This is why Pieper insists that temperantia is *selfward-turned* in a way the other cardinal virtues are not. Iustitia faces outward (toward others). Fortitudo faces outward (toward dangers). Even prudentia faces outward (toward reality). Temperantia faces inward -- it governs the self's relationship with its own desires. It is the most intimate virtue and, Pieper argues, the most easily misunderstood.

**Temperantia does not reject pleasure. It orders pleasure.** Aquinas is explicit: the temperate person enjoys food, drink, and rest -- enjoys them properly, in the right measure, at the right time, in the right way (q. 141, a. 6, ad 2). The person who takes no pleasure in food (the Stoic ideal of *apatheia*) is not temperate but *insensibilis* -- deficient, not virtuous. The opposite of temperantia is not pleasure but *slavery to pleasure*: the state where appetite overrides reason and the person is no longer governing themselves but being governed by their desires.

Pieper: "Temperantia is the safeguarding of the inner order of man... It is not to be confused with the 'taming' of the body by the will. It means, rather, that the free power of the person's self-determination remains intact."

### Humilitas: The Surprise Under Temperantia

One of the most unexpected features of Aquinas's architecture: **humilitas (humility) is a species of temperantia** (qq. 161-163). This seems strange until you understand the logic.

Humilitas is the moderation of the desire for one's own excellence. It is not self-abasement or false modesty (Aquinas rejects both of these as defects, q. 161, a. 1, ad 3). Humilitas is the *accurate assessment of one's own standing* combined with the *disposition not to exalt oneself beyond that standing*. It is a form of inner ordering: the humble person's self-image is proportioned to reality, not inflated by pride.

Aquinas: the humble person can recognize and accept their genuine gifts and achievements (q. 161, a. 3). What they do not do is inflate those gifts or use them to lord over others. Humilitas is compatible with confidence, competence, and even greatness -- it is incompatible with self-inflation.

Where does pride (*superbia*) fit? Aquinas treats superbia as the queen of all vices (IIa-IIae, q. 162), opposed to humilitas. Superbia is the disordered desire for one's own excellence -- wanting to be more than you are, or wanting your excellence to be acknowledged more than it deserves. Since humilitas moderates this desire, and temperantia is the virtue that moderates desires generally, humilitas falls under temperantia.

For stories: humilitas is extraordinarily rich material. The character who knows what they can do, does it without boasting, and does not need applause. The character who listens to criticism without defensiveness. The character who helps without needing to be recognized.

### Studiositas vs. Curiositas

Another revealing sub-virtue: **studiositas** (q. 166) -- the ordered desire for knowledge -- opposed to **curiositas** (q. 167) -- the disordered desire for knowledge.

Studiositas is the disciplined pursuit of knowledge that is genuinely worth knowing, in the right way, at the right time, for the right purposes. Curiositas is the restless, undisciplined appetite for novelty, gossip, distraction, and knowledge sought for power or vanity rather than truth.

Aquinas wrote this in the 13th century, but it could have been written this morning. The distinction between studiositas and curiositas maps directly onto the difference between focused reading and mindless scrolling, between genuine learning and clickbait consumption, between attention and distraction. Temperantia governs even the intellectual appetites.

For stories: the character who reads carefully vs. the character who flips through everything. The character who asks a question because they genuinely want to understand vs. the character who asks because they want to show off their knowledge. In a children's book register: the animal who pays careful attention to the teacher vs. the animal who is always distracted by the next shiny thing.

### What Temperantia Actually Means (Not the English Shorthand)

Temperantia is **the inner governance of the self -- the ability to order one's own desires, appetites, and impulses so that they serve one's genuine good rather than ruling one's life.** It involves:

- **Self-knowledge.** The temperate person knows their own desires, impulses, and vulnerabilities. They are not surprised by their own appetites.
- **Proportioned response.** Not suppressing desire but ordering it: enjoying what is genuinely good, in the right measure, at the right time. The key word is *proportioned*.
- **Freedom, not repression.** Temperantia creates freedom: the temperate person can choose because they are not compelled. The intemperate person is a slave to appetite -- they cannot choose not to eat, not to drink, not to rage, not to scroll. Temperantia restores the capacity for choice.
- **Patience with oneself.** Temperantia is the virtue of the long game. It does not expect instant perfection but the gradual development of self-governance through practice.
- **Protection of the self.** Intemperance is self-destructive. Temperantia preserves one's integrity, one's capacity for right action, one's clarity of mind.

### What Temperantia Is NOT

- **Not puritanism.** The puritan rejects pleasure itself; the temperate person orders pleasure. Aquinas is clear: the person who takes no pleasure in legitimate goods is not virtuous but deficient (*insensibilitas*, q. 142, a. 1). Enjoying a good meal, a beautiful sunset, a game with friends is not intemperance. Only when pleasure overrides reason and dominates the will does it become a problem.
- **Not willpower.** Modern culture frames self-control as a matter of brute willpower -- gritting your teeth and forcing yourself. Aquinas's temperantia is deeper: it is a *disposition* that makes self-governance feel natural. The temperate person does not constantly fight their appetites; they have gradually ordered their appetites so that the right thing becomes the desired thing. This is the Aristotelian insight: virtue makes the virtuous act pleasurable.
- **Not self-denial for its own sake.** Fasting, abstinence, and asceticism can be expressions of temperantia, but they are not its essence. Temperantia is about right order, not about suffering. A person who denies themselves food not for any good reason but from a masochistic or controlling impulse is not temperate but disordered in a different direction.
- **Not severity toward others.** The temperate person governs *themselves*. Temperantia does not license judging others' pleasures. The person who eats moderately but constantly criticizes others' eating habits has not understood the virtue.
- **Not the absence of desire.** Desire is natural and good. The problem is not desire but *disordered* desire -- desire that has escaped the governance of reason and taken control of the person. Temperantia perfects desire; it does not eliminate it.
- **Not rigidity.** The temperate person is flexible within right order. They can enjoy a feast at a celebration and eat simply on an ordinary day. Rigid adherence to a fixed routine regardless of circumstances is not temperantia but a kind of compulsion.

### C.S. Lewis on Temperantia

Lewis in *Mere Christianity* III.2 on temperance: "Temperance referred not specially to drink, but to all pleasures; and it meant not abstaining, but going the right length and no further. It is a mistake to think that Christians ought all to be teetotallers." This is the essential corrective in one sentence. Temperance is about measure, not abstinence.

In *The Screwtape Letters*, Letter 17, Screwtape discusses gluttony -- and reveals that it comes in two forms. The first is obvious excess: eating too much. The second is more insidious: *fussiness* -- the person who is obsessed with having exactly the right food in exactly the right way. The mother who says "Oh, just give me a bit of dry toast" and then sends it back three times because it's not done to her precise specification. Lewis's point: intemperance is not always excess. It can be a disordered *fixation* on pleasure -- not too much pleasure but too much *attention* to pleasure. The person whose entire mental life is organized around the next meal, even if the meal is modest, has a disordered appetite.

This Screwtape insight is valuable for stories: temperantia is not about how much a character consumes but about *whether appetite is governing the character or the character is governing appetite*.

### How Temperantia Appears in a Children's Story

The natural analogue of temperantia shows up whenever a character:

- **Resists an impulse and is better for it.** The character who wants to eat all the berries but saves some for later (or for others). The character who wants to keep playing but knows it's time to stop.
- **Makes a choice when tempted.** The critical moment: the character sees the tempting thing, feels the pull, and chooses differently. The choice must be shown as a real choice, not an easy one.
- **Practices self-control and gains something better.** The story shows that ordering one's desires leads to a better outcome than indulging them. Not as a moral lecture but as cause-and-effect: the character who saves food for winter survives; the character who eats it all goes hungry.
- **Owns a mistake of excess and corrects it.** A character who overindulges (eats too much, plays too rough, speaks too harshly), feels the consequences, and learns to moderate. This is Augustine and the pears: the stolen fruit wasn't even good. The excess brought no genuine satisfaction.
- **Shows humility.** A character who could boast but doesn't. A character who has done something impressive and shrugs it off. A character who listens instead of talking.
- **Pays attention when distracted.** Studiositas over curiositas: the character who focuses on the task at hand instead of chasing every novelty.
- **Waits.** Temperantia and patientia overlap here: the character who can delay gratification, who can sit with the discomfort of not having something yet.

**The emotional through-line of a temperantia story**: desire/impulse --> temptation to indulge --> moment of choice --> self-governance (or failure and consequences) --> better outcome from ordering desire (or worse outcome from failing to). The key moment is the **choice** -- the point where appetite pulls one way and reason pulls the other, and the character decides.

**Concrete visible actions** (for illustration):
- A character looking at a tempting thing (a pile of sweets, a shiny object, a forbidden door) and visibly choosing not to take it (turning away, hands behind back, closing eyes)
- A character dividing food into "now" and "later" portions
- A character stopping in the middle of an activity (putting down a toy, stepping back from a game) when they recognize it's time to stop
- A character taking a deep breath before responding to something that made them angry (the visible pause of self-control)
- A character sitting quietly while others are loud and reckless (not sulking, but genuinely at peace)
- A character helping to clean up after a meal or celebration (ordering the aftermath of pleasure)
- A character admitting "I had too much" or "I went too far" with a rueful but honest expression
- Two images in contrast: the same character first indulging (face bright, grabbing) and then dealing with the consequence (stomach ache, broken toy, lost friend)

### Rating Temperantia 0-5 in a Story

| Rating | Description | Example |
|:------:|-------------|---------|
| **0** | Self-control, moderation, or inner ordering plays no role in the story. | A story about an adventure with no temptation or impulse-control dimension. |
| **1** | A character briefly exercises or fails to exercise self-control, but it is incidental. | A character grabs an extra cookie. It has no consequences and the story moves on. |
| **2** | Self-control or its failure is visible and has some consequences, but is not the emotional center. | A character rushes ahead impulsively and gets lost, but the story is mainly about the rescue, not the impulse. |
| **3** | Temperantia is a significant theme. A character faces a genuine temptation to excess or indulgence, and their choice to resist (or failure to resist) meaningfully affects the outcome. But other virtues share the stage. | Peter Rabbit: Peter knows he shouldn't enter the garden (impulse control), enters anyway (failure of temperantia), and suffers real consequences. But the story is also about fortitudo (escaping) and the adventure itself. |
| **4** | The story's central conflict is about self-governance. A character's relationship with their own desires is the main tension. The temptation is real, the choice is difficult, and the outcome turns on whether the character can govern themselves. | A story about a squirrel who must store nuts for winter but keeps eating them. The tension is entirely about whether appetite or foresight will win. The climax is the moment of choice: eat the last nut or store it. |
| **5** | The entire story is built around temperantia. The character's inner ordering (or disordering) is the emotional core. Excess is shown to be genuinely destructive (not just inconvenient), and self-governance is shown to be genuinely freeing (not just rule-following). The consequences of intemperance and the rewards of temperantia are deeply felt. | Augustine and the Pears is close to this: the stolen fruit brings no joy; the act of excess is immediately empty; the aftermath is remorse and the recognition that the desire itself was disordered -- he didn't even want the pears. A pure temperantia-5 story would follow a character through a full arc of indulgence --> emptiness --> recognition --> gradual reordering of desire --> genuine enjoyment of ordered goods. |

## V. How Pieper, Lewis, and Others Make the Cardinal Virtues Accessible

### Pieper's Key Moves

Josef Pieper's *The Four Cardinal Virtues* (1966) remains the best single accessible treatment. His essential insights, summarized:

1. **Prudentia is about seeing reality, not being careful.** The prudent person's first act is to look at the world honestly. Self-deception is the primary enemy of prudentia.

2. **Iustitia is the minimum of love.** Before you can love your neighbor, you must first be just to them. Justice is what you *owe*; love is what you *give beyond* what is owed. But without justice, love is a lie.

3. **Fortitudo is about endurance more than attack.** The brave person's most characteristic act is holding firm, not charging forward. Vulnerability is the precondition of courage, not its opposite.

4. **Temperantia preserves the self.** It is not about denying pleasure but about not being destroyed by it. The temperate person is free; the intemperate person is enslaved.

5. **The virtues form an organic unity.** No single virtue can function well without the others. A person who is brave but not prudent will be reckless. A person who is just but not brave will be ineffective. The virtues are not a menu to pick from but a single integrated character.

### Lewis's Key Moves

C.S. Lewis discusses the cardinal virtues primarily in *Mere Christianity* III.2-4 and indirectly throughout *The Screwtape Letters*, *The Chronicles of Narnia*, and his essays:

1. **"Courage is the form of every virtue at the testing point."** Every virtue requires fortitudo when practicing it becomes costly. This insight makes fortitudo the "testing virtue" -- the one that reveals whether your justice, temperance, or prudence is genuine or merely convenient.

2. **The cardinal virtues are for everyone, not just Christians.** Lewis insists that these are natural human excellences recognizable by any culture. The Christian claim is not that these virtues exist (everyone knows they do) but that they are perfected by grace.

3. **Virtue is not natural in the sense of being easy.** Virtue must be practiced and cultivated. The analogy Lewis uses: virtue is like a skill. A good tennis player has practiced so much that the right stroke becomes natural. But "natural" here means "second nature through practice," not "effortless from birth."

4. **The modern decay of virtue language.** Lewis notes that words like "prudent," "temperate," and "just" have lost their philosophical weight in modern English. "Prudent" means "timid." "Temperate" means "teetotal." "Just" means "merely." Recovering the original meanings is essential work -- which is exactly what this document does.

### Vigen Guroian on Virtue and Story

Guroian's *Tending the Heart of Virtue* (1998) argues that the moral imagination is formed primarily by stories, not by instruction. Children learn virtue not by being told what is right but by *seeing* what is right embodied in characters they care about. A child who has lived through Aslan's sacrifice, Charlotte's faithfulness, or Sam's perseverance has had their moral imagination enlarged in ways no lecture could accomplish.

Guroian's central thesis aligns perfectly with our project: **the story must work as a story first.** The virtue is embedded in the structure (want + obstacle + choice + consequence), not appended as a moral. If the child would not enjoy the story without knowing the "lesson," the story has failed.

## VI. Practical Application: Our Current Coverage

### Cardinal Virtue Coverage Across All Books

| Book | Prudentia | Iustitia | Fortitudo | Temperantia |

| Augustine Steals the Pears | 0 | 3 | 1 | 4 |
| Dada and the Cockroach | 0 | 0 | 2 | 0 |
| Locusts and Dragon | 2 | 0 | 4 | 0 |
| Lion/Witch/Wardrobe | 1 | 3 | 3 | 2 |
| Link and the Stolen Treasure | 4 | 0 | 5 | 2 |
| Peter Rabbit | 3 | 1 | 2 | 4 |
| The Enormous Turnip | 1 | 2 | 3 | 0 |
| **Total** | **11** | **9** | **20** | **12** |

### Analysis

**Fortitudo (20)** is by far the best-covered cardinal virtue. This is not surprising -- courage is the natural engine of adventure stories, and most of our books involve characters facing dangers. Link and the Stolen Treasure (5) and Locusts and Dragon (4) both center on brave action, and nearly every other book involves at least some difficulty to overcome.

**Temperantia (12)** is moderately covered, carried primarily by Augustine Steals the Pears (4) and Peter Rabbit (4) -- both stories where the central conflict is about appetite overriding self-governance. The other books barely touch it.

**Prudentia (11)** is unevenly distributed. Link and the Stolen Treasure (4) and Peter Rabbit (3) carry most of the weight. Notably, three books score 0 or 1 for prudentia -- the collection needs more stories where *seeing clearly and thinking well* is the virtue that matters, not just bravery or self-control.

**Iustitia (9)** is the weakest cardinal virtue in the collection. No book scores above a 3. The collection has no story where justice -- fairness, rendering what is due, standing up for rights, truthfulness -- is the central theme. Augustine (3) and Lion/Witch/Wardrobe (3) touch on justice, but neither is *about* justice in the way that, say, a Judgment of Solomon story or a wrongful-accusation story would be.

### Story Types That Would Fill the Gaps

**For iustitia (justice/fairness/rendering what is due)**:
- A "wrongful accusation" story: a character is falsely blamed, another character investigates and clears them -- because the truth is owed. Combines iustitia (veritas, rendering what is due) with prudentia (discerning the truth) and fortitudo (the courage to speak up).
- A "fair sharing" story: a community must divide scarce resources. The leader must figure out what each member needs and deserves. Distributive justice made visible.
- A "promise kept at cost" story: commutative justice. A character made a promise and circumstances change, making the promise costly to keep. The character keeps it because a promise is a debt.
- A "gratitude" story: a character receives help and must find a way to acknowledge and reciprocate it. Gratitudo as a form of iustitia. The Lion and the Mouse (already on the priority list) is an excellent candidate.

**For prudentia (practical wisdom/discernment)**:
- An "Emperor's New Clothes" type story: seeing through deception when others are fooled. Prudentia as honest perception.
- A "crossroads" story: a character must choose between two paths based on reading the signs. The right choice comes from paying attention, not from luck or bravery.
- A "listen to the wise elder" story: a character receives advice, ignores it, suffers consequences, returns to the advice, and acts on it. Docilitas and memoria.
- A "think before you act" story: a character's first instinct is wrong; pausing to think reveals the right course. Consilium and iudicium over impulsiveness.

**For temperantia (self-governance/moderation)**:
- A "too much of a good thing" story: a character discovers something delightful and overdoes it until the delight turns to misery. The resolution is not rejection of the good thing but learning to enjoy it rightly. Goldilocks structure.
- A "patience/waiting" story: a character must wait for something good (a seed to grow, a season to change, a wound to heal). The temptation is to rush or force the process; the virtue is in accepting the right pace.
- A "humility" story: a character who is genuinely talented must learn to use their talent without pride. Not self-effacement but accurate self-knowledge and service to others.

**For fortitudo (already strong, but could deepen)**:
- The collection is heavy on aggredi (attack/initiative) forms of courage. It would benefit from a story centered on sustinere (endurance) -- a character who must bear a long difficulty, not just face a dramatic danger. The migrating bird, the wounded animal healing, the long winter.
