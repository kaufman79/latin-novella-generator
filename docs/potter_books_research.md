# Beatrix Potter Books on Project Gutenberg

Research date: 2026-03-28

We already have **The Tale of Peter Rabbit** (ebook 14838) as our baseline. This document catalogs all other Potter books available on Gutenberg with full illustrations, evaluates them for Latin picture book adaptation, and ranks the top candidates.

Peter Rabbit baseline for comparison: 28 `<img>` tags in the HTML, ~27 story illustrations, ~15 pages of text.

---

## All Individual Potter Books on Gutenberg

### 1. The Tale of Benjamin Bunny
- **Gutenberg**: #14407 | [HTML with images](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/14407/pg14407-images.html)
- **Illustrations**: ~27 story illustrations (28 img tags total)
- **Summary**: Benjamin Bunny visits cousin Peter Rabbit and the two sneak back into Mr. McGregor's garden to retrieve Peter's lost clothes from the scarecrow. They gather vegetables but get trapped under a basket by a cat for five hours until Benjamin's father rescues them.
- **Simplicity**: Very good. Short sentences, concrete action, familiar garden setting. Slightly more complex than Peter Rabbit because two protagonists. Would work well at 4-7 word Latin sentences.
- **Virtue**: **Fortitudo** (courage to go back into the garden), **pietas** (family loyalty -- Benjamin helps his cousin). Secondary: prudentia (Benjamin's father as the wise elder who resolves the crisis).
- **Peter Rabbit connection**: **Direct sequel.** Peter and Benjamin are cousins. Mr. McGregor's garden returns. The child already knows the setting and stakes.
- **Notes**: Outstanding candidate. The child already knows Peter -- now he meets Benjamin. Same garden, same danger, new adventure.

### 2. The Tale of Jemima Puddle-Duck
- **Gutenberg**: #14814 | [HTML with images](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/14814/pg14814-images.html)
- **Illustrations**: ~27 story illustrations (28 img tags total)
- **Summary**: Jemima the duck desperately wants to hatch her own eggs. She flies into the woods and meets a charming gentleman (actually a fox) who offers her a shed to nest in. He plans to eat her, but Kep the farm dog rescues her just in time. The fox-hound puppies unfortunately eat the eggs, but Jemima later hatches four ducklings.
- **Simplicity**: Good. The plot is linear and visual. The fox's deception is a great dramatic concept that pictures carry well. Moderate complexity -- the fox's politeness creates irony a toddler may not fully grasp, but the rescue scene is clear.
- **Virtue**: **Prudentia** (the cautionary tale -- Jemima lacks it, Kep embodies it). Secondary: fortitudo (Kep's decisive action), fides (trusting the wrong person vs. trusting real friends).
- **Peter Rabbit connection**: Standalone, but the Puddle-Ducks appear in Tom Kitten.
- **Notes**: One of Potter's best stories dramatically. The "stranger danger" theme is evergreen. Kep is a great hero figure.

### 3. The Tale of Tom Kitten
- **Gutenberg**: #14837 | [HTML with images](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/14837/pg14837-images.html)
- **Illustrations**: ~23 story illustrations (28 img tags total)
- **Summary**: Mrs. Tabitha Twitchit dresses her three kittens (Tom, Mittens, Moppet) in fine clothes for a tea party. She sends them to the garden to stay clean, but they climb a wall and lose their clothes to the Puddle-Ducks. Mother is embarrassed, punishes the kittens, and tells her guests they have measles.
- **Simplicity**: Very good. Extremely visual -- kittens losing clothes piece by piece, ducks wearing them. Physical comedy. Very short, very simple text. Perfect for 3-7 word Latin sentences.
- **Virtue**: **Temperantia** (disobedience and lack of self-control lead to consequences). Secondary: the gentle humor undercuts any heavy-handedness.
- **Peter Rabbit connection**: Standalone. Mrs. Tabitha Twitchit appears in several other books.
- **Notes**: Very funny, very visual. The ducks wearing kitten clothes is a great picture book moment. One of Potter's simplest stories.

### 4. The Tale of Squirrel Nutkin
- **Gutenberg**: #14872 | [HTML with images](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/14872/pg14872-images.html)
- **Illustrations**: ~27 story illustrations (28 img tags total)
- **Summary**: Squirrels visit Owl Island daily to gather nuts, bringing polite gifts to Old Brown the owl. Nutkin alone is rude and impertinent, mocking the owl with riddles instead of showing respect. On the sixth day, Nutkin jumps on the owl's head; Old Brown catches him and nearly skins him. Nutkin escapes but loses most of his tail.
- **Simplicity**: Mixed. The repetitive daily structure (six days of gifts + riddles) is great for language learning. But the riddles are untranslatable English wordplay and would need to be cut or replaced. The core narrative is simple.
- **Virtue**: **Prudentia** and **temperantia** (Nutkin's impudence has real consequences). The contrast between the respectful squirrels and reckless Nutkin is clear.
- **Peter Rabbit connection**: Standalone.
- **Notes**: The repetitive structure is a language-learning goldmine. But the English riddles are the heart of the original and can't translate. Would need significant adaptation -- essentially keeping the frame story and replacing the riddles with simpler repeated taunts.

### 5. The Tale of Mrs. Tiggy-Winkle
- **Gutenberg**: #15137 | [HTML with images](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/15137/pg15137-images.html)
- **Illustrations**: ~27 story illustrations (29 img tags total)
- **Summary**: A girl named Lucie loses her handkerchiefs and follows a path up a hill to a tiny door. Inside she finds Mrs. Tiggy-Winkle, a hedgehog washerwoman, ironing and starching clothes for all the local animals. Lucie helps deliver the clean laundry, then turns around to find Mrs. Tiggy-Winkle has vanished -- she was "nothing but a hedgehog."
- **Simplicity**: Good but episodic. The ironing/laundering of each animal's clothes is repetitive (good for Latin) but there's no real conflict or dramatic arc. It's a charming vignette, not a story with stakes.
- **Virtue**: Weak. Diligentia (hard work) perhaps, but no real moral choice or character growth.
- **Peter Rabbit connection**: Standalone. Peter Rabbit's blue jacket appears among the laundry (a fun Easter egg).
- **Notes**: Charming but low on story. The repeated laundry items are great Latin vocabulary opportunities (vestimenta!). Would work as a gentle, cozy book but doesn't meet our "want + obstacle = story" framework well.

### 6. The Tale of the Flopsy Bunnies
- **Gutenberg**: #14220 | [HTML with images](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/14220/pg14220-images.html)
- **Illustrations**: ~20+ story illustrations (31 img tags total)
- **Summary**: Benjamin Bunny and his wife Flopsy have many children. The young bunnies eat soporific lettuce in McGregor's rubbish heap and fall asleep. McGregor captures them in a sack. Mrs. Flopsy Bunny and Thomasina Tittlemouse rescue the babies by cutting the sack open and stuffing it with rotten vegetables. McGregor carries home the decoy sack, hilarity ensues.
- **Simplicity**: Good. Clear danger (captured bunnies!), clever rescue, satisfying trick ending. The lettuce-sleep-capture-rescue arc is very visual. 4-7 word sentences would work well.
- **Virtue**: **Fortitudo** (the mother's courage to rescue her children), **prudentia** (the clever substitution plan). Secondary: caritas (Thomasina helps despite being a different species).
- **Peter Rabbit connection**: **Direct sequel.** Benjamin and Flopsy are married. Peter Rabbit appears. Mr. McGregor's garden returns. This is the third book in the Peter Rabbit saga.
- **Notes**: Excellent sequel option. Requires Benjamin Bunny first (which requires Peter Rabbit). Could be book #3 in a Peter Rabbit trilogy. The rescue plot is more exciting than Benjamin Bunny.

### 7. The Story of Miss Moppet
- **Gutenberg**: #14848 | [HTML with images](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/14848/pg14848-images.html)
- **Illustrations**: ~14 story illustrations (17 img tags total)
- **Summary**: A kitten named Miss Moppet tries to catch a teasing mouse. After several failed attempts, she wraps her head in a duster and pretends to be hurt. The mouse comes close out of curiosity, and she pounces. But the mouse escapes through a hole in the cloth and dances triumphantly on the cupboard.
- **Simplicity**: **Extremely simple.** One of Potter's two simplest books (with Fierce Bad Rabbit). Very short, very visual, almost wordless in places. Perfect for the youngest readers. 3-5 word Latin sentences.
- **Virtue**: Mild -- **prudentia** (the mouse is cleverer). The trickster-tricked structure is fun but light on virtue content.
- **Peter Rabbit connection**: Standalone. (Moppet is also a kitten in Tom Kitten, but this is a different character.)
- **Notes**: Very short -- only ~10 pages of text. Might be too short for our 15-30 page target unless we expand it. But the cat-and-mouse chase is universal and very visual.

### 8. The Story of a Fierce Bad Rabbit
- **Gutenberg**: #45265 | [HTML with images](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/45265/pg45265-images.html)
- **Illustrations**: ~13 story illustrations (17 img tags total)
- **Summary**: A fierce rabbit steals a carrot from a gentle rabbit and scratches him. A hunter sees the fierce rabbit on a bench, shoots at him. The bad rabbit escapes but loses his tail and whiskers. The gentle rabbit watches from his burrow.
- **Simplicity**: **Extremely simple.** Potter's simplest book, originally designed as a "panoramic" fold-out. Very short sentences, minimal text. 3-5 word Latin sentences easily.
- **Virtue**: **Iustitia** (bad behavior meets consequences). Simple but clear moral causality.
- **Peter Rabbit connection**: Standalone, though it's about rabbits.
- **Notes**: A gun and shooting might be a concern for some parents (Potter wrote this specifically because a parent complained Peter Rabbit wasn't morally clear enough). Only ~8-10 pages of text -- likely too short for our format. The 13 illustrations are borderline for our 15+ requirement.

### 9. The Tale of Two Bad Mice
- **Gutenberg**: #45264 | [HTML with images](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/45264/pg45264-images.html)
- **Illustrations**: ~24 story illustrations (30 img tags total)
- **Summary**: Tom Thumb and Hunca Munca, two mice, break into a dollhouse expecting real food. Finding everything is painted plaster, they destroy the fake meals in frustration, then steal useful furniture for their own home. Later, Tom Thumb pays for the damage with a found sixpence, and Hunca Munca sweeps the dollhouse daily as penance.
- **Simplicity**: Good. Very visual -- mice smashing fake food, stealing tiny furniture. The redemption ending is sweet. Would work at 4-7 word Latin sentences.
- **Virtue**: **Iustitia** (making amends for wrongdoing), **temperantia** (initial lack of self-control, then growth). The restitution theme is genuinely good.
- **Peter Rabbit connection**: Standalone.
- **Notes**: Strong virtue content with the restitution arc. The dollhouse setting is fun and very visual. Good candidate.

### 10. The Tale of Mr. Jeremy Fisher
- **Gutenberg**: #15077 | [HTML with images](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/15077/pg15077-images.html)
- **Illustrations**: ~14+ story illustrations (31 img tags total -- many may be decorative/repeated)
- **Summary**: Mr. Jeremy Fisher, a frog, goes fishing in the rain. After catching only a stickleback, a huge trout attacks and nearly swallows him. He escapes because the trout dislikes his mackintosh. He gives up fishing and hosts a dinner party serving grasshopper instead of fish.
- **Simplicity**: Good. Linear adventure, clear danger, funny ending. Short and visual.
- **Virtue**: Light -- **prudentia** (knowing when to quit), resilience. Not strong virtue content.
- **Peter Rabbit connection**: Standalone.
- **Notes**: Fun little adventure. The trout attack is dramatic and visual. But thin on virtue content.

### 11. The Tale of Mrs. Tittlemouse
- **Gutenberg**: #17089 | [HTML with images](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/17089/pg17089-images.html)
- **Illustrations**: ~29 story illustrations (29 img tags total)
- **Summary**: Mrs. Tittlemouse, an extremely tidy wood-mouse, is plagued by unwanted visitors in her underground home: beetles, spiders, a bumble bee who nests in her storeroom, and Mr. Jackson the toad who makes a tremendous mess looking for honey. After they leave, she spring-cleans for two weeks, then hosts a tidy party for mice friends.
- **Simplicity**: Moderate. Episodic -- a series of unwanted visitors. Repetitive structure is good for language. But no real dramatic arc or climax.
- **Virtue**: Weak for our framework. Diligence? Hospitality (grudging)?
- **Peter Rabbit connection**: Standalone. Thomasina Tittlemouse appears in Flopsy Bunnies.

### 12. The Tale of Mr. Tod
- **Gutenberg**: #19805 | [HTML with images](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/19805/pg19805-images.html)
- **Illustrations**: ~55+ story illustrations (57 img tags total)
- **Summary**: Tommy Brock the badger kidnaps Benjamin Bunny's baby rabbits. Peter Rabbit and Benjamin track them to Mr. Tod the fox's house. While the fox and badger fight each other, Peter and Benjamin rescue the babies.
- **Simplicity**: **Too complex.** Potter's longest book. Dense text, multiple plotlines, dark tone (Potter herself said "I am quite tired of making goody goody books"). Not suitable for a toddler.
- **Virtue**: Fortitudo, caritas (rescuing family). But buried in complexity.
- **Peter Rabbit connection**: **Direct sequel** to both Benjamin Bunny and Flopsy Bunnies. Peter, Benjamin, and Flopsy all appear.
- **Notes**: Would be book #4 in the Peter Rabbit saga but is far too long and complex for our format. Skip.

### 13. The Tale of Samuel Whiskers (The Roly-Poly Pudding)
- **Gutenberg**: #15575 | [HTML with images](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/15575/pg15575-images.html)
- **Illustrations**: ~25-30 story illustrations (60 img tags total -- many decorative)
- **Summary**: Tom Kitten hides in a chimney on baking day and falls into the home of two enormous rats, Samuel Whiskers and Anna Maria. They capture him and try to roll him into a roly-poly pudding. A carpenter dog rescues Tom by sawing through the attic floor.
- **Simplicity**: **Too complex.** One of Potter's longer books. Dark content (rats trying to cook a kitten). Dense text.
- **Peter Rabbit connection**: Standalone (features Tom Kitten).
- **Notes**: Skip -- too long, too dark for a toddler.

### 14. The Tailor of Gloucester
- **Gutenberg**: #14868 | [HTML with images](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/14868/pg14868-images.html)
- **Illustrations**: ~28 story illustrations (30 img tags total)
- **Summary**: A poor tailor must finish an embroidered coat for the Mayor's wedding on Christmas morning but falls ill. His cat Simpkin had trapped some helpful mice under teacups. The freed mice work through the night to finish the coat, except for one buttonhole. The tailor's fortunes are restored.
- **Simplicity**: **Too complex.** Potter's most literary book. Long, ornate prose. Beautiful but not for a toddler.
- **Peter Rabbit connection**: Standalone.
- **Notes**: Skip for our purposes despite beautiful illustrations.

### 15. The Tale of the Pie and the Patty-Pan
- **Gutenberg**: #15234 | [HTML with images](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/15234/pg15234-images.html)
- **Illustrations**: ~11 story illustrations
- **Summary**: Ribby the cat invites Duchess the dog to tea. Duchess secretly switches pies because she fears mouse pie. Comic confusion ensues about a missing patty-pan.
- **Simplicity**: Moderate but talky. Social comedy driven by dialogue and misunderstanding. Not very visual.
- **Peter Rabbit connection**: Standalone.
- **Notes**: Too dialogue-heavy and too few illustrations. Skip.

### 16. The Tale of Timmy Tiptoes
- **Gutenberg**: #14797 | [HTML with images](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/14797/pg14797-images.html)
- **Illustrations**: ~16 story illustrations
- **Summary**: Squirrel Timmy Tiptoes and wife Goody store nuts carefully. Other squirrels falsely accuse Timmy of theft and trap him in a tree. A chipmunk helps him. A storm frees him.
- **Simplicity**: Simple enough. Clear injustice arc.
- **Virtue**: **Iustitia** (false accusation and vindication).
- **Peter Rabbit connection**: Standalone.
- **Notes**: Decent but not top-tier. The false accusation is interesting but less visual than other options.

### 17. The Tale of Johnny Town-Mouse
- **Gutenberg**: #15284 | [HTML with images](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/15284/pg15284-images.html)
- **Illustrations**: ~13 story illustrations
- **Summary**: Country mouse Timmy Willie accidentally visits the city; city mouse Johnny visits the country. Each prefers his own home. A retelling of Aesop's town mouse / country mouse.
- **Simplicity**: Very simple. Clear contrast. Good for repetitive Latin ("in urbe... in agro...").
- **Virtue**: Light. Contentment, gratitude for one's own home.
- **Peter Rabbit connection**: Standalone.

## Top 5 Candidates, Ranked

### #1: The Tale of Benjamin Bunny (14407)

| Criterion | Rating |
|-----------|--------|
| Illustrations | 27 -- excellent |
| Simplicity | Very good -- concrete action, visual storytelling |
| Virtue | Fortitudo + pietas (courage and family loyalty) |
| Peter Rabbit connection | **Direct sequel** -- Peter and Benjamin are cousins |

**Why #1**: This is the obvious next book. The child already knows Peter Rabbit, Mr. McGregor's garden, and the stakes. Benjamin is a new character who is braver than Peter -- a great foil. The plot is simple (sneak in, get clothes, get trapped, get rescued) with clear visual beats. The father's arrival provides a satisfying resolution. Natural sequel that builds on existing knowledge.

**Virtue arc**: Benjamin chooses to help his frightened cousin. Peter starts timid (still shaken from his first adventure) but follows Benjamin into danger. The virtue is earned through the relationship -- Benjamin's courage is motivated by loyalty to Peter.

---

### #2: The Tale of Tom Kitten (14837)

| Criterion | Rating |
|-----------|--------|
| Illustrations | 23 -- good |
| Simplicity | **Excellent** -- one of Potter's simplest stories |
| Virtue | Temperantia (disobedience has consequences) |
| Peter Rabbit connection | Standalone (but Mrs. Tabitha appears elsewhere) |

**Why #2**: Extremely simple, extremely visual, extremely funny. Kittens losing clothes piece by piece is physical comedy that needs no explanation. Ducks wearing kitten clothes is a perfect picture book moment. The Latin can be very short and punchy. "Vestimenta ceciderunt!" The temperantia theme (mother says stay clean, kittens don't, consequences follow) maps perfectly onto toddler life.

**Virtue arc**: The kittens choose to disobey and climb the wall. The consequences are comic but real -- they lose their clothes, get punished, and miss the tea party. Cause and effect, not moralizing.

---

### #3: The Tale of Jemima Puddle-Duck (14814)

| Criterion | Rating |
|-----------|--------|
| Illustrations | 27 -- excellent |
| Simplicity | Good -- linear plot, strong visual storytelling |
| Virtue | Prudentia (Jemima lacks it, Kep has it) |
| Peter Rabbit connection | Standalone |

**Why #3**: Dramatically the strongest story on this list. The fox is a genuinely menacing villain disguised as a gentleman -- the illustrations make this clear even if the text is subtle. Kep the dog is an excellent hero. The rescue is thrilling. But the irony (Jemima doesn't realize the fox wants to eat her) may go over a toddler's head. The pictures would need to do heavy lifting on the "this fox is bad" front.

**Virtue arc**: Jemima's naive trust vs. Kep's prudent action. The story hinges on Kep's choice to intervene. Strong single virtue (prudentia) with a clear through-line.

---

### #4: The Tale of the Flopsy Bunnies (14220)

| Criterion | Rating |
|-----------|--------|
| Illustrations | 20+ -- good |
| Simplicity | Good -- clear danger, clever rescue |
| Virtue | Fortitudo + prudentia (mother's courage, clever rescue plan) |
| Peter Rabbit connection | **Direct sequel** (book #3 in the saga) |

**Why #4**: Excellent story with a thrilling capture-and-rescue plot. Mrs. Flopsy Bunny is the hero -- a mother who saves her children through cleverness and courage. The sack-swap trick is satisfying. But this really wants Benjamin Bunny first (it's Benjamin's family), so it should be book #3 in the series, not book #2. Ranked #4 for now because it depends on doing Benjamin Bunny first.

**Virtue arc**: The mother faces a crisis (children captured) and acts decisively. The rescue is her own plan, not luck. Strong fortitudo and prudentia.

---

### #5: The Tale of Two Bad Mice (45264)

| Criterion | Rating |
|-----------|--------|
| Illustrations | 24 -- very good |
| Simplicity | Good -- visual destruction comedy, sweet ending |
| Virtue | **Iustitia** (making restitution for wrongdoing) |
| Peter Rabbit connection | Standalone |

**Why #5**: The restitution arc is genuinely excellent virtue content. The mice do wrong (destroy things, steal), then freely choose to make amends (pay for damage, sweep daily). This is iustitia modeled perfectly -- not punishment from outside, but the characters' own choice to set things right. The dollhouse setting is visually rich. Edged out of #4 by the Flopsy Bunnies' Peter Rabbit connection.

**Virtue arc**: Want (real food) + obstacle (everything is fake) + wrong choice (destroy it) + right choice (make restitution). The resolution comes from the characters' own moral growth. Textbook virtue arc.

## Recommended Sequence

If building a Potter Latin series:

1. **The Tale of Peter Rabbit** (14838) -- DONE
2. **The Tale of Benjamin Bunny** (14407) -- next
3. **The Tale of Tom Kitten** (14837) -- standalone palate cleanser
4. **The Tale of the Flopsy Bunnies** (14220) -- return to Peter Rabbit world
5. **The Tale of Jemima Puddle-Duck** (14814) -- dramatic standalone
6. **The Tale of Two Bad Mice** (45264) -- strong virtue content

This sequence alternates between Peter Rabbit sequels and standalones, building complexity gradually, and covers fortitudo, temperantia, prudentia, and iustitia.

## Books NOT Recommended

| Book | Reason |
|------|--------|
| The Tale of Mr. Tod (19805) | Too long and complex for a toddler |
| The Tale of Samuel Whiskers (15575) | Too long, dark content (rats cooking a kitten) |
| The Tailor of Gloucester (14868) | Too literary, ornate prose |
| The Tale of the Pie and the Patty-Pan (15234) | Too dialogue-heavy, too few illustrations |
| The Tale of Ginger and Pickles (14877) | Abstract economics, not for toddlers |
| Cecily Parsley's Nursery Rhymes (23350) | Not a story |
| The Story of a Fierce Bad Rabbit (45265) | Too short (13 illus, ~8 pages), gun content |
| The Story of Miss Moppet (14848) | Too short (14 illus, ~8 pages) for our 15-30 page format |

**Note on Fierce Bad Rabbit and Miss Moppet**: Both are extremely simple and would make perfect Latin texts, but they're physically too short for our picture book format (only 8-10 pages of text, 13-14 illustrations). They were originally published as fold-out panoramas, not bound books. Could work if we expanded the text slightly, but that changes the adaptation calculus.
