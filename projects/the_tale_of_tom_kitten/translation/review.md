# Latin Review

## Summary
Overall quality: **Good**
Pages reviewed: 20
Pages with corrections: 2

The translation captures the physical comedy of the original through punchy, visual Latin. Sentence density is well-calibrated for a toddler picture book -- short bursts of action that match the illustrations. The vocabulary is concrete and body-centered (washing, combing, dressing, falling, climbing), which is excellent for comprehension at this level. The repeated word "vestimenta" (clothes) runs through the whole book as a natural through-line, and "fibulae desiluērunt" (buttons popped off) recurs as a comic refrain.

Two corrections made: one verb form (page 6) and one imperative number inconsistency (page 7).

### Page 6
- Status: **Corrected**
- Latin: "Fibulae dēsiluerunt! Mater eas resuit." (was: "resusit")
- Correction: resusit --> resuit. The perfect of suo (to sew) is sui, so the re- compound is resuit (she sewed back). "resusit" is not a standard Latin form.
- Notes: fibulae (clasps/buttons) is the right word -- classical Latin has no word for "button" specifically, and fibula (clasp/brooch) is the closest functional equivalent. desiluērunt (they jumped off) is a wonderfully vivid verb for buttons popping. This line will become a comic refrain when it echoes on page 10. 6 words.

### Page 7
- Status: **Corrected**
- Latin: "Ite in hortum! Vestimenta munda servate!" (was: "I in hortum")
- Correction: I --> Ite. Mother is addressing all three kittens, so the plural imperative of eo is required. servate is already correctly plural. The singular I would be inconsistent.
- Notes: "Vestimenta munda servate" is crisp and imperative -- literally "keep your clothes clean." munda (clean, neuter plural agreeing with vestimenta) as predicate adjective with servo is natural. This is the central command of the story -- the one the kittens immediately violate. 8 words.

## Vocabulary Notes

- **feliculus** (kitten): Diminutive of feles (cat). Not attested in classical Latin but transparently formed following standard diminutive patterns (cf. homunculus, opusculum). Immediately comprehensible. A charming coinage.
- **Digitalia** (Mittens): From digitale (finger-covering). Creative Latin name that captures the meaning of "Mittens." Used as an indeclinable proper name throughout.
- **fibulae** (buttons, pp. 6, 10): Classical fibula means clasp, brooch, or pin -- the closest functional equivalent to buttons, which did not exist in antiquity. Clear in context.
- **petasus** (hat, pp. 10, 13): A broad-brimmed hat. Attested in Plautus and elsewhere as a loanword from Greek. Matches the illustration perfectly.
- **stola** (dress/pinafore, p. 8): A long garment worn by Roman women. Used here for the kittens' pinafores -- a reasonable extension.
- **vibrissa** (whisker, p. 4): Attested in Pliny (NH 11.170) for animal whiskers/facial hair. Perfect word.
- **convivium** (tea party, p. 2): Literally a "living-together" / shared meal. No classical word for tea party exists; this is the natural paraphrase. The illustration shows the social gathering context.
- **stagnum** (pond, p. 20): Well-attested for a still body of water, pool, or pond.

## Style Notes

- **Physical comedy drives the Latin.** The vocabulary is heavily bodily and concrete: washing (lavit), combing (pexit), dressing (vestivit), tripping (offenderunt), climbing (ascendit), falling (cecidit/ceciderunt), laughing (riserunt), punishing (punivit). This is ideal for a toddler -- every Latin word maps to a visible action in the illustration.
- **Comic refrain works well.** "Fibulae desiluērunt" appears on pages 6 and 10, creating a running joke about buttons popping off. The word "ceciderunt" (fell) runs through pages 9, 11, 14, and 20 -- everything falls in this story: kittens, clothes, Mittens off the wall, clothes into the pond.
- **"Sed" as story engine.** The conjunction "Sed" (but) appears on pages 5, 11, and 19, always marking a comic reversal: Tom is dressed BUT he's too fat; the sisters try to help BUT the clothes all fall off; Mother sends them to bed BUT they don't stay. This structural repetition teaches the child that "Sed" means something funny is about to happen.
- **Temperantia is modeled through cause and effect.** Mother gives a clear command (servate vestimenta munda). The kittens immediately violate it. Consequences follow (punivit, in lectum misit). But the humor undercuts any heavy-handedness -- the final image is ducks in a pond, not a moral lecture. The virtue is structural, not stated.
- **Level matches Peter Rabbit.** Comparable sentence complexity, slightly leaner word counts. Appropriate for the target audience.
