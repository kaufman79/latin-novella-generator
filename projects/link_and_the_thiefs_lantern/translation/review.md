# Latin Review: Lincus et Lucerna Furis

**Reviewer:** latin-censor agent
**Date:** 2026-03-28
**Overall verdict:** Very strong. Only two macron errors found; both fixed in translation.json. The Latin is natural, concise, and reads like a real picture book.

---

## Errors Found and Fixed

### Page 5 — Macron error (fixed)
- **Was:** `"Fūrem invēnī!" Lincō dīxērunt.`
- **Issue:** `invēnī` with long ē reads as perfect indicative 1st person ("I found the thief!"). The imperative singular of inveniō is `invenī` (short e, long final ī).
- **Now:** `"Fūrem invenī!" Lincō dīxērunt.`

### Page 14 — Macron error (fixed)
- **Was:** `Parvus Moblīnus in mediō sēdēbat.`
- **Issue:** `sēdēbat` has a spurious long ē on the stem. The stem of sedeō is sed- (short e). The long ē appears only in the perfect (sēdī/sēdit). The imperfect is `sedēbat`.
- **Now:** `Parvus Moblīnus in mediō sedēbat.`

## Items Reviewed but Accepted

### Page 2 — `ūnō māne`
`māne` is typically indeclinable or treated as a neuter noun. `ūnō māne` (with ūnō agreeing as if neuter ablative) is a common and natural construction. Acceptable.

### Page 3 — `quīnque plūrēs`
"Five more" — slightly compressed but clear in context and within the sentence-length target. More explicit alternatives (`quīnque aliae`) would lose the punchy rhythm. Acceptable.

### Page 11 — `Lincus castra exspectāvit`
"Link expected a camp." exspectō + acc. for "expect/anticipate" is natural. `castra` (neut. pl.) as object works fine.

### Page 19 — `Ūnā in lūce sēdērunt`
`Ūnā` as adverb ("together") is good classical usage. The word order (adverb first, then prepositional phrase) reads naturally.

### Page 21 — `In stīpitēs reposuit`
`stīpitēs` (acc. pl. of stipes) — the macron on the first ī is debatable (some authorities give stĭpes with short i, others stīpes with long). Not confident enough to call it wrong. Left as-is.

### Page 23 — `ad speluncam relīquit`
`ad` + acc. meaning "near/at/by" (rather than directional "toward") is well-attested Latin (cf. ad portam, ad flūmen). Clear in context.

## What Works Well

### Refrain structure
`Quis lucernās capit?` appears on pages 3, 4, 7, 11, and is finally answered on page 22. The repetition is excellent for a toddler — it teaches the phrase through recurrence and builds narrative tension. The present tense gives it an incantatory quality.

### Sentence length and rhythm
Nearly every sentence falls in the 2-5 word range, often shorter. Pages 18 (`Lincus sēdit.`) and the fragment constructions on page 12 (`Sōlum spelunca. Sōlum ūnus parvus Moblīnus.`) are particularly effective — the Latin breathes like a picture book.

### Show, don't tell
Page 16 (`Moblīnus faciem tēxit. Tremēbat. Ictum exspectābat.`) and page 17 (`Lincus nōn sē mōvit. Moblīnum spectāvit. Lucernās spectāvit.`) deliver the emotional core through concrete, visible actions. No abstractions, no moralizing.

### Name declension consistency
Lincus (nom.) on pages 6, 9, 11, 12, 15, 17, 18, 20, 21, 22, 23; Lincō (dat.) on page 5; Moblīnus (nom. sg.) on pages 12, 14, 15, 16, 20; Moblīnī (nom. pl.) on page 11. All consistent with the established declension table. No slips.

### Macron accuracy (overall)
Apart from the two errors above, macrons are accurate throughout. Key forms checked and confirmed correct:
- `lūcēbant` (p. 1), `āberant` (p. 2), `clāmāvērunt` (p. 4), `dīxērunt` (p. 5)
- `tēctum` (p. 6), `rapuit` (p. 8), `dēsiluit` / `secūtus est` (p. 9)
- `surrēxit` / `cēpit` / `fūgit` (p. 20), `relīquit` (p. 23)
- `plēna` / `accēnsae` (p. 13), `tēxit` (p. 16), `sēdit` / `sēdērunt` (pp. 18-19)

### Verb tense handling
The narrative moves naturally between imperfect (background: `habēbat`, `lūcēbant`, `tremēbat`, `exspectābat`) and perfect (action: `rapuit`, `fūgit`, `sēdit`, `surrēxit`). This is exactly right for Latin narrative and will give the child natural exposure to both tenses without any sense of artificiality.

### Title
`Lincus et Lucerna Fūris` — clean, correct genitive, parallel to the series naming pattern.

## Summary

Two macron errors corrected (pages 5 and 14). The Latin is otherwise excellent: natural, concise, well-macronized, and perfectly calibrated for the target audience. The refrain structure and the emotional compression of the cave scenes are standout strengths.
