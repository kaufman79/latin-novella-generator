## Vocabulary Notes

- **sūdārium** (handkerchief, p. 2): From sūdor (sweat). Classical Latin for a cloth used to wipe the face. The correct word for Potter's "pocket-handkerchief."
- **cōnsobrīnus** (cousin, p. 3): Strictly "mother's sister's son" but used broadly in classical Latin for any first cousin. Natural for the Benjamin-Peter relationship.
- **formīdō** (scarecrow, p. 9): Same word used in Peter Rabbit page 22. Attested in Vergil (Georgics 1.156) for a garden bird-scarer.
- **cēpa** (onion, p. 11, 13, 22): Feminine 1st declension. Well-attested in agricultural Latin (Columella, Pliny). The long ē in cēpa is correct.
- **identidem** (again and again, p. 13): A vivid adverb that captures Peter's repeated fumbling. Classical and natural.
- **corbula** (basket, p. 11, 16): Diminutive of corbis. Same word used in Peter Rabbit page 4. Consistency across the two books.
- **trepidō** (to tremble/be anxious, p. 13): More vivid than timeō -- conveys physical nervousness, not just mental fear. Peter's hands are shaking.
- **insiliō** (to leap onto, p. 19): A strong, physical verb. Perfect for Mr. Bouncer's dramatic pounce on the cat.
- **poenās dare** (to pay the penalty, p. 20): Standard Latin idiom. More natural than an abstract "was punished."

## Style Notes

- **Tense usage follows the Peter Rabbit pattern.** Perfect for completed plot actions (cecidit, vīdērunt, invēnit, insiluit), imperfect for ongoing states and character descriptions (sedēbat, habēbat, timēbat, trepidābat, poterant). The mix is natural and consistent.
- **Word order is naturally Latin.** Object-before-verb (Mūrum ascendērunt, Tunicam vīdērunt, cēpās collēgērunt), subject-first for topic shifts (Peter..., Beniāmīn..., Dominus Bouncer...).
- **Dialogue is crisp.** "Cūr trīstis es?" / "Venī!" / "Eam recipiēmus!" / "Abīre dēbēmus!" -- all natural spoken Latin, short enough for a toddler to hear the rhythm.
- **Single-word exclamations work well.** "Trepidābat!" (page 13) and the exclamatory sentences throughout maintain energy.
- **The emotional arc reads clearly in Latin.** Misery (sōlus sedēbat, nōn habēbat) → courage (Venī! recipiēmus!) → adventure (cēpās collēgērunt) → danger (fēlem vīdērunt, sē abdidērunt) → rescue (insiluit, fūgit) → consequences (poenās dabat) → resolution (Tunicam suam habēbat iterum!).
- **Vocabulary reuse across Peter Rabbit and Benjamin Bunny** is deliberate and valuable: formīdō, corbula, tunica, fēlēs, hortus, porta, domum, calceōs. The child hears familiar words in a new story.
- **Benjamin's character comes through in the Latin.** His boldness is shown through action (dētrāxit, ēdit, nihil timēbat) while Peter's anxiety shows through different vocabulary (trepidābat, āmittēbat, sonitum audīvit). The contrast is carried by word choice, not narration.
