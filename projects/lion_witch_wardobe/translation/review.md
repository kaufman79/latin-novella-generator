# Latin Review

## Summary
Overall quality: **Needs Work**
Pages reviewed: 30
Pages with corrections: 6

The most ambitious translation in the collection -- 30 pages condensing an entire novel. The Latin is generally competent and occasionally elegant (pages 19-21, 26 are highlights). However, six pages had errors ranging from wrong case endings to an incorrect vocabulary choice (renis/kidneys for reindeer). Several pages exceed the 7-word target significantly, with some hitting 12-16 words. This is the most complex Latin in the collection and probably pushes the limits of the target audience (toddler ~3.5 years old).

All errors have been corrected. The translation is now grammatically sound throughout.

### Density Check
Word counts range from 4 to 16 per page, averaging ~7.8 Latin words per page. This is well above the other books (4.5 for Dada, 5.2 for Augustine, 6.5 for Cicadae). Pages 1 (14 words), 16 (12 words), and 29 (16 words) are the most overloaded. The gift-list page (16) is acceptable as a catalogue, but pages 1 and 29 try to compress too much narrative into single pages. If the book is ever revised, these should be split.

## Vocabulary Notes

- **armārium** (wardrobe, pp. 2, 9, 29): The standard Latin for a cabinet/wardrobe. Used consistently.
- **Malefica / Alba Malefica** (the Witch / the White Witch, throughout): malefica (evil-doer, feminine) works well. "Alba Malefica" (White Evil-doer) for the full title on page 6.
- **thea** (tea, p. 5): Well-established Neo-Latin borrowing. No classical equivalent.
- **dēliciae Turcīcae** (Turkish Delights, p. 11): Creative compound. dēliciae (delights/sweets) + Turcīcae (Turkish). Clear and evocative.
- **trahā** (sledge, p. 10): From traha (drag-sled), attested in agricultural Latin. Replaced the erroneous "rēnīs" (kidneys).
- **pōtiō sānāns** (healing potion, p. 16): pōtiō (drink/potion) + present participle sānāns. Clear.
- **pūgiō** (dagger, p. 16): Classical military vocabulary. Concrete and visual.
- **Mēnsa Lapidea** (Stone Table, pp. 18-19): Descriptive proper noun. Immediately clear.
- **corōnat** (crowns, p. 27): From corōna. The English loanword helps recognition.

## Macron Audit Summary

Corrections made:
- Page 2: Luciā -> Lucia (case correction, not macron -- but the macron on the final ā was part of the wrong case)
- Page 4: Lampā -> Lampas (case/form correction)
- Page 28: Luciā -> Lucia (same error as page 2); Lenis -> Lēnis (added macron on long ē)

Key macrons verified correct:
- Ablative of time (Tempore, Diē pluviōsō, Mediā nocte)
- Genitives (Bellī, Terrārum, Professōris, dracōnis, Maleficae, Castorum, Edmundī)
- Verb forms (habitābant, intrāvit, mōnstrābat, invītāvit, crēdidērunt, intrāvērunt, invēnērunt, vocāvit, revocāvit, corōnat, vocābit)
- Accusative + infinitive constructions (merērī, moritūrum, factūram)
- Proper noun Nātālis (long a) consistently marked

Recurring error pattern: "Lucia" written as "Luciā" (ablative) twice (pages 2 and 28). This suggests the translator may have been uncertain about the nominative of first-declension names ending in -ia. The nominative is Lucia (short final -a); the ablative would be Luciā (long final -ā).

## Style Notes

- **The most complex Latin in the collection.** Accusative + infinitive constructions (pp. 6, 7, 12, 14, 18, 19), comparative adjectives (profundior, p. 21), ablative absolutes and ablatives of means (proditione, p. 17). This is appropriate for the source material but pushes the target audience.
- **The tense shift at pages 22-25 is effective.** After Aslan's death and resurrection (past tense), the narrative suddenly switches to present tense (Vivit! Currit! tangit! frangit! pugnat!) -- creating urgency and triumph. This is a sophisticated narrative technique.
- **Pages 19-21 form the emotional core** and the Latin is at its best here: "Aslan sē prō eō moritūrum esse dīxit" (sacrifice), "Lucia et Susanna flēbant" (grief), "Magīa profundior Aslanem revocāvit" (resurrection). Simple vocabulary, powerful content.
- **"Nix liquēscit, et vēr venit" (page 26)** is the single best line in any book in the collection. Five words that carry the entire thematic resolution.
- **Density is the main concern.** This book averages nearly 8 words per page vs. the target of 3-7. The novel's plot simply cannot be compressed into 30 short pages without strain. Consider this book as appropriate for slightly older children or as a read-aloud where the parent can expand on the Latin.
