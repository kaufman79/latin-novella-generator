## Errors Found and Fixed

### Pages 5, 9, 13, 20 -- Macron error on refrain (fixed)

- **Was:** `"Adesne adhuc?"` with a spurious macron on the e (Adesne)
- **Issue:** The 2nd sg. present of adsum is ades (ad + es), with a SHORT e. The interrogative form is therefore adesne, not adesne with a long e. The long-e form would suggest a subjunctive or a different verb entirely.
- **Now:** `"Adesne adhuc?"` on all four occurrences (pages 5, 9, 13, 20).

This was the only error found. All four instances of the refrain have been corrected consistently.

## Items Reviewed but Accepted

### Page 3 -- `Despexit`
despexit (perf. of despicio) -- the long e in the -spex- syllable is correct (from contraction of spec + s). Verified.

### Page 16 -- `frigidae`
manUS frigidae (cold hands) -- manus is 4th declension feminine; frigidae (nom. pl. f. of frigidus) agrees correctly. The long i on the first syllable is correct (frigidus, from frigeo).

### Page 16 -- `Funis defecit`
"The rope gave out" -- defecit (perf. of deficio) is the right verb for something running out or failing. Natural and clear.

### Page 19 -- `in umeros posuit`
in + accusative for motion onto the shoulders. Correct and natural.

### Page 20 -- `clauserunt`
"They closed their eyes" for English "They blinked." A reasonable rendering in the context of emerging from total darkness into sunlight. The plural subject (Link + Foliolus) is implied from context. Acceptable.

### Page 22 -- `abiit`
The perfect of abeo. The first i could arguably take a macron (abiit, from the long perfect stem i- of eo), but per project guidelines ("wrong macrons are worse than missing ones"), leaving it unmarked is the safer choice. No change.

## What Works Well

### The Refrain
`"Adesne adhuc?" ... "Adsum!"` appears four times (pages 5, 9, 13, 20), and the meaning shifts each time:

1. **Page 5:** First contact. The voice asks; Link answers with confidence and urgency.
2. **Page 9:** Second attempt fails. The voice asks again; Link is still trying.
3. **Page 13:** The voice is fading. "Vox minor erat." The question hangs alone, unanswered on the page. The turn.
4. **Page 20:** The roles reverse. Now Foliolus asks and Link answers -- in sunlight, face to face. The question has become a statement of relationship, not a call for help.

This is excellent refrain design. The Latin phrase teaches itself through repetition while carrying the entire emotional arc of the story.

### Sentence Length and Rhythm
Every sentence falls within the 3-7 word target, and many are shorter (1-2 words). The staccato fragments are used to great effect:

- **Page 3:** `Despexit. Obscurum. Valde obscurum.` (1, 1, 2)
- **Page 4:** `Tum -- vox. Parva. Longe infra.` (2, 1, 2)
- **Page 7:** `Nimis brevis.` (2) -- the whole page
- **Page 15:** `Intravit. Deorsum. In tenebras.` (1, 1, 2)
- **Page 16:** `Saxum udum. Manus frigidae. Funis defecit. Pede marginem invenit.` (2, 2, 2, 3)

These pages read like heartbeats. The short phrases force the reader to slow down and let the illustrations carry the emotion.

### Comedy of Accumulation (Pages 6-12)
The rope sequence is a perfect comedy escalation rendered in minimal Latin:

- `Funem invenit.` (rope)
- `Vitem longam invenit.` (vine)
- `Cingulum detraxit.` (belt)
- `Lineam aviae cepit.` (grandma's clothesline)

Each attempt uses the same structure (found/took + tied), and each ends with `Nimis brevis` or `Adhuc nimis brevis.` The repetition teaches the vocabulary (invenit, alligavit, nimis brevis, deorsum) while building comic tension. The clothesline detail (lineam aviae) is a lovely touch -- aviae in two words tells you everything about Link's desperation.

### Name Declension Consistency
All Latinized names are declined correctly throughout:

- **Lincus** (nom.): pages 1, 5, 10, 14, 19
- **Linci** (gen.): page 22
- **Foliolus** (nom.): pages 18, 20, 21, 22
- **Foliolum** (acc.): page 19

No slips. The genitive Linci on page 22 (`in caput Linci`) is the only non-nominative form of Link's name, and it is correct.

### Emotional Turn (Pages 13-15)
The pivot from comedy to gravity is handled by three pages of nearly wordless Latin:

- Page 13: `Vox minor erat. "Adesne adhuc?"` -- the voice is fading; the refrain hangs unanswered
- Page 14: `"Adsum," dixit Lincus. Foramen obscurum spectavit. Funem rapuit.` -- the decision
- Page 15: `Intravit. Deorsum. In tenebras.` -- the descent

The shift from rapuit (he grabbed) to intravit (he entered) is the whole story: Link stops trying to solve the problem from a distance and goes in himself. The grammar carries the moral without a single abstract word.

### The Reveal and Reversal (Pages 17-21)
Page 18 (`Facies parva. Folium parvum. Foliolus!`) is a masterclass in picture-book pacing -- three fragments that zoom in from eyes to face to leaf to name. The exclamation mark on the name alone does all the work.

The comedy punchline on page 21 (`Volare poterat!` -- "He could fly!") retroactively reframes the entire story. Link climbed into a dark well to rescue someone who could have flown out. But that does not diminish what Link did -- it deepens it. He trusted a voice in the dark.

### Natural Latin Throughout
Highlights of natural, non-textbook Latin:

- Page 2: `Sed hunc puteum numquam viderat.` -- pluperfect (viderat) is exactly right narratively, and the word order (demonstrative before noun) is natural spoken Latin.
- Page 6: `Demisit -- deorsum, deorsum, deorsum.` -- the dash and repetition create a visual rhythm that works in any language.
- Page 10: `Cingulum detraxit. Alligavit.` -- two-word sentences with implied objects. A child hearing this will understand from the illustrations. The Latin trusts the pictures.
- Page 17: `Tum -- duo oculi. In tenebris lucebant.` -- the dash creates a breath before the reveal. The imperfect (lucebant) is right for an ongoing glow.

### Tense Usage
Tenses are used deliberately and correctly:

- **Imperfect** for states and background: sciebat (knew), erat (was), lucebant (were glowing), poterat (could)
- **Perfect** for actions: despexit (looked down), invenit (found), demisit (lowered), intravit (entered), rapuit (grabbed), posuit (put), ascendit (climbed)
- **Pluperfect** for backstory: viderat (had seen) on page 2 -- correctly establishes that the well is new
