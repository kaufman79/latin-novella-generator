# Latin Review

## Summary
Overall quality: **Good**
Pages reviewed: 28
Pages with corrections: 7

A domestic comedy that works well as a simple Latin text. Present tense throughout creates an immediacy suitable for the genre (bedtime story told as if happening now). Vocabulary is concrete and visual. The main issues were a spelling error ("exlāmāvit"), a broken passive infinitive where a finite verb was needed (page 22), a tense inconsistency (page 25), and a few macron issues. All corrected.

### Density Check
Word counts range from 2 to 9 per page, averaging ~4.5 Latin words per page. This is the simplest book in the collection, appropriate for the youngest audience. Short pages like "Nox erat." (2 words) and "Pater surgit." (2 words) work as dramatic beats.

## Vocabulary Notes

- **telephōnum** (phone, p. 7): Standard modern Latin borrowing. Clear and unambiguous.
- **charta hygiēnica** (toilet paper, p. 21, 25): Modern compound. hygiēnica from Greek hygieinikos. Vowel quantities on the Greek portion are uncertain -- macrons placed on the long ē only, which is the most confident marking.
- **culcita** (changing pad, p. 16): Literally "cushion/pad." Good paraphrase for a modern object.
- **quisquiliae** (trash, p. 25): Classical word for rubbish/refuse. Perfect fit.
- **pannōrum** (diapers, p. 14): Literally "cloths." In context with cista (basket), "cloth diapers" is the clear reading.
- **blatta** (cockroach, p. throughout): Classical Latin for cockroach. Perfect.

## Style Notes

- **Present tense narrative is a strong choice** for this genre -- it creates the feeling of a bedtime story unfolding in real time. Only one verb breaks this pattern (exclāmāvit, page 6), and it works as a dramatic punctuation.
- **Repetitive structure is effective.** "Blatta in X currit" recurs across pages 5, 14, 16, 19, giving the child a predictable pattern. "Pater calceum levat et percutit" appears on pages 17 and 20.
- **The simplest book in the collection.** Average 4.5 words per page, mostly concrete nouns and action verbs. Appropriate as the baseline text for the youngest readers.
- **Humor carries the story.** The cockroach's refusal to die, Father's escalating efforts, and the dry final line ("Blatta nōn redit") all work without needing the child to understand every word.
