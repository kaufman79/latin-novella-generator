## Vocabulary Notes

- **formīdō** (scarecrow, p. 22): Attested in Vergil (Georgics 1.156) and Horace for a scarecrow or bird-scarer in gardens. Perfect for this context.
- **vās aquārium** (watering can, p. 14): Creative descriptive compound. No single classical Latin word for "watering can" exists; this is clear and natural. A good example of the paraphrase strategy recommended in the guidelines.
- **mōra** (blackberries, p. 5): Neuter plural of mōrum. Well-attested (Vergil, Ovid). Concrete and visual.
- **corbula** (basket, p. 4): Diminutive of corbis. Natural for a small shopping basket.
- **pipiāvērunt** (chirped, p. 12): From pipiō/pipiāre. Late Latin but onomatopoeic and immediately understandable. A good pragmatic choice.
- **chamomilla** (chamomile, p. 24): Medieval/botanical Latin. No classical equivalent exists. Vowel quantities left unmarked since they're uncertain — correct approach.
- **rādīcēs** (radishes, p. 7): Literally "roots" (rādīx). In context with lettuce and beans, "radishes" is the clear reading. Natural semantic extension.

## Style Notes

- **Tense usage is excellent throughout.** Imperfect for settings and ongoing states (habitābat, gerēbat, errābat, quaerēbat), perfect for completed plot actions (cucurrit, vīdit, āmīsit). The tense shifts within pages (e.g., haesit → haerēbat on page 11) are particularly effective.
- **Word order is naturally Latin.** Object-before-verb (Petrum quaerēbat, portam invenīre), subject-first for topic shifts (Peter..., Māter...), verb-first for drama (Cucurrit et cucurrit!). No English-calqued SVO patterns.
- **Dialogue is crisp and idiomatic.** "Cōnsiste, fūr!" and "Nōlīte īre in hortum" are both natural Latin speech. "Perge! Perge!" from the sparrows is charming.
- **Single-word exclamations are effective.** "Fragor!" (page 16) and "Līber!" (page 21) add dramatic punch without inflating word counts.
- **The story arc reads clearly in Latin.** Disobedience (Sed Peter rēctā ad hortum cucurrit!) → consequence (Venter eius dolēbat / tunicam āmīsit) → escape (Līber!) → aftermath (Valdē fessus erat / cunīculī bonī pānem habēbant). Temperantia is modeled through cause and effect, not moralized.
