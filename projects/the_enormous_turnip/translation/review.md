- Latin: "Omnēs adiuvērunt. Etiam minima." --> "Omnēs adiūvērunt. Etiam minima."

- Notes: "Etiam minima" works beautifully as the closing line. "minima" is feminine superlative of parvus, agreeing with Mūs (characterized as female throughout -- "parva sum" on page 9). As a substantive adjective ("even the smallest one"), it's a resonant, simple closer.

## Vocabulary Notes

- **rāpa** (turnip): Using the feminine 1st-declension form rāpa rather than the neuter 2nd-declension rāpum. Both are attested. The feminine works well here since the turnip is almost a character in its own right.
- **iūs rāpārum** (turnip soup): Natural and idiomatic. iūs = broth/soup is well-attested. No forced vocabulary here.
- **prehendit** (grabbed): Good choice over the contracted "prēndit." All vowels short in both present and perfect 3rd singular forms.
- **Avus/Avia** (Grandfather/Grandmother): Clean, simple, immediately understandable. Good character naming.
- **Puella** (Girl/Granddaughter): Used as a name rather than neptis (granddaughter) -- simpler and more visual. Good choice for this age level.

## Style Notes

- The tense mixing (perfect for calling/grabbing, historical present for pulling/being-unable) is effective and consistent. It creates a sense of completed preparation actions followed by ongoing dramatic struggle.
- The refrain "Trahunt et trahunt. NŌN POSSUNT!" is identical across pages 5-8. The payoff "POSSUNT!" on page 13 lands perfectly.
- The progression from "Mē adiuvā" (page 5, singular) to "Nōs adiuvā" (pages 6-9, plural) is a nice touch that reflects the growing group.
- Sentence lengths range from 2-7 words, well within target. Most are 3-5.
- The Latin is at or slightly below the level of "Augustīnus et Pirus" -- appropriate for the target audience.
