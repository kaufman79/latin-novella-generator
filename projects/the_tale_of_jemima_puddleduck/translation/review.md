# Latin Review: Fābula dē Jēmimā Anate

## Overall Assessment

The Latin is clean, natural, and appropriate for a picture-book audience. Sentences range from 4-8 words, with good variety of construction. The narrative flows well and lets the illustrations carry the emotional weight.

## Corrections Applied

1. **Page 7**: `ēlegāns` → `elegāns`. The initial `e-` in `elegāns` is short.
2. **Page 12**: `cōmis` → `comis`. The `o` in `comis` (courteous, friendly) is short.
3. **Page 16**: Removed `colliēnsem` (not a Latin word). "Collie" is a modern English breed name with no Latin equivalent. Simplified to `Kep canem` — the illustration shows the collie clearly, so no further description is needed.
4. **Page 17**: `Omnia Kep nārrāvit` → `Eī omnia nārrāvit`. The original was ambiguous (Kep could be read as nominative subject). Using the dative pronoun `eī` makes clear that Jemima (understood subject) told *him* everything.

## Vocabulary Notes

- **fundus** for "farm" — a standard classical word for an estate/farm. Good choice.
- **acta diurna** for "newspaper" — literally "daily proceedings," the Roman public gazette. A well-established analogy for newspaper in living Latin.
- **digitālēs** for "foxgloves" — post-classical botanical Latin (Digitalis purpurea). A reasonable compromise; the plant is central to the scene.
- **salvia** for "sage" (the herb) — medieval botanical Latin, widely used. Acceptable minor latinity compromise.
- **tugurium** for "shed/hut" — classical (Cicero, Caesar). Good word for the fox's rough shelter.
- **anāticula** for "duckling" — diminutive of `anas`. Well-formed and natural.

## Style Notes

- Good use of repetition: `vulpēs` appears multiple times, reinforcing the word naturally.
- The contrast between Jemima's naivety and Kep's quick understanding (pages 15-17) works well in the Latin — `nihil intellegēbat` vs. `statim intellēxit`.
- Page 22's `quattuor tantum` is a strong, quiet ending — lets the bittersweet land without over-explaining.
- Direct speech is used sparingly (page 9 only), which is appropriate for this level.

## Virtue Framework Check

- **Prudentia** is the clear primary virtue, modeled through *absence* (Jemima) and *presence* (Kep). Jemima fails at all three acts: she does not deliberate (consilium), does not judge correctly (iudicium), and acts on bad judgment (imperium). Kep hears the same facts and instantly sees the truth (intellectus, solertia) and acts decisively (imperium).
- The fox's cleverness is explicitly *not* prudentia — it is calliditas (cunning for a bad end). The Latin `comis` and `subrīdēns` subtly mark this.
- The bittersweet ending (eggs lost despite rescue) gives weight to the failure of prudence — consequences are real.
