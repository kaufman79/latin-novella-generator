# Latin Review: Fābula dē Duōbus Mūribus Malīs

**Reviewer**: Self-review (Claude)
**Date**: 2026-03-28

## Overall Assessment

The Latin is clear, natural, and appropriate for a toddler picture book. Sentences range from 3-8 words, with most landing at 5-6. The vocabulary is concrete and visual. Grammar is unsheltered but straightforward — perfect tenses for narrative, present tense for the final habitual action.

## Page-by-Page Notes

### Page 1
- **"Ecce domus pulchra!"** — Natural exclamatory opening. *Ecce* is excellent for picture books (pointing word).
- **"Mūrī rubrī et fenestrae albae."** — Nominal sentence, no verb needed. Clear and visual.

### Page 2
- **"Duae pūpae ibi habitābant"** — *pūpa, -ae* (f.) is good classical Latin for "doll" (literally "little girl/puppet"). Imperfect for background narration.
- **"Iāna coqua erat"** — *coqua* (f. of *coquus*) is attested in later Latin. Natural and clear.

### Page 3
- **"Cibus pulcher in mēnsā erat"** — Straightforward. *pulcher* works for "pretty/beautiful" food.
- **"perna, piscis, et lībum"** — *perna* (ham), *piscis* (fish), *lībum* (cake/pudding) — all good classical vocabulary.

### Page 4
- **"Ōlim māne pūpae exiērunt"** — *Ōlim* + perfect: "once upon a time" feel. *māne* (in the morning) is indeclinable, correct.
- **"Domus tacita erat"** — Simple and effective.

### Page 5
- **"Tom Pollex mūs erat"** — *Pollex* = "Thumb" — good calque of the English name. Keeps the charm.
- **"Hunca Munca uxor eius erat"** — Name left untranslated (as it should be — it's a nonsense name).

### Page 6
- **"Mūrēs ad domum rēpsērunt"** — *rēpō, rēpere, rēpsī* (to creep). Perfect form correct.
- **"Iānuam impulērunt!"** — *impellō* (push open). Vivid.

### Page 7
- **"Cēnāculum invēnērunt"** — *cēnāculum* (dining room) is classical (Cicero, Vitruvius). Good choice over *triclīnium* which is too formal.
- **"Quanta epulae!"** — Changed from *Quantae* to exclamatory nominative. CORRECTION: should be **"Quantae epulae!"** — *epulae* is f. pl., so the exclamatory adjective must be **quantae**. Fixed.

### Page 8
- **"pernam secāre temptāvit"** — *secō* (cut), *temptō* (try). Natural construction.
- **"Culter flexus est"** — *flectō* passive perfect. The knife was bent. Correct and vivid.

### Page 9
- **"Perna dūrissima erat"** — Superlative *dūrissima*. Effective for the child's understanding — "very very hard."

### Page 10
- **"dē pātellā cecidit"** — *pātella* (plate/dish). *cadō* perfect *cecidit*.
- **"sub mēnsam volūta est"** — *volvō* passive perfect participle + *est*. Accusative after *sub* for motion. Correct.

### Page 11
- **"Cibum batillō frēgit"** — *batillum* (fire-shovel). Ablative of instrument. *frangō* perf. *frēgit*.
- **"Gypsum erat!"** — *gypsum* (plaster). Short, punchy reveal. Excellent.

### Page 12
- **"Piscem in ignem posuērunt"** — Accusative for motion into fire. Correct.
- **"Nōn ārsit!"** — *ārdeō* perf. *ārsī*. "It did not burn!" Two words, maximum impact.

### Page 13
- **"camīnum ascendit"** — *camīnus* (chimney/fireplace). *ascendō*.
- **"Nūlla fūlīgō!"** — *fūlīgō, fūlīginis* (f.) (soot). Exclamatory nominal. Effective.

### Page 14
- **"vāscula invēnit"** — *vāsculum* (small container/canister). Diminutive of *vās*. Good.
- **"Sed vacua erant!"** — Simple and clear.

### Page 15
- **"omnia per fenestram iēcit"** — *iaciō* perf. *iēcit*. Vivid action.

### Page 16
- **"culcitam plūmeam domum tulit"** — *culcita plūmea* (feather mattress/bolster). *domum* (homeward, accusative of direction). Classical construction.

### Page 17
- **"Cūnās quoque cēpit"** — *cūnae, -ārum* (f. pl.) (cradle). *capiō* perf. *cēpit*.
- **"caveam avium"** — *cavea avium* (birdcage). Genitive of content. Natural.

### Page 18
- **"Quanta ruīna!"** — CORRECTION: should be **"Quanta ruīna!"** — *ruīna* is f. sg., so *quanta* is correct as written. Good.

### Page 19
- **"mūrēs paenituit"** — Impersonal *paenitet* in perfect: "it caused the mice regret." Accusative *mūrēs* as object of impersonal verb. Grammatically correct and natural. This is a slightly advanced construction but the picture does all the work.
- **"prō omnibus quae frēgerat solvit"** — *solvō* (to pay). *prō* + ablative. Relative clause with pluperfect *frēgerat* (what he had broken). The longest sentence in the book, but the key moral moment deserves the weight.

### Page 20
- **"cotīdiē māne"** — "every day in the morning." *cotīdiē* is the correct classical form.
- **"domum pūpārum verrit"** — *verrō* (to sweep). *pūpārum* genitive plural. Present tense for habitual action — the only present-tense narration in the book, signaling that this is ongoing. She still sweeps. Effective shift.

## Corrections Applied

1. **Page 7**: "Quanta epulae!" changed to **"Quantae epulae!"** — agreement with f. pl.

## Vocabulary Level

Age-appropriate. Core nouns are concrete and visible in the illustrations: *domus, mūs, perna, piscis, culter, ignis, camīnus*. The only potentially unfamiliar items (*gypsum, batillum, fūlīgō, vāsculum, culcita*) are all illuminated by the pictures — the child does not need to know these words to follow the story.

## Verdict

**Approved.** Natural, clear Latin at appropriate difficulty. The moral arc (destruction --> remorse --> restitution) comes through in the Latin without any moralizing. The shift to present tense on the final page is a nice touch.
