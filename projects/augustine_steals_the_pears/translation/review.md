## Vocabulary Notes

- **conscientia** (conscience, pp. 7, 16): Abstract concept, but the illustration makes it concrete (cricket on shoulder). Used consistently as the moral through-line.
- **furari** (to steal, pp. 7, 19): Deponent verb. The child hears this word twice -- in the prohibition and in the prayer -- reinforcing it.
- **pirus / pira** (pear tree / pears, pp. 4-5, 10, 13-14): pirus is the tree (2nd declension feminine), pira is the neuter plural fruit. Both classical.
- **cubitum iit** (went to bed, p. 17): Classical supine construction. The child doesn't need to parse the grammar -- "went home and went to bed" is clear from context and illustration.
- **iampridem** (long ago, p. 20): Slightly advanced vocabulary, but in a sentence with sensit and remiserat, the meaning is carried by context.

## Style Notes

- **The moral arc is built into the Latin structure.** conscientia appears in pages 7, 16 -- bookending the theft. The child hears the word twice, in different emotional contexts.
- **Tense usage serves the storytelling.** Imperfect for the pastoral opening (erat, amabat, stabat), perfect for the heist sequence (ierunt, agitaverunt, ceciderunt, abstulerunt, fugerunt), then imperfect again for the sleepless aftermath (poterat). Smart instinctive tense management.
- **Pages 11-12 form a single flowing sentence** split across pages. The enjambment ("abstulerunt et fugerunt, / et ridentes sub stellis") creates momentum and breathlessness -- the boys running and laughing.
- **The contrast on page 15** ("Porci erant laeti. Augustinus non erat laetus.") is the moral pivot, stated without moralizing. The pigs enjoy what the boy cannot. Cause and effect carries the lesson.
- **The prayer (page 19) is appropriately the most complex Latin.** A child hearing this read aloud gets the gist from "Domine meus" and "doleo" even without parsing every word. The gravity of the language matches the gravity of the moment.
