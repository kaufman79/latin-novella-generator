# Latin Review

## Summary
Overall quality: **Good**
Pages reviewed: 23
Pages with corrections: 5

An action-heavy adventure story with solid Latin. The translation uses a natural narrative past tense throughout, with good imperfect/perfect distinction. One grammatical error fixed (page 1: taedet used as a personal verb). Several macron additions and corrections made. Some pages are on the dense side (10-12 words) but the action genre justifies the pace. The cicada-based vocabulary (stridere, pungere) is creative and consistent.

### Density Check
Word counts range from 3 to 12 per page, averaging ~6.5 Latin words per page. This is the densest book in the collection. Pages 4, 10, 12, and 18 each hit 10-12 words. For an action story this is acceptable -- the pace should feel rapid -- but some pages could be split if the book is ever revised. The hortatory subjunctives on page 5 ("Eamus! Draconem vincamus!") are a highlight.

## Vocabulary Notes

- **cicāda** (cicada, throughout): The title says "Cicādae et Dracō" (Cicadas and Dragon). The English title says "locusts" but cicāda is the correct Latin for these singing insects. The story is more cicada than locust -- they buzz (strīdēre), not swarm.
- **strīdēre / strīdit / strīdēbant / strīdāmus** (to buzz/chirp, pp. 15-17, 20, 23): This verb does heavy lifting as the cicadas' signature action. Used in hortatory subjunctive (strīdāmus, p. 15), present (strīdit, p. 16), imperfect (strīdēbant, pp. 16, 20, 23). The child hears it repeatedly.
- **ōtiōsae** (idle/bored, p. 1): Replaced taedēbant. A concrete adjective that the illustration can show.
- **cubīle** (lair/den, p. 9): Classical for an animal's sleeping place. Good for a dragon's lair.
- **forāmina** (tunnels/holes, p. 8): Neuter plural of forāmen. More specific than "viās" for cave passages.
- **acervum aurī** (pile of gold, p. 9): acervus is "heap, pile" -- exactly right for a dragon's hoard.

## Style Notes

- **Action-genre Latin works well.** The rapid sequence of perfects in the battle scenes (volāvit, mordēns, occīdit, cecidit) creates pace. The imperfect provides breathing room between action beats (tremēbant, strīdēbant).
- **The buzzing strategy is the narrative centerpiece.** Pages 15-18 build from Antonius's idea to collective action to victory -- a clear cause-and-effect chain that models fortitūdō and teamwork without moralizing.
- **Word order is naturally Latin throughout.** Object-before-verb patterns (Dracōnem vincāmus, cavernam implēvit, cōnsilium quaesīvērunt) feel organic, not forced.
- **The title discrepancy** between Latin (Cicādae et Dracō) and English (Locusts and Dragon) is worth noting. Cicāda is more accurate for buzzing insects; locusta would be the Latin for locust. The Latin title is correct for the story's content.
- **Densest book in the collection** at ~6.5 words/page average. Could benefit from splitting some of the 10-12 word pages if a revised edition is ever produced.
